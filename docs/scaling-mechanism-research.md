# Formation & Transition — Scaling Mechanism Research

---

## Purpose

This is the Phase 1, item 1 document of the Formation & Transition development track. Its job is to answer one question before Phase 2 design begins: *has formation-at-scale been done before, and if so, how — and what of it is transferable to an evangelical congregation attempting this transition?*

This document does not design the remnant cohort (Phase 2, item 6). It does not prescribe the formation track (Phase 2, item 7). It identifies the structural features that have produced genuine formation in group contexts historically, evaluates each model's transferability, and draws mechanism-level implications for Phase 2 design.

**Research basis and honesty note:** This document is a synthesis of secondary scholarship on four historical models, not primary-source reading of Wesley's journals, the full *Apostolic Tradition*, or the complete Rule of Benedict. The structural claims about each model are well-supported in the secondary literature and represent the scholarly consensus. Where primary source reading would add precision, it is flagged as a research gap. No claim here should be treated as more certain than the sourcing warrants.

---

## The Research Question

The CI formation mechanism requires: a formed practitioner, a 1:1 relational container, a phased engagement arc, and sustained between-session disciplines. It produces *hexis* — a trained perceptive disposition operating beneath deliberation — through repeated practice over time.

The question is whether this mechanism, or something equivalent, has ever been made to work at congregational scale — with groups of 8–15 rather than individuals, without a licensed practitioner for every participant, over the extended arc that *hexis* formation requires.

The short answer is yes. Four historical models have done it, with varying degrees of success, for varying reasons. What follows is the structural analysis of each.

---

## Model 1 — The Wesleyan Class Meeting (1740s–1800s)

### Structure

John Wesley organized the Methodist revival into a three-tier structure:
- **Societies** (large gatherings, equivalent to congregational worship)
- **Classes** (10–12 members, meeting weekly under a lay leader)
- **Bands** (4–6 members, same sex and marital status, for deeper accountability)

The class meeting was the primary formation unit. Each weekly meeting centered on a fixed question put to every member in turn: *"How is it with your soul?"* — or in fuller form, *"What known sins have you committed since our last meeting? What temptations have you met with? How were you delivered? What have you thought, said, or done, of which you doubt whether it be sin or not?"*

Membership in a Methodist society required a ticket, renewed quarterly. The ticket was earned by consistent class attendance. A member who stopped attending lost their ticket and their society membership. Attendance was not optional — it was the structural mechanism by which the formation culture sustained itself.

Class leaders were lay members, not ordained clergy. Wesley selected and monitored them personally, replacing poor leaders and requiring leaders to submit reports on their class members. The class leader's own formation was Wesley's prerequisite for giving anyone the role.

### What Made It Work

**1. The question focused on interior state, not content.** "How is it with your soul?" is not "what did you learn this week?" It is a direct probe of the interior — what is actually happening in you, what you have done and left undone, where you are struggling. This is precisely the *katanoeō* (deliberate self-examination) that *Assembled, Not Attending* establishes as a prerequisite for genuine assembly. The class meeting ritualized it weekly.

**2. The formed leader held the container.** Wesley's requirement that class leaders be themselves formed was the mechanism's load-bearing element. A class leader who had not been through this formation could not hold the non-anxious presence the vulnerable sharing required. Where Wesley personally supervised leaders and removed those who weren't formed enough, the meetings produced formation. Where he couldn't, they didn't.

**3. Structural accountability made formation non-optional.** The ticket system meant you couldn't drift in and out of formation without consequences. This is countercultural — it is also the feature that kept formation from being self-selected by the already-motivated. The class meeting reached people who would not have pursued formation voluntarily, because the ticket required it for society membership.

**4. Small size created genuine *koinōnia*.** 10–12 people who meet weekly and report honestly on their interior state develop the shared stake — *koinōnia* — that the founding documents identify as a prerequisite of genuine assembly. The class meeting was itself a functioning *ekklēsia*, not merely preparation for one.

**5. The arc was long.** Class meeting membership was not a 6-week course. It was the ongoing structure of Methodist life. *Hexis* formation requires time; the class meeting assumed time and built no exit ramp into its structure.

### What Caused Its Decline

By the 1860s in American Methodism, class meeting attendance had become optional. By the 1880s, most urban Methodist churches had effectively discontinued it. The decline followed a consistent pattern:

- **Sunday school replaced the class meeting** as the primary formation vehicle — a content-delivery model replacing an interior-accountability model
- **Respectability culture** in Victorian Methodism made the vulnerable honesty of "how is it with your soul?" feel inappropriate for middle-class church life
- **Once optional, attendance fell** — the people who needed formation most self-selected out, leaving only the already-motivated
- **Once attendance fell, the culture disappeared** — within a generation of the class meeting becoming optional, the formation culture it had produced was gone

The decline is the most important data point in this model. It establishes a predictive rule: a formation mechanism that becomes voluntary in a consumer religious culture will not survive. The ticket system was not bureaucratic — it was the immune system that kept consumption from displacing formation. Once removed, consumption won within a generation.

*Primary scholarship:* David Lowes Watson, *Accountable Discipleship* (1984); Kevin Watson, *The Class Meeting* (2014); D. Michael Henderson, *John Wesley's Class Meeting* (1997).

### Transferability Assessment

| Feature | Transferable? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Small group container (10–12) | Yes | Already present in most evangelical churches |
| Fixed interior-state question | Yes | The most directly transferable feature |
| Lay facilitator (not clergy) | Yes | Reduces dependency on pastoral bandwidth |
| Formed facilitator requirement | Yes — and critical | Must precede cohort launch; is the gateway condition |
| Weekly meeting frequency | Yes | Requires participant commitment |
| Structural accountability (ticket) | Not directly | Membership consequences unavailable in most evangelical contexts; requires cultural change first |
| Extended arc (ongoing, not time-limited) | Partial | Possible to set a defined cohort arc (18–24 months) as covenant entry; "ongoing" is Phase 3 territory |

**Verdict:** The class meeting is the closest historical analogue to what Phase 2 needs to build. Its structural features — small group, interior-state question, formed lay facilitator, extended arc — are translatable. Its mandatory accountability mechanism is not directly translatable in the evangelical context, though a covenant-entry structure (see Mechanism Implications below) approximates it.

## Model 2 — The Early Church Catechumenate (2nd–4th centuries)

### Structure

The catechumenate was the process of formation before baptism and admission to full eucharistic assembly. Its most detailed early description appears in the *Apostolic Tradition* (c. early 3rd century; authorship debated in current scholarship — attributed to Hippolytus historically, though this attribution is contested).

Four stages:

**Initial inquiry:** The inquirer comes to the community. Teachers examine their motives, social situation, and occupation (some occupations were considered incompatible with Christian formation — actors, soldiers, idol-makers). Sponsors (established members) vouch for the candidate's character. If accepted, the candidate enters the catechumenate.

**Catechumenate proper (1–3 years):** The catechumen receives instruction in Scripture and Christian life, but is dismissed before the eucharistic portion of the assembly (*missa est* — "you are dismissed" — is the origin of the word "Mass"). They are present for the Word, absent from the Table. Formation is the prerequisite for full participation, not an add-on after it.

During this period: regular exorcism prayers (understood as the displacement of the false self — the old identity being systematically displaced by covenant identity); fasting; moral instruction; community observation of the candidate's life and character.

**Final preparation (40 days before baptism — what became Lent):** The *electi* (chosen ones) received daily instruction, scrutinies (formal examination of their progress), exorcism, and fasting. They learned the Creed and the Lord's Prayer — received by oral transmission, memorized, and returned (the *traditio* and *redditio symboli*).

**Baptism and mystagogy:** Baptism at the Easter Vigil. The week after baptism (mystagogy) was instruction in the sacraments they had now received — explained only after the experience, not before, because prior explanation was understood to substitute explanation for encounter.

### What Made It Work

**1. Formation was the prerequisite to full participation** — not the other way around. You did not consume ekklēsia and then (optionally) form. You formed, and then the formed person was admitted to the assembly's full life. This is the catechumenate's most important structural feature and its most countercultural implication for the contemporary church.

**2. The community assessed formation, not just the candidate.** Sponsors observed candidates in daily life and reported to the church's teachers. The community's judgment — not self-assessment — determined readiness for the next stage. This is *sympatheia* in institutional form: the body registering who is ready.

**3. The exorcism/renunciation structure addressed false identity directly.** The displacement of the old identity through renunciation was not merely ritual. It was the catechumenate's formative mechanism for what CI coaching names as false identity work: the old self is named, renounced, and replaced by covenant identity. The catechumenate's exorcism prayers are the most direct historical analogue in the record to CI coaching's Phase 2 (false identity naming and displacement).

**4. The arc was long enough for character formation.** One to three years is the formation arc the catechumenate assumed. The implicit claim: you cannot know whether a person's conversion has reached the implicit level (produced *hexis*) in less time than that. Character formation — not just belief formation — was the stated goal. What the candidate believed was assessed at the end; what they had *become* was assessed throughout.

**5. The body was protected as well as the candidate formed.** The catechumenate's exclusion of catechumens from the eucharistic assembly was not elitist — it was body-protective. Unformed participation had a cost to the body (1 Cor 11:17–22 is the NT precedent). The catechumenate named that cost institutionally and structured around it.

### What Caused Its Decline

The catechumenate was not abandoned because it stopped working. It became irrelevant when Christianity became the Roman Empire's official religion (post-313 AD). When the entire population was nominally Christian and infants were baptized, there was no adult population to form before baptism. The catechumenate presupposed a conversion from outside the faith; when everyone was inside from birth, the mechanism had no entry point.

The 20th-century revival: The *Rite of Christian Initiation for Adults* (RCIA), instituted in Catholic practice after Vatican II (1972), is an explicit catechumenate revival. Where implemented seriously — with genuine multi-year formation arcs and community sponsor involvement — it has produced measurable formation. Where implemented as a content-delivery course with a fixed end date (as frequently happens), it produces the same consumer outcome as any other content delivery.

*Primary scholarship:* Alan Kreider, *The Change of Conversion and the Origin of Christendom* (1999); Edward Yarnold, *The Awe-Inspiring Rites of Initiation* (2001).

### Transferability Assessment

| Feature | Transferable? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Formation as prerequisite to full participation | Conceptually yes; structurally difficult | Would require rethinking the admission culture of the congregation |
| Community assessment of readiness | Yes, in cohort form | Cohort members and facilitator assess each other's formation |
| Extended time arc (1–3 years) | Yes | Requires defined covenant commitment at entry |
| Exorcism/renunciation structure (false identity work) | Yes — via CI tools | CI coaching's Phase 2 is the functional equivalent |
| Communal sponsor (accountability partner) | Yes | A sponsor structure within the cohort is directly translatable |
| Mystagogy (explanation after encounter) | Yes, principle | Formation precedes explanation; this disciplines the facilitator against over-explaining |

**Verdict:** The catechumenate supplies the conceptual architecture that justifies formation-before-participation — providing the historical and theological precedent for the claim that admission to genuine assembly requires prior preparation. Its specific mechanisms (exorcism rites, full dismissal before the Table) are not directly translatable, but their structural logic is: the body is protected by forming its members before they participate in its full life.

## Model 3 — The Benedictine Novitiate (6th century–present)

### Structure

The Rule of Benedict (c. 530 AD), chapter 58, describes the formation of new monks. The novitiate is a structured admission process designed to form the candidate and allow the community to assess them before permanent commitment.

The sequence:
- Newcomers are not given easy entry; they wait at the door four or five days — the first test of perseverance
- Admitted to the guest house, not the community
- After two months, the full Rule is read to them and they are asked: "Here is the law under which you wish to fight. If you can observe it, enter; if not, you are free to leave."
- Six months later: the Rule is read again; the same question is asked
- After four months more: the same
- Each reading is an act of informed consent — the candidate knows precisely what they are committing to before committing

The novice is placed under a senior monk (the novice master) whose sole function is the formation of novices. The novice master has himself been formed through years of monastic life; he is not assigned the role because of administrative competency but because of formation depth.

If the novice makes profession (takes vows), they commit to three things: *stabilitas* (stability — remaining in one community), *obedientia* (obedience — to the abbot and the Rule), and *conversatio morum* (conversion of life — ongoing transformation as a permanent commitment, not a completed event).

The daily structure (the *horarium*) — fixed hours of prayer (*lectio divina*, the Divine Office), work, and communal meal — is the between-session discipline. Formation does not only happen in formal meetings; it is built into the structure of each day.

### What Made It Work

**1. The novice master is a formed practitioner.** This is the Benedictine model's most direct parallel to what this track requires. You cannot assign novice formation to whoever is available. The novice master's formed *hexis* is the container. Fifteen centuries of Benedictine practice confirm: where the novice master is formed, novices form; where the novice master has not been through the formation himself, novices do not form regardless of how faithfully the Rule is observed externally.

**2. Informed consent, repeated.** Reading the Rule at 2 months, 6 months, and 10 months — and asking each time whether the candidate can keep it — is not procedural. It is the mechanism by which the commitment to formation is renewed before it is deepened. The candidate cannot claim at vows that they didn't know what they were entering. This is the precedent for a covenant cohort entry process that fully discloses what formation will require before anyone commits to it.

**3. *Conversatio morum* — conversion as ongoing, not completed.** The vow is not "I have converted." It is "I commit to the ongoing process of conversion." This is the correct framing of what *hexis* formation produces and requires: not a state achieved but a direction sustained. The telos (Interior Life as Telos) is a description of fruit, not an arrival point. *Conversatio morum* is the vow-shaped version of the same claim.

**4. The between-session structure is built into daily life.** The *horarium* — fixed daily rhythms of prayer, work, and communal life — means formation is not only happening in formal meetings. The daily structure is the between-session discipline. In a non-monastic context, the parallel is: formation cohort members need a between-session rule of life, not just a weekly meeting.

**5. Community observation precedes full admission.** The novitiate period is explicitly a mutual assessment — the novice assessing whether this life is for them, the community assessing whether this person is forming. Neither party commits fully until the formation process has been observed over time.

### What Caused Decline or Failure in the Model

The Benedictine model has not declined — it has persisted for fifteen centuries. Where monastic formation has failed historically, the pattern is: externalism (observing the *horarium* without interior formation), abbatial failure (an unformed abbot produces unformed monks — leadership formation is the gate), or structural pressure from institutions taking over formation roles (when monasteries became centers of scholarship or political power, formation was displaced by function).

The monastic failure modes confirm the founding documents' warnings: structure without interior formation produces performance. An abbot whose own formation is incomplete cannot form monks regardless of how faithfully the external Rule is kept.

*Primary source:* The Rule of Benedict, ch. 58; available in numerous translations (RB 1980, Liturgical Press, is standard scholarly edition).

### Transferability Assessment

| Feature | Transferable? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Formed novice master (practitioner) | Yes — and critical | Direct parallel to formed cohort facilitator |
| Informed consent structure (Rule read at intervals) | Yes | Covenant disclosure before commitment at cohort entry |
| *Conversatio morum* (ongoing conversion as commitment) | Yes | Reframes the cohort not as a program to complete but a direction to sustain |
| Between-session *horarium* (daily rule) | Partial | A rule of life for cohort members between sessions — the between-session disciplines |
| Community observation of formation | Yes | Cohort community assessing each other's formation over time |
| *Stabilitas* (commitment to one community) | Partial | Covenant cohort requires commitment to the specific cohort for the arc duration |
| Total immersion (24/7 monastic common life) | No | Not available in parish context |
| Vow structure (permanent commitment) | No | Translates as covenant for defined arc, not lifelong vow |

**Verdict:** The Benedictine novitiate supplies three elements the Wesleyan model does not: the practitioner formation requirement as explicit (not just implied), the informed-consent structure for cohort entry, and *conversatio morum* as the frame for what the cohort is committed to. These are directly usable in Phase 2 cohort design.

## Model 4 — Pietist *Collegia Pietatis* (Spener, 1675)

### Structure

Philip Jacob Spener (1635–1705), the Frankfurt Lutheran pastor often called the father of Pietism, published *Pia Desideria* in 1675. It proposed several reforms for the German Lutheran church, including the establishment of small groups (*collegia pietatis*) meeting during the week for Scripture study, mutual edification, and prayer — supplementing rather than replacing Sunday worship.

Spener had already been experimenting with such groups, hosting them in his Frankfurt home. Features of his groups:
- Small and intimate (variable size; met in a home)
- Met during the week, not replacing Sunday parish worship
- Scripture-focused but directed toward practical Christian living, not theological debate
- Members encouraged and exhorted one another (*parakalein* — the NT vocabulary of encouragement and formation)
- Lay participation was central — Spener wanted laypeople engaged in active formation, not passive reception of clergy-delivered content

Spener's own formation background was notable: correspondence with Reformed and Catholic mystics (including Jeanne Guyon's tradition indirectly), Johann Arndt's *True Christianity* (1605) as a primary influence. He was a formed person leading formed groups.

The *collegia pietatis* directly influenced August Hermann Francke (who established the Halle institutions of mercy and education), Count Nikolaus von Zinzendorf (whose Herrnhut community was a direct Pietist descendant), and — critically for this track — John Wesley, who was deeply shaped by the Pietist tradition through the Moravian Brethren and his reading of the German Pietists.

### What Made It Work

**1. Addition to, not replacement of, parish worship.** Spener's groups were not separatist. They met alongside the Sunday assembly, not instead of it. This is important for the formation-to-*ekklēsia* framework: the formation cohort meets in a context that feeds into the larger congregational assembly, not as an alternative church.

**2. The practical orientation prevented intellectualism.** Spener was explicit: the groups were not for theological debate but for mutual encouragement toward godly living. This parallels the formation posture of the founding documents — the goal is interior formation, not information transfer.

**3. The leader's formation preceded the group's.** Spener was himself a formed leader before creating the groups. His formation through Arndt and the broader Pietist tradition shaped what he created and how he held it. The groups produced formation because a formed person was holding the space.

**4. Lay engagement was structural, not incidental.** Laypeople in Spener's groups were expected to participate actively — speak, encourage, pray, exhort. This is the 1 Cor 14:26 principle (*each one has*) in a small-group context: everyone arrives expecting to contribute, not to receive.

### What Caused Problems and Decline

**1. Accusation of separatism.** Even though Spener's groups were explicitly not separatist, the intensity of the formation culture created a perceived two-tiered church — the formed group and the rest of the parish. This is the two-tiered church problem that Phase 2 cohort design must explicitly address.

**2. No sustained mechanism for training lay facilitators.** When Spener and his direct disciples were present, the groups produced formation. Without a mechanism for training the next generation of formed facilitators, the model became dependent on the next exceptional leader. The class meeting's failure in Methodism had the same root cause: the formed leader is the load-bearing element, and no system for producing formed leaders means the model collapses with the first generation.

**3. Emotional drift in later Pietism.** As the movement developed, emotional experience (conviction, weeping, religious feeling) became the primary formation indicator in some streams. Emotional states were mistaken for *hexis* — felt experience became the measure of formation, replacing character formation as the actual target. This is a formation posture failure: the interior state became the performance goal rather than the fruit of formation.

*Primary source:* Philip Jacob Spener, *Pia Desideria* (1675; English translation, Fortress Press, 1964). *Primary influence:* Johann Arndt, *True Christianity* (1605).

### Transferability Assessment

| Feature | Transferable? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Addition to, not replacement of, Sunday assembly | Yes | Critical for avoiding separatism charge |
| Practical/formation orientation (not theological debate) | Yes | Formation posture check: interior state, not doctrinal content |
| Lay active participation (*each one has*) | Yes | Cohort entry expectation |
| Leader's formation as prerequisite | Yes | Same as every other model |
| Mechanism for producing next-generation facilitators | Not built — and is the failure point | Phase 2 cohort design must include a facilitator pipeline |

**Verdict:** The *collegia pietatis* supplies the evangelical-context precedent (Protestant, voluntary, non-separatist) and identifies the facilitator pipeline problem as the critical sustainability issue. Without a mechanism for producing formed facilitators from within the cohort, every cohort ends when the first facilitator leaves or burns out.

## Model 5 — The Liturgical Year (Early Church Through Present)

### Structure

The Christian liturgical calendar organizes congregational life around an annual cycle of seasons — Advent, Christmas, Epiphany, Lent, Holy Week, Easter, Pentecost, Ordinary Time — each with its own formation arc, Scripture emphasis, and affective register. The three-year lectionary (adopted in Catholic practice after Vatican II; adapted by many Protestant denominations) cycles through most of the canonical witness over a three-year sequence, ensuring the congregation is formed by the full range of Scripture rather than the pastor's topical preferences.

The calendar's formation mechanism is temporal embodiment: the congregation inhabits the gospel narrative in its body and community life across 52 weeks, rather than receiving the gospel as information. Advent forms longing; Lent forms penitence and self-examination; Holy Week forms the weight of the cross; Easter forms resurrection confidence; Pentecost forms expectation of the Spirit's work. Ordinary Time — roughly half the calendar year, running from Pentecost to Advent — is the long unhurried season of formation between dramatic peaks, where the daily discipline of following Christ without emotional intensification does its deepest work.

### What Made It Work

**1. Counter-calendar formation.** The liturgical year structures congregational life against the secular calendar's formation arc rather than alongside it. The secular year has its own formation liturgies — Christmas-as-consumption, the sports season's tribal loyalties, the academic year's achievement pressures. The liturgical year counter-forms these by structuring the congregation's time around a different story, enacted in the body and community rather than only proclaimed verbally.

**2. Canonical breadth through the lectionary.** Topical preaching series follow the pastor's thematic interests and the congregation's perceived needs. The lectionary follows the full canonical arc, exposing the congregation to texts it would not choose and seasons it would not naturally dwell in. This is formation-by-canon rather than formation-by-preference — the community is shaped by the whole witness of Scripture, not the portions it finds most accessible.

**3. Ordinary Time as the formation majority.** The high-drama seasons (Advent, Lent, Holy Week, Easter) occupy a minority of the year. Ordinary Time occupies the majority. This proportion is not accidental: genuine *hexis* formation does not happen primarily in emotional peaks. It happens in the long, unhurried middle — where the discipline of daily faithfulness, prayer, and communal life roots itself without the support of seasonal intensity. The near-total neglect of Ordinary Time in free-church Protestantism reflects a formation theology that equates spiritual depth with emotional experience.

### What Caused Decline

The liturgical year was not abandoned because it failed — it was abandoned in free-church Protestant traditions as a Reformation-era rejection of "Catholic" practice, without distinguishing the formation mechanism (annual narrative cycle, canonical breadth) from the institutional form (episcopal liturgical authority, specific ritual content). Once abandoned, it was replaced by topical preaching series following the pastor's interests, with no structure for canonical breadth or temporal counter-formation.

**Evidence note (design judgment):** The claim that observing the liturgical year produces measurably stronger formation outcomes is not well-supported by the available research. Catholic and Anglican populations who observe the full liturgical year do not demonstrate uniformly better formation outcomes than non-liturgical Protestant communities. The liturgical year provides a formation architecture that *could* sustain formation if the community inhabits it formatively — it does not produce formation by being formally observed. Treat as a promising mechanism, not a demonstrated one.

### Transferability Assessment

| Feature | Transferable? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Annual narrative cycle (Advent through Ordinary Time) | Yes | Requires congregational commitment to the rhythm and leadership capacity to sustain it |
| Lectionary (three-year canonical coverage) | Yes | Can be adopted without full liturgical form |
| Seasonal affective formation (longing, penitence, resurrection confidence, etc.) | Yes | Requires inhabiting the seasons formatively, not merely noting them on a calendar |
| Ordinary Time as the primary formation season | Yes — and underused | The most neglected and arguably most important season for *hexis* formation |
| Specific liturgical content and ritual form | Not required | The annual narrative structure is the mechanism; specific rites are not |
| Counter-calendar function | Yes — and urgently relevant | Structuring congregational time against the secular year's formation arc is the most immediately deployable application |

**Verdict:** The liturgical year's most directly deployable feature is the counter-calendar mechanism — structuring congregational formation against the secular year's competing formation arc. For Phase 2 cohort design, adopting the annual narrative cycle (even informally, without full liturgical apparatus) gives the cohort's between-session disciplines a temporal architecture — Advent practices differ from Lenten practices differ from Ordinary Time practices. Evidence base for outcomes is thin; flag as design judgment, not demonstrated mechanism.

## Model 6 — New Monasticism (2000s–Present)

### Structure

New Monasticism is a movement of intentional Christian communities, typically in urban settings, organized around a shared rule of life. Key figures and communities: Shane Claiborne (The Simple Way, Philadelphia), Jonathan Wilson-Hartgrove (Rutba House, Durham NC), Chris Haw, and others who gathered in 2004 at Reba Place Fellowship to articulate the 12 Marks of New Monasticism as the defining document.

The 12 Marks include: relocation to abandoned places of empire, sharing economic resources with fellow community members and the neighborhood, hospitality to the stranger, lament for racial divisions, humble submission to Christ's body (the church), intentional formation in the contemplative life, nurturing common life among members, support for celibate singles alongside covenanted relationships, geographical proximity to community members, care for the earth, peacemaking in the midst of violence and conflict, and commitment to a rule of life for the whole community.

The model draws consciously from Benedictine monasticism — particularly the Rule, stability, and daily office — adapted for voluntary non-residential or semi-residential communities in urban neighborhoods rather than enclosed monasteries.

### What Made It Work

**1. Proximity as the condition for genuine mutual knowledge.** Members choosing to live near each other — not necessarily under one roof, but within a neighborhood — created enough geographic proximity for the mutual knowledge that the *allēlōn* commands require. The mechanism is not the residential requirement itself but what residential proximity produces: people knowing one another's daily lives, being present for one another's struggles, and bearing the costs of genuine community rather than the convenience of parallel presence. Where this proximity was sustained, communities developed *sympatheia* — the shared registration of each other's states — that most suburban congregations do not achieve.

**2. Mission-integrated formation.** Formation happened through serving neighbors together, not through extracted formation programs. Sharing a meal with someone experiencing homelessness, repairing a neighbor's house, or practicing hospitality to a stranger is itself a formation event — it disrupts consumer formation patterns, engages the body in practices that form desire differently from consumption, and creates conditions the Spirit works through that a classroom setting does not provide.

**3. Shared rule of life as between-session formation structure.** A common covenant governing the community's rhythms — prayer, meals, economic sharing, service, Sabbath — provided the formation structure that individual discipline alone rarely sustains. The rule creates accountability without enforcement: members who observe it feel the formation it produces; those who drift feel the loss. This is the Benedictine principle translated into voluntary form.

### Track Record — Honest Assessment

The track record is mixed and must be stated plainly. Communities that have sustained more than 10 years are rare. The Simple Way and Rutba House are notable exceptions. The majority of communities that formed in the first decade of the movement dissolved within 3–7 years. The formation architecture (rhythm, proximity, shared rule) is sound; the dissolution rate is real and should not be explained away.

### What Caused Failure

**1. Residential burnout.** The demands of shared life in under-resourced neighborhoods, combined with inadequate formation infrastructure for the members themselves, exceeded what communities could sustain over time. Formation requires formation of the formers; communities that did not build this in were consuming their founders.

**2. Mobility.** Members moved away for employment, family, or health — and the proximity the community's functioning depended on dissolved with them. The stability the model requires is more fragile in a mobile culture than the model's designers acknowledged.

**3. Founder-dependency.** Formation depth concentrated in one or two founding members; when they left or burned out, the formation culture collapsed. This is the same failure mode as the Pietist *collegia* — no mechanism for producing the next generation of formed leaders from within the community.

**4. Ideological frame displacement.** In some communities, political activist identity increasingly defined the community's purpose, with formation becoming instrumental to the activist agenda rather than the other way around. The formation posture inverted: the community existed to do justice rather than to be formed toward Christlikeness, with justice as a fruit.

### Transferability Assessment

| Feature | Transferable? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Proximity as formation condition | Yes — as principle | The minimum is enough shared life for genuine mutual knowledge; residential co-location is the maximum expression, not the mechanism |
| Shared rule of life | Yes — most directly transferable | The mechanism; specific content is contextually variable |
| Mission-integrated formation (formation through service together) | Yes | Service to neighbors as a cohort formation practice, not merely outreach |
| Economic mutual aid and sharing | Partial | Full resource pooling is not transferable; economic solidarity and mutual aid practices are |
| Residential requirement | No | Maximum expression; not transferable to most parish contexts |
| Urban poverty location specificity | No | Content-specific, not mechanism |
| Formation of the formers | Yes — and critical | The failure mode (founder burnout) is a formation infrastructure problem; Phase 2 cohort design must address this |

**Verdict:** New Monasticism is the only model in this set designed explicitly for the modern Western voluntary context — making it the most immediately relevant reference for constraint navigation, if not for direct replication. Its dissolution rate is a warning against treating the form as the mechanism. The mechanism — enough shared life to produce genuine mutual knowledge, organized around a common rule, through mission together — is transferable at a range of commitment levels. Phase 2 cohort design can draw from this without requiring residential commitment: shared meals outside formal meetings, economic mutual aid practices, neighborhood presence together, and a between-session rule of life adopted at cohort entry are all accessible expressions of the same mechanism.

## Cross-Model Synthesis

### What All Four Models Share

These features appear across all four models without exception. Their universality across different eras, ecclesiologies, and cultural contexts is the strongest evidence that they are not contextual features but mechanism features — structural requirements for formation-at-scale.

**1. Small group container (8–15 people)**
No model attempted formation-at-scale through the large assembly. All four used a small-group structure as the primary formation unit. The small assembly is where *koinōnia*, *sympatheia*, and the *allēlōn* functions can actually operate. Formation in the large assembly is not what any of these models describe.

**2. Formed facilitator as the load-bearing element**
Every model fails at the same point: when the facilitator is not formed. Wesley's class meeting produced formation where class leaders were formed and supervised; it produced drift or performance where they weren't. The Benedictine novitiate produces formed monks where the novice master is formed; it produces externalism where he isn't. The *collegia pietatis* worked while Spener and his formed successors led; it drifted into emotionalism when that formation chain broke. The catechumenate's community teachers were themselves catechetically formed. This is the most consistent finding across all four models: the facilitator's *hexis* is the container. The model is only as strong as the formed practitioner holding it.

**3. Interior-state focus, not content delivery**
"How is it with your soul?" (class meeting) — exorcism and character assessment (catechumenate) — *conversatio morum* (novitiate) — mutual encouragement toward godly living (Pietist groups). All four models are asking the same question: what is actually happening in you, and is the formation reaching the implicit level? Content delivery is present in some (catechumenate instruction, Pietist Bible study) but it is always instrumental to the interior-state question, never the primary goal.

**4. Formation as prerequisite to full participation** (in three of four)
The catechumenate, the novitiate, and the class meeting all structure formation as the *condition* for fuller participation — not as an optional enhancement for the already-motivated. The *collegia pietatis* is the exception (it was fully voluntary), and voluntary formation is also where it showed the earliest weakness. The formation-first structure is not elitist; it is body-protective (1 Cor 11:17–22 is the NT precedent) and practically necessary for sustaining a formation culture in a consumer environment.

**5. Extended time arc (minimum 1–2 years)**
No model assumed that *hexis* could be formed in a short program. The catechumenate ran 1–3 years; the novitiate ran a minimum of a year before profession; the class meeting was an ongoing structure with no exit date; the *collegia pietatis* groups were sustained indefinitely. Hebrews 12:11 — "later it yields the peaceful fruit" — is the biblical basis, and the historical record confirms it: formation that produces *hexis* (not just insight or emotional experience) takes 18 months minimum and 3–5 years to produce the fruit visible in corporate assembly.

**6. Between-session structure sustaining formation between meetings**
The novitiate's *horarium*, the catechumenate's fasting and daily prayer, the class meeting's weekly accountability creating pressure between meetings, the *collegia pietatis'* expectation of practical application between gatherings. Formation does not only happen in the meeting. All four models built a structure for the intervals.

**7. Community observation and assessment**
In all four models, the community — not just the individual — assessed formation. Sponsors in the catechumenate. The novice master and monastic community in the novitiate. Class leaders reporting to Wesley on class members. Spener's group members exhorting one another. Formation was not a private project assessed by the individual alone; it was a communal project observed communally.

### What Is Transferable to an Evangelical Congregation

- Small group container (8–12 members)
- Interior-state question as the primary meeting structure
- Formed facilitator as the non-negotiable prerequisite
- Covenant entry structure with full disclosure of what formation requires (Benedictine informed consent)
- *Conversatio morum* frame (formation as ongoing direction, not completed program)
- Between-session rule of life for cohort members
- Community observation and honest assessment of one another's formation
- Lay facilitation (reduces clergy bandwidth dependency; enables scaling)
- Addition to, not replacement of, Sunday assembly (avoids separatism)

### What Is Not Transferable

- Mandatory participation with membership consequences (ticket system) — requires a formation culture to already exist before it is possible; cannot be imposed to create the culture
- Total immersion in common life (Benedictine) — parish context cannot provide 24/7 formation environment
- Formal vow structure — can be approximated by a covenant but not replicated
- Pre-admission exclusion from full assembly (catechumenate) — structurally possible but would require significant congregational culture shift that is itself Phase 3 territory, not Phase 1

### The Two-Tiered Church Problem

Every model that achieved genuine formation culture also produced a perceived two-tiered church: the formed cohort and the rest of the congregation. Spener was accused of separatism. Wesley's societies were sometimes perceived as elitist. The catechumenate literally had two tiers (catechumens and the faithful). The Benedictine monastery is structurally apart from the parish.

This is not a bug to be fixed before formation can begin; it is a feature that formation produces. A formed group will function differently from an unformed group. The goal is not to prevent the difference from being visible — it is to prevent the difference from being experienced as exclusion. The catechumenate addressed this by making the formation track fully open and the exclusion temporary (the catechumen was on their way to full participation). The class meeting addressed this by making class membership the entry point for society participation — the structure was open to anyone willing to commit.

Phase 2 cohort design must address this directly. The two-tiered church problem is not solved by making the cohort invisible; it is addressed by making the cohort's formation visibly available rather than exclusive.

## Transferability Criteria Framework

The following five criteria provide a structured basis for evaluating whether a historical formation mechanism is transferable to a modern Western evangelical congregational context. They are design judgments derived from the cross-model synthesis above — stated explicitly so they can be applied, tested, and revised as Phase 2 design proceeds. They are not derived conclusions; treat them as a working evaluative framework.

### The Five Criteria

**Criterion 1: Institutional Authority Dependency**
Does the mechanism require coercive or juridical authority to function — authority that can deny membership, enforce vows, or impose consequences for non-participation? Mechanisms requiring this kind of authority are not transferable to voluntary congregational contexts where no such authority exists. Mechanisms that benefit from *relational* authority (not coercive) are transferable if the relational conditions can be created.

**Criterion 2: Geographic/Residential Dependency**
Does the mechanism require physical proximity over sustained time — residential co-location or geographic stability — to produce its outcome? The operative question is not "does it require a monastery?" but "does it require enough shared life to produce genuine mutual knowledge?" Mechanisms requiring permanent residential commitment are not transferable; mechanisms requiring sufficient sustained proximity for mutual knowledge are partially transferable — the proximity can be created by structural commitment without requiring residential vows.

**Criterion 3: Content vs. Form Specificity**
Is the mechanism dependent on specific liturgical content, theological vocabulary, or cultural context — or on the *form* and structure itself? The catechumenate's exorcism rites are content-specific to their cultural context; the formation-prerequisite-to-participation logic and the sponsor-accountability relationship are form-specific and transferable. Separate the form from the content before evaluating transferability.

**Criterion 4: Exit Availability and Entry Cost**
Does the mechanism require high entry commitment and low exit availability to function? In voluntary evangelical contexts, exit is always available at zero cost. Mechanisms that depend on captive participants or coercive retention are not transferable. Mechanisms that generate intrinsic motivation to remain — through genuine community, visible formation, relational investment — are transferable because they create functional stability without requiring coercive retention. Covenant entry (commitment made explicitly at the outset) approximates the entry-cost function without enforcement.

**Criterion 5: Scalability of the Core Function**
Can the core mechanism function in a small group of 8–15 people within a larger congregation, rather than requiring full-congregation participation or a self-contained institution? All six models operate in small units or cohort form. None scale directly to the full congregation. Transferability means: can this function as a sub-unit architecture that feeds formation back into the larger assembly?

### What "Works" Means for This Track

A mechanism transfers successfully if it produces, in a modern Western voluntary congregational sub-structure, the conditions necessary for communal formation toward the telos named in *The Telos of a Formed Congregation*: mutual knowledge sufficient for *sympatheia*, enough sustained proximity for the *allēlōn* commands to function, and accountable between-session formation practice. The indicator at 12–18 months: does this group know one another well enough to bear one another's burdens in the Galatians 6:2 sense? Is there observable formation that is not self-reported?

## Mechanism Implications for This Track

The four models converge on a mechanism architecture. Phase 2 (Remnant Cohort Design and Formation Track Design) should build from this:

**The covenant cohort model** — drawing primarily from the Wesleyan class meeting with elements from the catechumenate (formation-before-participation logic, community assessment), the Benedictine novitiate (informed consent structure, *conversatio morum* frame, between-session rule of life), and the Pietist model (non-separatist, lay-led, addition to Sunday assembly):

- **Container:** 6–10 members (small enough for genuine *koinōnia*; large enough to sustain *sympatheia* when members miss a week)
- **Entry structure:** Covenant, not enrollment — full disclosure of what formation requires before commitment; explicit consent to the arc duration and between-session structure
- **Facilitator:** Formed prior to facilitating; the load-bearing element; not assigned by availability
- **Primary question:** Oriented toward interior state, not content; structured around *katanoeō* (prior consideration of others) and *dokimazō* (self-examination) before meeting
- **Between-session structure:** A rule of life agreed to at covenant entry; not optional; the formation mechanism operates between meetings, not only in them
- **Arc:** 18–24 months minimum covenant commitment; longer-arc formation possible by cohort renewal
- **Assessment:** Communal, not only self-assessed; cohort members and facilitator observe one another's formation over the arc
- **Relationship to assembly:** Formation feeds into the Sunday assembly; the cohort is not an alternative assembly; formed members eventually constitute the functioning *ekklēsia* body within the larger congregation

This is the mechanism this track's Phase 2 documents should design into. The specific practices, questions, and arc structure are Phase 2 territory.

## What This Document Doesn't Resolve

**The facilitator pipeline problem.** Every model fails when the formed facilitator chain breaks. This document identifies the problem but does not design the solution. Phase 2 must include a deliberate mechanism for producing formed facilitators from within each cohort — so the second cohort does not require the pastor to be the only facilitator.

**The two-tiered church problem in practice.** Named above; not resolved. The design question — how to offer cohort formation visibly and openly without it reading as exclusion — is Phase 2 work.

**The transition from voluntary to structural.** The ticket system worked; voluntary formation doesn't persist in a consumer culture. What is the evangelical-context equivalent of structural accountability that doesn't require membership consequences that the culture won't support? This is an open question. The covenant entry structure partially addresses it (commitment made at entry rather than defaulted to); it does not fully resolve it.

**Emotional experience vs. *hexis* formation.** The Pietist drift into emotionalism (feeling as formation indicator) is a persistent risk. The formation track needs explicit markers for *hexis* formation that are distinct from emotional experience. Phase 2 must name these markers or it will produce the same drift.

# Formation & Transition — Pastoral Formation Pathway Assessment

## Purpose

The Development Plan establishes in First Things §4 that a formation-to-*ekklēsia* transition is not reliably available to a congregation whose lead pastor has not been through formation. The gateway question is not *"does our pastor believe this?"* but *"has our pastor been through this?"*

Phase 1 Item 6 asks the prior question: **does any currently existing pastoral formation pathway reliably deliver what §4 requires — and is it accessible to a pastor today?**

This is the most structurally dangerous open question in the development track. If no available pathway delivers the gateway condition, then either (a) the scope must expand to build or designate one, or (b) the gateway condition must be restated as aspirational. Either answer changes the roadmap. This document establishes which.

*Complete when:* at least two existing pathways have been assessed against §4's gateway criteria with a specific verdict — delivers, partially delivers, or does not deliver — and the roadmap implication is stated.

---

## Part 1 — The Gateway Criteria

First Things §4 describes what the pastor must have received, not merely what they must believe or intend. The formation must produce three things:

**1. Stable interior.** Not reactive, not defended, not managing anxiety through control. The pastor's interior is settled enough that the presence of others' dysregulation — an anxious elder board, an angry consumer member, a financially stressed congregation — does not destabilize their own interior state. This is not emotional flatness or indifference; it is the capacity to remain present and non-reactive without disengaging.

**2. Non-anxious regulatory capacity.** The ability to hold steady under sustained pastoral pressure. This goes beyond isolated composure; it describes *hexis* — a habitual, trained capacity that does not require moment-by-moment willpower management. Under criticism, attendance loss, financial pressure, or relational conflict, the pastor's regulatory capacity holds without reverting to control, performance, or avoidance.

**3. *Hexis* that holds under cost.** Implicit-level formation that has been tested under real pressure and does not revert. The formation must have occurred at the implicit level — not the level of knowledge or declared intention, but the level of embodied, habitual response. And it must have been tested: a pastor who received genuine formation in a protected container but has not yet carried it under congregational pressure has not yet met the gateway condition. The transition will test the *hexis* at a level the formation process may not have yet reached.

**What the gateway criteria exclude:**

These criteria are not met by:
- Seminary training (informational formation; does not address implicit-level patterns)
- Correct theology about formation (knowledge of the mechanism is not the mechanism)
- Years of faithful pastoral ministry (faithfulness can coexist with defended interiority and affirmation-dependence)
- Sincere spiritual disciplines practiced privately (self-directed disciplines without a formed guide cannot reach the practitioner's own blind spots)
- A genuine encounter with God (significant spiritual experience does not produce sustained *hexis* without formation to anchor it)

The criterion is not "has this pastor tried hard enough?" It is "has this pastor's implicit-level patterns actually been addressed?" These are different questions with different answers.

## Part 2 — Pathway Assessments

### Pathway 1: Spiritual Direction (Traditional)

**What it is.** A regular relationship (typically monthly, one hour per session) with a trained director who listens for God's movement in the directee's life. The director's role is primarily receptive and reflective — attending to the directee's interior movements (consolation/desolation in Ignatian terms; affections, resistances, and the Spirit's presence in the broader tradition) and occasionally naming what the directee cannot yet see. ==The tradition has roots in the Desert Fathers' *abba/amma* relationship; the contemporary form varies widely in depth, training, and quality.

**Assessment against gateway criteria:**

*Contact and frequency.* Monthly one-hour sessions = 12 hours of direct formation contact per year. Implicit-level *hexis* is formed through repeated relational exposure across varied emotional contexts and sustained over time — not through monthly reflection conversations. Frequency is the primary structural limitation. Spiritual direction, as typically practiced, does not provide sufficient contact for implicit-level pattern reformation. A director may notice the pattern; the formation contact to shift it is unlikely to be sufficient in the standard structure.

*Quality ceiling.* The best practitioners in this tradition — formed themselves through extended direction, contemplative practice, and supervised experience — can create conditions for genuine interior movement that reaches implicit-level patterns. The history of extended spiritual direction (the Desert tradition, Ignatian direction over years, the *starets* relationships in Eastern Orthodox formation) shows that sustained direction with a highly formed guide can produce the stable interior §4 names. The literature on contemplative formation (Martin Laird, *Into the Silent Land*; Thomas Keating's centering prayer integrated with direction; Thomas Merton's writings on the formation relationship) supports this claim. The word *can* is load-bearing here: delivery depends entirely on the director's own formation.

*Accessibility.* Trained, genuinely formed spiritual directors are unevenly distributed. The market for spiritual direction includes practitioners whose own formation is limited, producing relationships that operate at the level of emotional processing and reflective conversation — not implicit-level formation. A pastor seeking spiritual direction is unlikely to be able to assess in advance whether the director is formed enough to hold what §4 requires.

*Structural targeting.* Traditional spiritual direction is not structured to address the specific pastoral-formation gaps §4 names: identity built around ministry role, reactivity to criticism, non-anxious presence under congregational pressure, the false self constructed around pastoral competency and approval. A skilled director who notices these patterns can attend to them; the traditional structure does not guarantee they will be surfaced and addressed.

**Verdict: Partially delivers.**

Extended direction over 3+ years with a highly formed director, working at contemplative depth and explicitly attending to implicit-level patterns, can produce the stable interior §4 requires. The median case — monthly sessions with a director of average formation — produces reflective insight and increased self-awareness but insufficient implicit-level *hexis* formation. Delivery depends on the director's formation and the relationship's capacity to reach below the level of reflection. Not reliably accessible; not structurally targeted at pastoral-specific implicit patterns; no quality assurance mechanism is present in most contexts.

### Pathway 2: Covenant Identity Coaching

**What it is.** The CI formation arc applied in a 1:1 relational container with a formed CI practitioner: affections diagnostic → false identity surfacing → covenant truth received → between-session disciplines sustaining formation between sessions. The explicit mechanism: Spirit as primary agent, coaching relationship creates the formation context, diagnostic work surfaces the false identity structures driving behavior, covenant truth is applied and received at the level where the false identity operated, between-session disciplines sustain and deepen what the Spirit is working.

**Assessment against gateway criteria:**

*Mechanism fit.* CI coaching is the mechanism most precisely aligned with what §4 requires. The affections diagnostic is designed to reach below presenting behavior to the ordered and disordered desires driving it — the implicit level. The false identity work is designed to surface the identity constructions that drive reactive, defended, or performance-oriented behavior — which in a pastor are most commonly organized around ministry role, affirmation from congregation, and theological competency as identity anchor. The between-session disciplines are designed to sustain formation between sessions and create conditions for the Spirit's continued work. The mechanism matches the gateway criteria specifically, not just generally.

*Pastoral-specific gap.* The standard CI arc does not automatically orient toward pastoral-specific implicit patterns. A CI practitioner working with a general coaching client will surface whatever presenting material the client brings. A pastoral client is likely to have presenting material about ministry challenges, congregational dynamics, and vocational questions — material that *contains* the relevant implicit patterns but does not automatically surface them as the diagnostic target. A CI practitioner working with a pastoral leader must hold the pastoral-specific patterns (identity-built-around-role, affirmation-dependence, reactivity to criticism, non-anxious presence) as explicit diagnostic targets rather than allowing the presenting material to define the work's scope.

This is possible within the CI model. It requires intentional case formulation by a practitioner who understands both the CI mechanism and the specific implicit-pattern landscape of pastoral ministry.

*Duration.* The CI arc at standard pace runs 12-18 months. Implicit-level *hexis* that "holds under cost" — the third gateway criterion — requires the formation to be tested under real pressure, not merely received in session. A pastor who completes a CI formation arc and immediately attempts a formation-to-*ekklēsia* transition may find the *hexis* has not yet been tested at the level the transition will require. The formation that holds under the pressure of the transition is likely not the same formation that was completed in the protected arc. The gateway condition in its full sense requires formation *and* testing — probably through a period in which the pastor is in active ministry under real pressure while the formation continues.

*Practitioner supply.* CI practitioners with sufficient formation to hold the container for a pastoral leader are a limited supply. This limits accessibility at present.

**Verdict: Can deliver, conditionally.**

CI coaching is the most mechanism-appropriate existing pathway for producing the gateway condition §4 requires. Conditions for delivery: (1) intentional targeting of pastoral-specific implicit patterns, not just presenting material; (2) sufficient duration — likely 12-18 months minimum, with a period in which the *hexis* is tested under real pastoral pressure; (3) a practitioner with both CI competency and understanding of the pastoral implicit-pattern landscape. Not widely accessible at present, given limited practitioner supply. When the conditions are met, CI coaching is the pathway most likely to produce what §4 names.

### Pathway 3: Extended Ignatian Formation (30-Day Spiritual Exercises)

**What it is.** The full Spiritual Exercises of Ignatius of Loyola — a 30-day directed retreat moving through four Weeks (purgation, meditation on the life of Christ, the Passion, the Resurrection) with a trained director in daily sessions. The Exercises have a 500-year track record and are the most intensive non-monastic formation program widely available to working ministers. The "19th Annotation" version spreads the Exercises over 9-12 months of daily prayer alongside ordinary life, with monthly director meetings.

**Assessment against gateway criteria:**

*30-day form.* The full retreat produces significant interior movement and is widely attested to be transformative. The Exercises are explicitly oriented toward *interior freedom* (Ignatian *indifference* — the capacity to respond to what God actually wills rather than what the self-protective interior prefers). This is close to what §4 names as stable interior and non-anxious regulatory capacity. Directors who hold the full Exercises are typically among the most formation-competent practitioners available.

Limitation: 30 days produces a formation *experience*, not a formed *hexis*. Re-entry to normal pastoral life after an intensive retreat typically involves significant reversion — the environmental conditions of the formation do not persist, and implicit-level patterns that are quiet under retreat conditions reassert under operational stress. What the full Exercises reliably produce: significant interior shift, clarity about the movements of consolation and desolation, and measurably more attentive interior life. What they cannot guarantee: sustained *hexis* that holds under sustained pastoral pressure without ongoing formation support.

Sustained impact from the Exercises depends heavily on what happens afterward: ongoing direction, continued contemplative practice, integration into ministry decision-making. Without the integration work, the Exercises are more likely to produce a significant experience with lasting orientation change than a *hexis* that has been tested and holds under cost.

*19th Annotation form.* Slower and more integrated into ordinary life, which addresses the re-entry limitation. The extended timeline (9-12 months) creates more opportunity for the formation to be tested under operational conditions. However, the monthly director contact (rather than daily) significantly reduces the formation's intensity and the director's capacity to hold the work at depth. More accessible; less formation-dense.

*Accessibility.* Trained Ignatian directors are more widely available than well-formed CI practitioners, and retreat centers offering the full Exercises exist in most regions. The 19th Annotation form is broadly accessible. The full 30-day retreat requires a significant block of protected time — a structural barrier for most active pastors.

**Verdict: 30-day form partially delivers.** Significant interior movement likely; sustained *hexis* that holds under cost requires ongoing integration work after the retreat. Without that integration, the Exercises produce a strong formation experience whose lasting effects depend on what the directee does with it afterward. The 19th Annotation form is more accessible and better integrated but lower-density and more dependent on the individual's own discipline. **Short retreat programs (8-day or less) do not deliver** — insufficient contact and continuity for implicit-level *hexis* formation; these function as restorative and reflective experiences, not formation at the implicit level.

### Pathway 4: Peer Accountability Structures

**What they are.** Regular meetings with a peer group — pastoral cohorts, accountability partnerships, denominational peer groups, Promise Keepers-style accountability structures — organized around mutual disclosure, behavioral accountability, and encouragement. Typical structure: regular meeting, honest sharing about behavioral struggles, mutual exhortation toward the biblical standard.

**Assessment against gateway criteria:**

*Structural orientation.* Peer accountability structures as typically practiced operate at the behavioral level. The orienting frame is corrective: identify the behavioral failure, hold the standard, provide encouragement to do better. This produces behavioral accountability; it does not produce implicit-level formation.

The reason is structural, not incidental: implicit-level *hexis* formation requires a relational container with asymmetry — someone who is further along the formation arc, who can perceive what the person being formed cannot yet see, and who can hold the formation posture without shifting to correction. A peer cannot provide this because a peer shares the same implicit-level blind spots. The accountability partner who is equally unformed cannot see the formation gap clearly enough to name it, let alone hold the space for the Spirit to work in it.

A pastoral peer group could in principle name the question correctly — "what is God doing in your interior?" — but without a formed guide holding that space, the conversation will typically resolve toward the explicit (what we are managing) rather than the implicit (what is managing us without our knowing it).

*Potential exception.* A structured pastor cohort held by a formed facilitator who orients the group toward interior state, practices *katanoeō* (other-oriented attentiveness), and can name implicit-level patterns would approach formation. This is extremely rare in practice; the facilitation requirement is the same as the gateway condition — a formed guide is required to hold formed space.

**Verdict: Does not deliver.** As typically practiced, peer accountability is corrective in structure and behavioral in scope. It may support a pastor already in formation; it cannot be the primary formation pathway. A structured cohort with a formed facilitator could partially deliver — but the form is rare, and the facilitator requirement simply moves the gateway condition one level up rather than solving it.

## Part 3 — Synthesis Finding

**No widely available pastoral formation pathway reliably delivers the gateway condition First Things §4 names.**

The standard available pathways for pastoral "formation" — seminary, conferences, short retreats, peer accountability, privately practiced spiritual disciplines — do not produce implicit-level *hexis*. They produce knowledge, skill, inspiration, and behavioral accountability. These are not formation at the level §4 requires.

The closest existing pathway to the gateway condition is CI coaching applied with intentional targeting of pastoral-specific implicit patterns and sufficient duration. The 30-day Ignatian Exercises can produce significant interior movement and partially deliver, particularly when followed by ongoing direction or CI engagement that integrates and sustains what the Exercises initiated.

Across all four pathways, two variables determine delivery more than any structural feature:
1. **The formation level of the guide.** A formed guide holding a well-structured pathway is more likely to deliver than an unformed guide holding a theoretically superior one. The formation flows downward — which is precisely First Things §4's argument applied one level up.
2. **Duration and testing.** The formation must be sustained long enough to be tested under real pastoral pressure. No program of weeks or months is sufficient for the *hexis* that holds under the cost the transition will extract. The formation process is measured in years, not program cycles.

## Part 4 — Roadmap Implication

The gateway condition as stated in First Things §4 is currently aspirational for the vast majority of pastoral leaders. It is the right criterion — the Development Plan should not lower it — but it cannot function as a binary gate (either met or not met) without eliminating nearly all potential deployment. Two complementary responses are required:

**Response A — Restate §4 for near-term deployment (adopt now).**

A pastor who is *actively in formation* — engaged in CI coaching, extended spiritual direction with a formed director, or sustained Ignatian direction with integration practice — and who demonstrates the disposition markers of the formation in progress (growing non-reactivity, increasing capacity to be with others' dysregulation without absorbing or managing it, willingness to have their own implicit patterns named and held) may be able to begin the transition before the gateway condition is fully met, under two conditions:

1. The transition is explicitly understood as an extension of their own formation (First Things §6 — the transition is *paideia*), not as something they are now prepared to lead from a place of arrival
2. They remain in active formation — the formation pathway is not completed before the transition begins; it continues through it

This is not a lowered bar. It is a recognition that the formation arc is not a program with a graduation date. The pastor in active formation under a formed guide, with genuine *hexis* beginning to show under pressure, is a different case than the pastor who has accumulated information about formation and declared themselves ready.

**Response B — Scope expansion for long-term development (flag for future Phase 1 addition).**

The track should eventually include: a designated pastoral formation pathway adapted from the CI arc, specifically targeting the implicit-pattern landscape of pastoral ministry — role-based identity, affirmation-dependence, non-anxious regulatory capacity under congregational pressure, and the capacity to hold formation posture under the corrective pressure of institutional leadership. This would function as the track's own answer to the gateway condition rather than depending on external pathways to deliver it.

This is a Phase 1 addition — a potential Item 7, designated in the Development Plan but not yet built. It cannot be built before the existing Phase 1 items are complete, because the criterion for "delivers" requires the Leader Formation Prerequisite Assessment (Item 3) to establish exactly what the pathway must produce. Once Item 3 is complete, the pathway design follows from it directly.

The two responses are not alternatives — they address different timeframes. Response A governs who can attempt the transition now. Response B governs what the track eventually builds.

## Part 5 — Practical Deployment Guidance

For a pastor actively considering whether they meet the gateway condition, or approaching this development track from the outside, the following questions are more useful than a checklist:

**1. Who is your formed guide?** Not: who do you meet with for accountability, mentoring, or theological input. The specific question: is there a person in your life whose formation is further along than yours — who can see what you cannot yet see about your own interior — and with whom you have an ongoing relationship specifically oriented toward your interior life, not your ministry performance?

**2. Has your interior been under pressure this year — and how did it hold?** Not: have you been busy or stressed? But: in the moments of highest relational, financial, or credibility pressure, what did your interior do? Did the formation hold, or did you revert to control, performance, or avoidance? Honesty with this question is itself a formation marker.

**3. Are you in active formation or preparing to enter it?** The gateway condition is met by an ongoing process, not a past program. If the formation pathway was completed more than two years ago without continuing engagement, the *hexis* testing question (third gateway criterion) applies: what has it held under since?

**4. Can you receive "not yet" about this without reorganizing around proving it wrong?** The inability to receive a "not yet" verdict without becoming defensive or immediately pivoting to a plan to qualify is itself a diagnostic indicator that the interior stability the gateway condition names is not yet present.

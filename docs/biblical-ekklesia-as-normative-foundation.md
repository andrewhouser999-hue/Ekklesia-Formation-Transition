# Formation & Transition — The Biblical Ekklēsia as Normative Foundation

---

## Purpose

This document makes the theological case for why genuine formation-to-*ekklēsia* transition must be governed by the biblical portrait of the *ekklēsia* — not by organizational design, pragmatic effectiveness, or sociological analysis. It is the warrant document for the track: the theological basis from which pastoral leaders draw the authority to call a congregation toward structural reform, and the standard against which every structural decision in that reform is evaluated.

Two things this document does that no other track document does: it establishes *why* the biblical *ekklēsia* must remain the governing reference point throughout the reform (not only at the start), and it specifies *what adequate grounding actually produces* — the four questions pastoral leadership must be able to answer before structural decisions are made. The methodology for conducting that examination lives in the Congregational Discernment Guide (Phase 2, Item 9). This document establishes why the examination is non-negotiable and what it must establish.

What this document does NOT address:
- What the communal telos of a formed congregation is (→ *Formation & Transition — The Telos of a Formed Congregation*)
- How the *sympatheia/allēlōn/paideia* mechanism works (→ *Assembled, Not Attending*; *Trained, Not Consumed*)
- What historical architectures have produced formation at scale (→ *Formation & Transition — Scaling Mechanism Research*)
- Cultural conditions generating demand for genuine *ekklēsia* (→ *Formation & Transition — The Demand Landscape*)
- The step-by-step examination process for pastoral discernment (→ Phase 2, Item 9 — not yet built)

## Part 1 — The Claim, Stated Precisely

The claim this document argues is not that the Bible gives a single structural blueprint every congregation must replicate. The NT does not provide that. The claim is:

**The biblical portrait of the *ekklēsia* establishes non-negotiables — about telos, communal ontology, constitutive practices, and leadership formation — that are normative for any gathering of Christ's people in any era. Any structural reform toward genuine formation must be *governed* by these non-negotiables throughout the reform process. Reform that is not so governed lacks the theological authority to call a congregation to what is costly, and lacks the diagnostic standard to know whether the reform is working.**

Three clarifications prevent misreading.

**Governing means active throughout, not consulted once.** The critical question for any congregation attempting this transition is not "did we start with the biblical *ekklēsia*?" but "is the biblical *ekklēsia* currently governing our structural decisions?" The examination is not a starting gate. It is the ongoing reference point against which every design decision — cohort structure, gathering form, leadership qualification, formation arc — must be evaluated. When it ceases to govern, the reform reverts to the inherited assumptions it displaced, because those assumptions are load-bearing in the congregation's culture and will reassert themselves under pressure. What structures and practices keep the biblical portrait as the active reference point — rather than a document consulted once and set aside — is Phase 2 design work. This document names the requirement. The cohort design and congregational discernment documents (Phase 2, Items 7 and 9) will need to build the mechanism for holding it.

**Normative does not mean prescriptive in every detail.** The biblical portrait leaves open what is contextually variable: meeting forms, polity structures, building use, size, frequency. These are wisdom questions every congregation and culture navigates differently. What is NOT left open is what the *ekklēsia* exists to display (its telos), what constitutes it (the Spirit through Christ's presence, not voluntary preference), what makes it function (*sympatheia*, *allēlōn* commands, *koinōnia* operating through formed members), and what kind of leadership it requires (leaders whose *hexis* holds the formation the body needs). Part 3 specifies each of these. Part 5 maps the normative/variable distinction in detail.

**This is a normative priority claim, not a chronological sequence claim.** Whether the biblical *ekklēsia* examination is literally the first thing a congregation does is a contextual judgment — some communities have arrived at formative structures through other entry points and then discovered their grounding retroactively. What cannot vary: structural decisions made without this governing standard active are running on inherited assumptions, and those assumptions will determine the outcome regardless of what the reform intended. Beginning with and returning to the biblical *ekklēsia* removes the most dangerous source of drift — but the requirement is that it *governs*, not merely that it *precedes*.

## Part 2 — Where the Inherited Structure Came From

Before the positive case can be made, the diagnostic must be honest: the structure most Western Protestant congregations operate in was not designed from the biblical *ekklēsia*. This is not a criticism of the people who built and maintained those structures — many served faithfully within them. It is a historical fact that matters enormously for reform, because every reform effort that has not understood it has reproduced what it intended to replace.

The arrangement historians call Christendom — in which the church occupied the center of a nominally Christian culture and the population assumed Christian identity as the default — produced a church designed for maintenance, not formation. When most people in a society identify as Christian, the church's primary task becomes nurturing that existing population: preaching to those who already attend, managing the religious dimensions of life transitions, sustaining the institution that organizes Christian community. Formation — the sustained, costly process of re-shaping a person's implicit-level patterns toward Christlikeness — was not the urgent priority. The population was already nominally Christian. The task was to service and sustain what was present.

Lesslie Newbigin, who spent 35 years as a missionary in India before returning to England in 1974, describes the shock of recognizing that England had become a mission field while its church remained organized as a maintenance institution. His diagnosis (*The Gospel in a Pluralist Society*, 1989): the Western church had been so shaped by Christendom assumptions that it had lost the capacity to understand its own missionary nature. It was designed to receive people from a broadly Christian culture. That culture no longer existed. The design had not been updated.

Alan Hirsch (*The Forgotten Ways*, 2006) names this "Christendom captivity" with structural precision. The attractional-institutional model — come to our building, receive our programs, belong to our community — was built for the Christendom situation. In that situation, Sunday services, professional clergy, and program delivery made sense as the primary formation architecture because the population would come and formation was assumed to happen through exposure. The model was coherent for its situation. The situation changed. The assumptions did not.

**The track record confirms the diagnosis.** From roughly 1970 to 2000, Western Protestant churches undertook a sustained period of structural reform: the seeker-sensitive church, the megachurch model, the small-group movement, the purpose-driven church. Each was a structural reform aimed at more effective discipleship and community. Each worked from within the inherited frame — asking how to make the existing institution more effective — without subjecting that frame to the governing standard of the biblical *ekklēsia*. The Guder-led *Missional Church* project (1998) was the theological response to this pattern — an attempt to reconnect structural reform to NT ecclesiology after a generation of pragmatic restructuring had failed. Hirsch and Roxburgh document the subsequent iteration of the same failure: "missional" language grafted onto attractional structures, producing vocabulary change without formation change. Small groups producing belonging community but not *hexis*-level formation. Purpose-driven clarity of mission statement among leaders while the congregation continued in consumption mode, because the structural assumptions underneath the mission statement were never evaluated against the biblical portrait.

The documented pattern: every reform that works from within the inherited framework, without the biblical *ekklēsia* actively governing structural decisions, produces a variation of the same fundamental failure — structure optimized for what the institution is currently designed to do rather than for what the *ekklēsia* is designed to be. The reform inherits the problem because it inherits the frame.

What this means for the present track: the primary question for any congregation is not "what changes will make our structure more effective?" but "what does the *ekklēsia* Scripture describes actually require, and which of our current structural assumptions are incompatible with it?" The first question accepts the inherited frame. The second questions it from outside. Only the second is capable of producing something the inherited frame cannot produce on its own terms.

## Part 3 — Four Theological Arguments

### 1. The warrant for reform must be theological — pragmatic arguments do not carry authority where it counts

A pragmatic case for structural reform — "this approach produces more genuine discipleship," "research shows formation improves spiritual health," "other congregations have tried this and it works" — can always be contested with other pragmatic evidence. Every structural reform in church history that has relied on an effectiveness argument has eventually faced a counter-effectiveness argument, and the most resistant voices in any congregation will produce one. Pragmatic cases do not carry the weight to call people toward what is genuinely costly over a genuinely long arc.

What carries that weight is the claim: this is what we are called by Scripture to be. Paul Minear's demonstration that the NT offers 96 distinct images of the church (*Images of the Church in the NT*, 1960) is more hermeneutically significant than any specific image: if the biblical portrait of the *ekklēsia* is that rich, then any inherited institutional form has captured only a fraction of what the text describes. The complete biblical portrait is the standard against which every institutional form must be judged — and no institutional form, compared against that portrait, comes out looking complete.

Goheen documents the failure of the first generation of the missional church movement precisely at this point (*A Light to the Nations*, 2011; *The Church and Its Vocation*, 2018). It grounded its case for structural reform in effectiveness rather than biblical ecclesiology. Congregations adopted missional language while their structures remained attractional, because the effectiveness argument did not carry theological authority to change what was actually load-bearing in the congregation's self-understanding.

A pastoral leader holding a congregation to a costly, long-arc reform — one that will produce consumer member attrition, criticism, and years of ambiguous fruit — needs the authority that comes from a theological claim: "We are not yet what we are called to be, and this is what being it requires." Not "this works better" but "we are responsible before God for becoming what he constituted us to be." That authority comes from the biblical *ekklēsia*, examined and applied with honesty. It does not come from organizational effectiveness research, however well-designed.

### 2. Telos must govern structure — you cannot design toward what you have not defined

Dallas Willard (*The Divine Conspiracy*, 1998; *The Great Omission*, 2006): the church's formation failure is not primarily structural — it is intentional. The church has not been seriously intending to form disciples. It has been intending to attract attenders and manage their religious participation. Structure follows intention, and if the intention is not formation, no structural reform produces it.

But behind intention lies telos. You cannot intend toward what you have not defined. The Great Commission (Matthew 28:19–20) is to make disciples — not converts, not attenders, not morally improved citizens, but learners and followers of Jesus, equipped to live and teach everything he commanded. That is the telos. The biblical *ekklēsia* is the community designed to pursue and embody it.

Newbigin's extension: "The congregation is the hermeneutic of the gospel" (*The Gospel in a Pluralist Society*, p. 227). The form of the congregation is not neutral — it either embodies or contradicts the gospel it is preaching. A congregation organized around the delivery of religious goods to passive consumers does not merely fail to produce disciples — it communicates something about the gospel that contradicts what is being proclaimed. The structure is always already a theological claim about what the church is for.

Without the telos the biblical *ekklēsia* establishes, every structural design decision — cohort structure, formation arc, leadership prerequisites, congregational entry points — has no fixed reference point. It evaluates itself against effectiveness criteria borrowed from the organizational world rather than against what the *ekklēsia* is designed to become. The telos is not available from any source other than the biblical portrait.

### 3. The *ekklēsia* has a defined ontology — it is not a voluntary association

Knowing what the *ekklēsia* is for (Argument 2) raises the deeper question: what IS it? The answer determines whether reform is organizational renovation or something categorically different.

The *ekklēsia* is not a human community that meets for religious purposes. It is a community constituted by the Spirit through Christ's presence. Bonhoeffer's *Sanctorum Communio* (1927) establishes this: the church is "Christ existing as community." This is not metaphor. The Ephesians 2:22 construction — *synoikodomeisthe*, "being built together simultaneously" — is Paul's ontological description: the dwelling of God by the Spirit is located in the joined community in a way that dispersed individuals, unjoined, do not replicate. [*Interpretive note: Individual Spirit-indwelling of believers is not denied. Paul's argument is that the community's joined life produces something additional. Volf, After Our Likeness (1998), maintains both.*]

What this means for reform: if the *ekklēsia* is constituted by the Spirit rather than voluntarily assembled by preference, then structural reform is not organizational renovation of a human institution. It is the recovery of conditions under which the Spirit constitutes what he designed. A structure built on Christendom maintenance assumptions suppresses those conditions because it was not designed to create them. The biblical portrait is the only source of knowledge about what conditions the Spirit uses — because the Spirit is the one who produces *ekklēsia*, and Scripture is where his constitutive work and its conditions are described.

Without this ontological clarity, reform drifts toward organizational improvement: better programming, more connected community, higher-quality experience. These are not wrong goals — they are the wrong category. A reform that doesn't account for this category has not yet begun to reform the right thing, and no pragmatic effectiveness argument can close the gap between organizational improvement and Spirit-constituted *ekklēsia*.

### 4. Formation is communal by ontological necessity — not programmatic by institutional choice

The *ekklēsia*'s ontology (Argument 3) has a direct formation consequence: if the community is Spirit-constituted with designed communal formation capacities, then formation is not something the church programs — it is something the church IS when it functions as designed.

The track's existing documents have established that individual formation and communal function are recursively related (*The Telos of a Formed Congregation* — the formative dynamic section). Ephesians 4:16: "the whole body, joined and held together by every joint with which it is equipped, when each part is working properly, makes the body grow so that it builds itself up in love." The body's functioning is not merely the expression of a telos already achieved. It is the progressive achievement of that telos. The body builds itself up through the act of building itself up.

Certain formation can only happen within the assembled body: the formation of mutual knowledge, the exercise of *allēlōn* functions under real conditions, the experience of *sympatheia*, the development of regulatory capacity through co-regulation with formed others. Private spiritual disciplines do not produce these. They require the body functioning as a body, with formed members present, over time.

Structural reform that ignores what the community IS — as a Spirit-constituted body with designed communal formation capacities — will design conditions for private spiritual development with a communal wrapper. Small groups that meet for study and prayer but never create conditions for *sympatheia* to operate; accountability structures that are corrective rather than formative; ministry programming that produces service without the mutual knowledge that makes genuine *koinōnia* possible. These are not failures of effort. They are the predictable results of designing formation structure without knowing what the body is constituted to be and how it is constituted to form. The governing question the biblical *ekklēsia* supplies for every Phase 2 design decision — does this architecture create conditions in which the body's designed formation mechanism can actually operate? — cannot be asked without the biblical portrait as the governing reference.

## Part 4 — What Adequate Grounding Requires

The argument so far establishes that the biblical *ekklēsia* must govern structural reform decisions. This section specifies what "adequately governed" actually means in a pastoral leadership context — four questions that must be genuinely engaged before structural decisions are made.

These are not questions a leadership team answers once by reading a book. They require sustained engagement — study, pastoral conversation, honest comparison of the biblical portrait against the congregation's current reality, and the willingness to let the answers land with diagnostic weight rather than theoretical interest. The distinction between a leadership team that has engaged these questions and one that has read about them is the same distinction the founding documents name between *hexis* and information: the difference is visible under pressure, when the reform costs more than expected.

One prior framing note governs all four questions: the examination this section describes is Spirit-guided discernment, not human analysis applied to a congregation. First Things §1 establishes that God is the architect and the primary agent — and this governs the examination itself. The four questions below identify what must be established before structural decisions are made; they do not establish it. The establishing is Spirit-operative: the Spirit surfaces what is true about this congregation's situation as the leadership team attends to him in sustained prayer, honest conversation, and willingness to receive answers that complicate rather than confirm their existing direction. A leadership team that approaches these questions as a theological checklist will produce informed self-assessment. A team that approaches them as Spirit-dependent discernment will produce something the reform can actually stand on. The difference between those two modes is not incidental — it determines the quality of what the examination can reveal.

**Question 1 — Telos:** What is this community constituted to display, and which current structural features make that corporate display impossible regardless of the spiritual quality of individual members?

A pastoral team adequately grounded in this question can name the *ekklēsia*'s communal telos in specific terms (not "grow in Christ" generically but: the fullness of Christ displayed through differentiated unity; the dwelling of God by the Spirit in the joined community; a corporate witness legible to the world). They can also name, specifically, which features of current structure foreclose that display architecturally — not because individuals are failing but because the architecture precludes it. Without this specificity, reform lacks direction. With it, design decisions have a fixed reference point.

**Question 2 — Ontology:** What constitutes this assembly as *ekklēsia* rather than a religious gathering, and which features of current structure operate on a different constitutive principle?

A pastoral team adequately grounded here understands the categorical difference between a Spirit-constituted covenant assembly and a voluntarily-gathered consumer community — and can name where their current structure is built on the second principle while aspiring to the first. This is the question that exposes the deepest layer of Christendom captivity: the structures that feel institutional and neutral but are in fact organized around consumer preference as the governing logic. Until a leadership team can name this, they cannot design differently from it.

**Question 3 — Constitutive practices:** Which practices are non-negotiable for the *ekklēsia* to function, and which of these are currently structurally foreclosed rather than merely underdeveloped?

A pastoral team adequately grounded here can distinguish between practices that are *absent* because members haven't been taught them (an educational problem) and practices that are *impossible* because the architecture precludes them (a structural problem). The *allēlōn* corpus requires mutual knowledge — you cannot bear the burdens you don't know exist. *Sympatheia* requires enough relational depth that a member's suffering registers before it is announced. *Koinōnia* requires shared stake. Where the architecture structurally forecloses these — platform-only delivery, anonymity as a feature, no mechanism for between-session life — no amount of exhortation produces them. The question must be answered structurally, not pastorally.

**Question 4 — Leadership formation:** Has the pastoral leadership team done the personal formation work the gateway condition requires, or is the congregation being called to something its leaders have not yet personally entered?

First Things §4 establishes the gateway condition: not theological knowledge about formation but *hexis* that holds under pastoral pressure. A leader who has not been through genuine formation will revert under pressure — consumer attrition, criticism, financial stress — to the pattern their *hexis* has been formed in, regardless of their sincere intention. The formation posture the transition requires cannot be maintained from the outside of formation.

One complication the track has already surfaced must be named here honestly (→ *Formation & Transition — Pastoral Formation Pathway Assessment*, Phase 1, Item 6): no widely available pastoral formation pathway reliably delivers the gateway condition. Spiritual direction partially delivers under specific conditions; CI coaching can deliver conditionally; the Ignatian Exercises partially deliver with sustained post-retreat integration; peer accountability does not deliver. The gateway condition is genuinely difficult to access for most pastoral leaders today.

This does not make the question unanswerable, but it changes how it must be answered. The binary — "our leadership has been through formation / has not" — is rarely the actual situation. The honest answer for most leadership teams will be one of three: the gateway condition has been substantially met; the lead pastor is actively engaged in a formation process with realistic prospect of meeting it; or the gateway condition is not being pursued. The first opens the door to transition. The second opens the door conditionally — the transition may begin under the named constraint that the lead pastor remains actively in formation, with that constraint held and visible to the leadership team. The third is a genuine prerequisite gap that structural reform alone cannot fill.

This question must be answered at the level of *hexis*, not aspiration — and it cannot be answered accurately in isolation. It requires outside perspective from someone who has known the pastor long enough to observe the pattern under cost: a spiritual director, a formed guide, or a peer with that kind of sustained knowledge. Self-assessment here is the least reliable form of engagement.

**The threshold and where it lives:** These four questions identify what the examination must establish before structural decisions are made. They do not supply the criteria for when each question has been adequately engaged — that is the work of the Congregational Discernment Guide (Phase 2, Item 9), which will specify the texts, the discernment process, and the return conditions for each question. This document tells a leadership team what the examination is for; the Guide tells them how to conduct it and when it has been sufficiently completed. A leadership team that works through Part 4 as a self-assessment checklist — outside the examination process the Guide will prescribe — will produce informed theological reflection. That is not the same as the grounded discernment the transition requires.

## Part 5 — What the Biblical Portrait Establishes as Normative

The most serious objection to this document's argument is the "which *ekklēsia*?" problem: Baptist, Presbyterian, Anglican, and Catholic traditions all claim to be following the biblical *ekklēsia* while arriving at structurally incompatible conclusions. The answer is specificity. The normative claims are not about structural details where genuine diversity exists — they are about what the *ekklēsia* IS.

**Normative — not contextually variable:**

*Telos.* The *ekklēsia* exists to display the fullness of Christ through the body's differentiated unity (Ephesians 4:11–16), to be the dwelling of God by the Spirit in the joined community (Ephesians 2:22), and to constitute a corporate witness legible to the world (John 13:35; 1 Peter 2:9). This is established in *The Telos of a Formed Congregation*. The normative implication: any structural decision that makes this corporate telos less achievable is not a contextual adaptation — it is a departure from what the *ekklēsia* is designed to be.

*Communal ontology.* The *ekklēsia* is constituted by the Spirit through gathering in Christ's name, not voluntarily assembled by shared preference. A community organized around consumer preference has replaced the constitutive principle of the *ekklēsia* with a different one, and that replacement cannot be undone by adding formation programs to the existing structure.

*Constitutive practices.* The *allēlōn* corpus, the *koinōnia* of Acts 2:42–45, the *sympatheia* of 1 Corinthians 12:26, and the *katanoeō* of Hebrews 10:24 are not optional features of *ekklēsia* life. They are what makes the assembled community an *ekklēsia* rather than an audience. A structure that makes these practices structurally impossible is not a variation of the *ekklēsia*. It is a different kind of gathering with a religious frame around it.

*Leadership formation chain.* 1 Timothy 3, Titus 1, Acts 20, and 1 Peter 5 are formation diagnostics, not governance documents. The elder's *hexis* is a necessary condition for the congregation's formation to proceed (*Formation & Transition — Polity Foundation*). The specific governance structures (elder plurality, congregational polity, episcopacy) are contextually variable. The formation requirement underlying whatever structure a congregation operates is not.

**Contextually variable — wisdom questions, not normative claims:**

Meeting forms, building use, frequency of gathering, polity governance structures beyond the formation requirement, programming, organizational size, financial structures, cultural expression — every congregation and cultural context answers these differently. The biblical portrait does not resolve them, and this track makes no normative claims about them. This is not a house-church argument, a small-church argument, or an argument against any polity tradition.

The practical implication: when pastoral leadership engages these two layers honestly, they will find that many structural decisions currently treated as institutional givens are contextually variable — and that the few that are not variable are precisely the ones that most determine whether genuine formation is possible.

## Part 6 — Honest Acknowledgments

**The NT does not present a single ecclesiological model.** Acts churches look different from Pauline churches; early and late NT writings reflect different structural moments. The normative claims in Part 5 are drawn from the family of resemblances across that diversity, not from a single prescriptive model. The claim that the non-negotiables are genuinely non-negotiable across that diversity is an argued theological position, not a reading obvious on the face of the texts. *(Strong inference.)*

**"Normative vs. descriptive" is itself a hermeneutical judgment.** Some theological traditions restrict normativity to explicit commands and reject the normativity of descriptive patterns. The document's use of this distinction in Part 5 is argued from the theological significance of what is being described, not from an uncontested hermeneutical rule. *(Strong inference.)*

**Christendom captivity is diagnosed at the pattern level, not proven for every congregation.** Its application to any specific congregation requires local diagnostic work. Some congregations have recovered more of the biblical *ekklēsia* than the general pattern suggests. The Constraint Matrix and the Congregational Discernment Guide (Phase 2) are where local diagnostic work happens. *(Fact at the pattern level; strong inference for any specific application.)*

# Covenant Identity — Assembled, Not Attending — Greek & Hebrew Word Study

**Purpose:** A full lexical reference for every Greek and Hebrew term used in *Assembled, Not Attending*, plus one additional term (*telos*, entry 21) from the sibling document *The Telos of a Formed Congregation*, added by request. Organized in document order. Each entry covers: etymology, semantic range, key canonical occurrences, how translation choices shift meaning, and interpretive debates where they exist. Intended as a companion for the practitioner who wants to trace the precision behind the document's exegetical claims.

**Abbreviations:** BDAG = Bauer, Danker, Arndt, Gingrich (*A Greek-English Lexicon of the New Testament*); LSJ = Liddell, Scott, Jones (*A Greek-English Lexicon*); BDB = Brown, Driver, Briggs (*Hebrew Lexicon*); LXX = Septuagint; ESV throughout.

## 1. ἐκκλησία (*ekklēsia*)

**Transliteration:** ek-klē-SIE-ah
**Greek:** ek (out of) + kalein (to call) → "the called-out ones," "the summoned assembly"

### Etymology
In classical Greek, the *ekklēsia* was the civic assembly of a Greek city-state — specifically the gathering of enfranchised citizens convened by a herald (*kēryx*) to conduct public business: legislation, deliberation, voting. Not a voluntary gathering; a *summoned* one. Membership was constituted by civic status, not preference. The assembly existed to transact common affairs.

### LXX Usage — *qahal* bridge
The LXX translators chose *ekklēsia* to render the Hebrew קָהָל (*qahal*) in approximately 100 occurrences — Israel's covenant assembly before God. This is the theological freight the NT inherits. See entry for *qahal* below.

### NT Semantic Range
**114 occurrences.** Three distinct referents:
- **Local assembly:** "the *ekklēsia* in Corinth" (1 Cor 1:2), "the *ekklēsia* in their house" (Rom 16:5). The gathered believers in a specific place.
- **Universal church:** "the *ekklēsia* of God" (1 Cor 15:9), "the head of the *ekklēsia*" (Eph 1:22; Col 1:18). The whole people of God across time and place.
- **Gathering-event:** Acts 19:32, 39, 41 — *ekklēsia* used for a secular civic assembly, preserving the original Greek sense and marking the contrast with Christian usage.

**Critical structural point:** *Ekklēsia* never refers to a building, a time slot, or a program in any NT occurrence. It always refers to *people*, either assembled or as a corporate body. You do not go *to* the *ekklēsia*; you *are* the *ekklēsia*, or you are not.

### Translation Issue
"Church" (the standard English rendering) carries three centuries of institutional meaning — buildings, denominations, services, clergy/laity divisions — none of which the word itself conveys. The damage is significant: "going to church" maps onto *ekklēsia* only if the original word meant "attending a program," which it did not. "Assembly" (used in some translations for Acts 19 and occasionally elsewhere) preserves the original sense better but creates inconsistency.

### Interpretive Debate
Whether the "universal church" Pauline uses represent a development beyond the earlier "local assembly" uses (i.e., whether Paul's ecclesiology became more cosmic over time) is debated in Pauline studies (compare Baird, Käsemann, and Thiselton). The theological freight does not materially shift for the argument in *Assembled, Not Attending*, but the local-assembly focus (1 Cor 11-14) is the primary domain of Paul's *ekklēsia* critique.

## 2. קָהָל (*qahal*)

**Transliteration:** kah-HAHL
**Hebrew root:** *qahal* — to assemble, to gather together

### Etymology and OT Semantic Range
*Qahal* appears 123 times in the OT (noun and verb forms). Two primary categories:
1. **General assembly** — any gathering of people (warriors, a mob, a council). Context-dependent.
2. **Covenant assembly** — Israel gathered before God, especially in liturgical, legal, or military covenant contexts. This is the theologically loaded usage.

**BDB:** "assembly, company, congregation, multitude" — with the covenant-assembly sense as the dominant theological register.

### Key Covenant Occurrences
- **Deut 9:10; 10:4; 18:16** — "the day of the assembly" (*qahal*) at Sinai: the definitive event in which God spoke to Israel from fire. The *qahal* of the LORD is the summoned covenant people standing before him at the mountain.
- **Num 16:3** — "all in the congregation (*qahal*) are holy." Korah's argument that every member of the *qahal* already has standing before God; Moses's response shows that the *qahal* is governed by the LORD's arrangement, not by popular assertion.
- **Deut 23:1–8** — "none may enter the assembly (*qahal*) of the LORD" — entry criteria for the covenant assembly, organized by categories of exclusion. Shows that *qahal* has constitutive requirements, not just physical presence.
- **Neh 13:1** — "no Ammonite or Moabite should ever enter the assembly (*qahal*) of God."
- **Ps 22:22, 25** — "in the midst of the congregation (*qahal*) I will praise you" — the psalmist's vow to praise in the corporate assembly after God's deliverance; picked up in Heb 2:12.

### LXX Bridge to NT
The LXX predominantly renders *qahal* as *ekklēsia* (vs. *synagōgē*, which renders *ʿedah* — a different Hebrew word for congregation/community). This translation choice was deliberate and theologically consequential: NT writers who use *ekklēsia* are importing the entire covenantal, summoned-people-before-God semantic field of the Hebrew *qahal*.

**The implication:** the NT *ekklēsia* is not a voluntary association of people with shared religious preferences. It is *Israel's covenant-assembly pattern fulfilled in Christ* — a people constituted by covenant relationship with God, summoned to present themselves before him together. This is why Paul can apply *qahal*-level judgments ("not for the better but for the worse," 1 Cor 11:17) to the Corinthian gathering — they are being measured against the *qahal* standard.

## 3. ἐπισυναγωγή (*episynagōgē*)

**Transliteration:** ep-ee-soon-ah-goh-GAY
**Greek:** epi (upon, intensifying prefix) + syn (together) + agein (to bring/lead) → "a purposive, concentrated gathering"

### NT Occurrences — Only Two
This is an extremely rare word. Only two NT occurrences:
1. **Heb 10:25** — "not neglecting to meet together (*episynagōgē*), as is the habit of some."
2. **2 Thess 2:1** — "our being gathered together (*episynagōgē*) to him [Christ]" — the eschatological reunion of believers with Christ at his coming.

### The *epi-* Prefix
The intensifying *epi-* prefix distinguishes *episynagōgē* from the simpler *synagōgē* or *synerchomai*. It suggests deliberate, purposive, concentrated gathering — not casual proximity. The word implies intentionality about *what the gathering is for*.

### Eschatological Register
The 2 Thess 2:1 occurrence is significant for reading Heb 10:25. The only other NT use of this word is eschatological — the final gathering of God's people to Christ. The Heb 10:25 context ("all the more as you see the Day drawing near") may deliberately echo this: the assembly of believers *now* anticipates and participates in the *episynagōgē* of Christ's return. This gives the gathering an eschatological weight that "don't skip church" completely misses.

### Translation Issue
Most translations render *episynagōgē* in Heb 10:25 as "to meet together" or "assembling together" — accurate but thin. The eschatological overtone of the word's only other use, combined with the *epi-* intensifier, suggests something closer to "purposive, covenantal gathering in view of the Day."

### Critical Structural Note
Heb 10:25 names *episynagōgē* as the *means* ("not neglecting... but encouraging one another"). The purpose of the *episynagōgē* is the *parakalontes heautous* — the encouragement actually happening between members. The *episynagōgē* is the container; the *allēlōn* function is the content. The text does not equate them.

## 4. συνέρχομαι (*synerchomai*)

**Transliteration:** soon-ER-khom-eye
**Greek:** syn (together, with) + erchomai (to come, to go) → "to come together, to assemble"

### NT Usage Pattern — 1 Corinthians 11–14
Paul uses *synerchomai* repeatedly in 1 Cor 11–14 to mark the physical event of gathering:
- 11:17 — "when you come together it is not for the better but for the worse"
- 11:18 — "when you come together as a church"
- 11:20 — "when you come together, it is not the Lord's supper that you eat"
- 11:33 — "when you come together to eat, wait for one another"
- 11:34 — "so that when you come together it will not be for judgment"
- 14:23 — "if, therefore, the whole church comes together"
- 14:26 — "when you come together, each one has..."

### The Key Point — Moral Neutrality
*Synerchomai* in itself is *morally neutral*. It simply describes the physical act of gathering. Paul uses the *same word* for both genuine assembly (14:26: "when you come together, each one has...") and failed assembly (11:17: "when you come together it is not for the better"). The word does not determine whether genuine *ekklēsia* is occurring. What happens *among those gathered* does.

This is the NT's own precedent for distinguishing physical gathering from genuine assembly. The linguistic evidence is in Paul's own usage — he can describe a failed gathering and a genuine gathering with the same verb.

## 5. προσκαρτερέω (*proskartereō*)

**Transliteration:** pros-kar-ter-EH-oh
**Participle form in Acts 2:42:** *proskarterountes* (present active participle, durative)
**Greek:** pros (toward, in relation to) + kartereō (to be strong, to endure, to persist) → "to persist steadily toward, to be constantly devoted to"

### BDAG
"To be devoted to, to persist in, to busy oneself with, to continue steadfastly."

### Aspect — Durative/Continuous
The present participle (*proskarterountes*) carries a durative aspect — not a single completed action but an ongoing state of orientation. "They were devoting themselves" continuously, not "they devoted themselves" once. This is the *structure of their common life*, not a program they attended.

### Key Occurrences
- **Acts 1:14** — the disciples "were devoting themselves to prayer" in the upper room before Pentecost. Same durative structure: the gathered community in sustained, corporate orientation toward God.
- **Acts 6:4** — apostles will "devote" themselves to prayer and the ministry of the word. The word describes vocational commitment.
- **Acts 2:46** — "day by day, attending the temple together" — the same pattern expressed differently.
- **Rom 13:6** — governing authorities are "attending to" (*proskarterountes*) their duties. Same word for steady functional commitment to an ongoing responsibility.
- **Col 4:2** — "Devote yourselves to prayer, being watchful in it with thanksgiving." *Proskartereite* — imperative form.
- **Mark 3:9** — Jesus told his disciples to have a boat ready for him — "in constant attendance" (*proskarterei*).

### Translation Issue
"Devoted themselves" (ESV) captures the directional commitment but doesn't convey the temporal aspect — that this was a *habitual structure*, not an event. "Were devoting themselves" would be more precise. The "fellowship" in Acts 2:42 was not an item on a schedule; *proskarterountes* describes how they oriented their whole common life.

## 6. κοινωνία (*koinōnia*)

**Transliteration:** koy-noh-NIE-ah
**Greek:** from *koinos* (common, shared, belonging to several) → *koinōneō* (to share in, participate in) → *koinōnia* (participation, sharing, community, fellowship)

### Semantic Range — BDAG
BDAG distinguishes four senses:
1. **Association, communion, relationship:** Gal 2:9 — James, Peter, and John gave Paul "the right hand of fellowship (*koinōnia*)" — formal recognition of shared participation in the mission.
2. **Participation in something:** Phil 3:10 — "the *koinōnia* of his sufferings" — sharing in Christ's suffering as a participatory reality, not a metaphor.
3. **Generous sharing of resources:** Rom 15:26; 2 Cor 9:13; Heb 13:16. *Koinōnia* as the concrete transfer of material resources to those in need.
4. **The community itself:** 1 John 1:3, 6, 7 — "our *koinōnia* is with the Father and with his Son Jesus Christ," "we have *koinōnia* with one another."

### The Economic Origin
*Koinos* (the root) is an economic term — "common property." *Koinōnos* is a business partner, one who holds a stake in a shared venture. The semantic range includes the financial/commercial domain from the beginning. This is not an accident: *koinōnia* was understood in the Greco-Roman world to carry implications of shared ownership, shared risk, and shared benefit.

### Acts 2 Context
Acts 2:42 lists *koinōnia* among the four marks of the early community: teaching, *koinōnia*, breaking of bread, prayers. Acts 2:44-45 immediately demonstrates what *koinōnia* looked like: "all who believed were together and had all things in common (*koina*), and they were selling their possessions and belongings and distributing the proceeds to all, as any had need." The *koinōnia* of v.42 is interpreted by vv.44-45 as a shared stake in one another's material lives.

### The Translation Problem
"Fellowship" (the standard English rendering) has deteriorated almost entirely into "pleasant social interaction among people with shared religious affiliation." This does not approach the Greek. *Koinōnia* requires:
- A *shared stake* — something held in common, not just shared feelings
- *Participation in* a common reality — Christ, the Spirit, the suffering, the mission
- In the Acts 2 context: *material movement* toward those in need

The post-service greeting and surface-level "how are you?" are not *koinōnia*. They are social pleasantry.

### Additional Significant Occurrence
**2 Cor 13:14** — "the *koinōnia* of the Holy Spirit" — the participation in, and sharing of, the Spirit's own life. This is the theological apex of the word: *koinōnia* names our participation in the triune life itself, which then constitutes the basis for member-to-member *koinōnia* in the body.

**Phil 1:5** — "your *koinōnia* in the gospel from the first day until now." Shared participation in the missionary enterprise — not just warm feelings about Paul.

## 7. ἀλλήλων (*allēlōn*)

**Transliteration:** al-LAY-lone (genitive); *allēlois* (dative); *allēlous* (accusative)
**Type:** Reciprocal pronoun derived from *allos* (other) + *allos* (other) — "of/to/for each other"

### Structure
*Allēlōn* is always *reciprocal* — the action must run in both directions. "Love one another" (ἀγαπᾶτε ἀλλήλους) cannot be fulfilled by one person loving another without the second loving in return. The commands require bilateral agency. This structurally rules out one-directional service delivery.

### The Full *Allēlōn* Corpus — Key Commands
The NT contains 40+ "one another" commands. Organized by theme:

**Love and honor:**
- Love one another (*agapāte allēlous*) — John 13:34-35; 15:12, 17; Rom 13:8; 1 Thess 3:12; 4:9; 1 Pet 1:22; 1 John 3:11, 23; 4:7, 11, 12; 2 John 5
- Be devoted to one another in brotherly love (*philadelphia*) — Rom 12:10
- Honor one another above yourselves — Rom 12:10
- Accept/welcome one another — Rom 15:7

**Building up:**
- Build up one another — 1 Thess 5:11
- Encourage one another — 1 Thess 4:18; 5:11; Heb 3:13; 10:25
- Admonish/instruct one another — Rom 15:14; Col 3:16
- Teach one another — Col 3:16
- Spur one another to love and good works — Heb 10:24

**Service and bearing:**
- Serve one another — Gal 5:13
- Bear one another's burdens — Gal 6:2
- Wash one another's feet — John 13:14
- Offer hospitality to one another — 1 Pet 4:9
- Serve one another with gifts — 1 Pet 4:10
- Submit to one another — Eph 5:21

**Forgiveness and reconciliation:**
- Forgive one another — Eph 4:32; Col 3:13
- Be kind to one another — Eph 4:32
- Bear with one another — Eph 4:2; Col 3:13
- Be patient with one another — Col 3:13

**Truth and confession:**
- Speak truth to one another — Eph 4:25
- Confess sins to one another — Jas 5:16
- Pray for one another — Jas 5:16

**Affective attunement:**
- Rejoice with those who rejoice, weep with those who weep — Rom 12:15 (the *allēlōn* structure is implied: all members participate, not just leaders)
- Live in harmony with one another — Rom 12:16
- Be humble toward one another — 1 Pet 5:5

**Prohibitions (reverse side of the commands):**
- Do not judge one another — Rom 14:13
- Do not speak evil of one another — Jas 4:11
- Do not grumble against one another — Jas 5:9
- Do not bite and devour one another — Gal 5:15
- Do not provoke or envy one another — Gal 5:26

### What the Corpus Represents
The *allēlōn* commands are not aspirational targets or ethical ideals layered onto an existing gathering. They are the NT's *behavioral specification* of what a functioning body *does* when it is functioning. Taken together, they constitute the job description of membership. A gathering where none of these are occurring is not a poorly performing *ekklēsia* — it may not be *ekklēsia* in Paul's sense at all.

## 8. παρακαλέω (*parakaleō*)

**Transliteration:** par-ah-kah-LEH-oh
**Participle in Heb 10:25:** *parakalontes* (present active, durative)
**Greek:** para (beside, alongside) + kaleō (to call) → "to call alongside, to summon to one's side"

### Semantic Range — Exceptionally Broad
*Parakaleō* is one of the NT's most semantically rich verbs. BDAG lists four primary registers:
1. **To comfort, console:** 2 Cor 1:3-7 ("God of all *paraklēsis*," repeated 8x in 7 verses)
2. **To exhort, urge, appeal:** Rom 12:1 — "I appeal (*parakaleō*) to you therefore, brothers"
3. **To encourage, strengthen:** 1 Thess 4:18; 5:11; Heb 10:25
4. **To request, beseech:** Phil 4:2; Acts 16:9

The same word carries all four meanings, with context determining which register is primary. This is not sloppiness — the semantic range reflects how closely comfort, encouragement, appeal, and request are related when one person comes *alongside* another.

### *Paraklētos* Connection
The noun *paraklētos* — the Spirit's title in John 14:16, 26; 15:26; 16:7 — is from the same root. The Spirit is "one called alongside" — Advocate, Helper, Counselor, Comforter (all translations used for different aspects of the same Greek term). This gives *parakaleō* a pneumatological gravity: when the assembly encourages one another (*parakalontes heautous*), they are doing — through one another — what the Spirit does by constitution.

### 2 Cor 1:3-7 — The Relational Chain
"Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, the Father of mercies and God of all comfort (*paraklēsis*), who *comforts* (*parakaleō*) us in all our affliction, so that we may be able to *comfort* (*parakaleō*) those who are in any affliction, with the comfort (*paraklēsis*) with which we ourselves are comforted by God." The chain is explicit: God comforts → we are equipped to comfort → we comfort others with *the same comfort*. Assembly is the structural context for this chain to operate.

### Heb 10:25 — Eschatological Urgency
"Encouraging one another *all the more as you see the Day drawing near.*" The *parakalontes* is tied to eschatological pressure. This is not casual encouragement; it is mutual strengthening in the face of what is coming. The urgency escalates as the Day approaches — the *allēlōn* function is not incidental to the Christian life but essential to endurance under eschatological conditions.

### Heb 3:13
"But *exhort* (*parakaleite*) one another every day, as long as it is called 'today,' that none of you may be hardened by the deceitfulness of sin." The frequency required — *every day* — is notable. The function Paul envisions is not a once-a-week service but a daily, member-to-member mutual exhortation. This calibrates expectations about what *parakalontes heautous* in Heb 10:25 is pointing toward.

## 9. οἰκοδομή (*oikodomē*)

**Transliteration:** oy-koh-doh-MAY
**Greek:** oikos (house, household) + domos/domeō (structure, to build) → "building, construction; the act of building; edification"

### Semantic Range
BDAG: "the erection of a building," "a building" (the physical structure), and by extension "the promotion of spiritual welfare, upbuilding, edification." The metaphorical use (strengthening persons) derives directly from the construction metaphor: progressive, cumulative, purposive work.

### Paul's Governing Criterion — 1 Corinthians 14
In 1 Cor 14, *oikodomē* appears 8 times as Paul's stated criterion for evaluating everything that happens in assembly:
- 14:3 — prophecy produces "*oikodomē*, encouragement, and consolation"
- 14:4 — speaking in tongues builds up (*oikodomeō*) oneself; prophecy builds up (*oikodomeō*) the church
- 14:5 — prophecy is greater "so that the church may be built up (*oikodomēn*)"
- 14:12 — "strive to excel in building up (*oikodomēn*) the church"
- 14:17 — "the other person is not built up (*oikodomeō*)"
- 14:26 — "Let all things be done for *oikodomēn*"

Verse 14:26 is the governing test: *every element of assembly* is evaluated by whether it contributes to *oikodomē*. Anything that does not build up fails Paul's criterion for genuine assembly practice.

### The Cumulative Metaphor
Construction is active, progressive, and cumulative — it requires a plan, materials, and sustained labor. The metaphor implies that *oikodomē* is not a single event but a building process over time, requiring sustained contribution from every member. A gathering where members leave no more built up than they arrived has, by Paul's criterion, failed its purpose.

### Key Occurrences Beyond 1 Cor 14
- **Rom 14:19** — "pursue what makes for peace and mutual *upbuilding* (*oikodomēn*)"
- **Rom 15:2** — "each of us is to please his neighbor for his good, to his *upbuilding* (*oikodomēn*)"
- **Eph 2:21** — the church as a holy temple "growing into a holy temple in the Lord" — *oikodomē* as the ongoing construction of the body
- **Eph 4:12** — gifts are given "for building up (*oikodomēn*) the body of Christ"
- **Eph 4:16** — "the body... builds itself up (*oikodomēn*) in love"
- **1 Thess 5:11** — "build up one another (*allēlous oikodomeite*)" — the *allēlōn* and *oikodomē* commands fused

## 10. οἰκονόμος (*oikonomos*)

**Transliteration:** oy-KON-oh-mos
**Greek:** oikos (house, household) + nomos (law, management, distribution) → "household manager, steward"

### Social-Historical Background
An *oikonomos* in the Greco-Roman world was typically a trusted slave or freedman who managed a wealthy household's affairs — controlled finances, supervised other slaves/servants, distributed resources, maintained accounts. He had significant delegated authority but *zero ownership*. Everything he managed belonged to the master (*kyrios*). His accountability was total: he would give an account of every resource entrusted to him.

### NT Semantic Range
- **Luke 12:42** — "the faithful and wise *oikonomos* whom his master (*kyrios*) sets over his household": the governing parable for stewardship responsibility
- **Luke 16:1-8** — "a rich man had a *oikonomos*": the parable of the dishonest manager, exploring the steward's accountability and the consequences of mismanagement
- **1 Cor 4:1-2** — "servants of Christ and *oikonomoi* of the mysteries of God. Moreover it is required of *oikonomoi* that they be found faithful." Apostles as household managers of the gospel.
- **Tit 1:7** — the bishop must be above reproach "as God's *oikonomos*"
- **1 Pet 4:10** — "as each has received a gift, use it to serve one another, as good *oikonomoi* of God's varied grace"

### The Structural Import for Assembly
1 Pet 4:10's stewardship frame applies *oikonomos* to *every believer's gifts*. The gift was given to you, but it belongs to the household — to God and to the members of his household. A member who sits passively in assembly, consuming without contributing, is not being humble or waiting appropriately. They are acting as though they own the resource entrusted to them, or as though they received nothing. Both are category errors about the nature of gifts.

"Each one has received a gift (*charisma*)" — the perfect tense (*elabon*) indicates completed past action with present consequence. The gift was already given. The *oikonomos* responsibility is simply to manage what was already entrusted.

## 11. συμπάσχω (*sympaschō*) / συμπαθής (*sympathēs*)

**Transliteration:** soom-PAS-khoh (verb); sym-path-EIS (adjective form in Heb 4:15: *sympathēsai* — aorist infinitive)
**Greek:** syn (with, together) + paschō (to suffer, to experience, to be affected) → "to suffer together with, to be affected together with"

### Two NT Occurrences
1. **1 Cor 12:26** — *ei pathei hen melos, sympathei panta ta melē* — "if one member suffers, all the members suffer together with it" (*sympaskhei*)
2. **Heb 4:15** — *ou gar echomen arkhierea mē dynamenon sympathēsai tais astheneiais hēmōn* — "we do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize (*sympathēsai*) with our weaknesses"

### Paschō — Broader than "Suffer"
*Paschō* itself means not only "to suffer" but more broadly "to be affected, to undergo, to experience" — any passive reception of an experience or state. BDAG: "to undergo an experience, be affected by something, suffer." This means *sympatheia* is the body's mechanism for being affected by *any* state of a member — suffering, joy, dysregulation, flourishing — not suffering alone.

### The Stoic and Medical Background — Structural, Not Emotional
*Sympatheia* (the noun form) was a technical term in Stoic philosophy and Hellenistic medicine *before* it entered NT Greek:
- **Galen** (2nd c. physician) used *sympatheia* for the organic interdependence of organs in a living body — how a condition in one organ *reverberates* throughout the whole system. A disease in the liver is registered in the skin; infection in one system produces systemic response. Not metaphor — structural claim about biological organization.
- **The Stoics** used *sympatheia* for cosmic interconnection — the universe as a living system whose parts are structurally responsive to one another's states.

The word is **not primarily emotional** in its philosophical register — it is *structural*. It names how a living system is *constituted*, not how people feel about each other.

### Grammar of 1 Cor 12:26 — Indicative, Not Imperative
This is the most important grammatical point in the *Assembled, Not Attending* document regarding *sympatheia*: "all the members *suffer together*" (*sympathei*) is **indicative mood** — a statement of *how the body is*, not an exhortation about *how members should behave*. Paul is making a structural claim about the body of Christ, not issuing an ethical appeal.

The body of Christ *is* wired for felt-with response to its members' states. This is its design — inherited from the Creator who designed organic bodies this way and who said *this body* is the body of Christ. Whether this mechanism is *functioning* in a given gathering — or whether defended interiors, numbing, or flooding are suppressing it — is a different question. The design is assumed as operational.

### Heb 4:15 — Christ's *Sympatheia*
"We do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize (*sympathēsai*) with our weaknesses, but one who in every respect has been tempted as we are, yet without sin." Christ's *sympatheia* is grounded in *having been through it* — incarnate experience, not theoretical compassion from safety. This is the model and the source for the body's *sympatheia*: felt-with capacity born of shared experience. The body participates in Christ's *sympatheia* as an outworking of union with him.

## 12. κατανοέω (*katanoeō*)

**Transliteration:** kah-tah-noh-EH-oh
**Hortatory subjunctive in Heb 10:24:** *katanoōmen* ("let us consider")
**Greek:** kata (down upon — intensifying prefix) + noeō (to perceive, to think, to understand) → "to fix the mind carefully down upon something, to observe with deliberate, concentrated attention"

### BDAG
"To think about carefully, to consider, to notice, to look at." The *kata-* prefix intensifies to the point of *focused, purposive intellectual engagement* — not passing thought.

### Key NT Occurrences
- **Heb 10:24** — "let us consider (*katanoōmen*) how to stir up one another to love and good works" — the *object* of the intensive focus is *allēlous* (one another), not God or oneself
- **Heb 3:1** — "consider (*katanoēsate*) Jesus, the apostle and high priest of our confession" — intensive contemplation of Christ; same word
- **Luke 12:24** — "consider (*katanoēsate*) the ravens" — attentive observation of natural phenomena as a lesson
- **Luke 12:27** — "consider (*katanoēsate*) the lilies how they grow"
- **Acts 11:6** — Peter "looking at it carefully (*atenísis*... *kateno*)" — attentive, deliberate visual examination of the sheet in the Cornelius vision
- **Luke 6:41** — "but do not notice (*ou katanoeis*) the log in your own eye" — the *ironic* use: failure to *katanoeō* oneself while *katanoeō*-ing the speck in another's eye

### The Critical Structural Point — Heb 10:24
In Heb 10:24, the command is: "let us *carefully* and *deliberately* fix our minds on [our object] — one another — in view of how to stir them up to love and good works." The direction of the intensive attention is *outward*, toward the other members. Not "prepare yourself for an experience" but "think carefully, in advance, about how your presence will function *for others*."

This is preparation language dressed as an ethic. The command assumes that this *katanoeō* act happens *before* you enter the assembly — not in the moment when you arrive. You cannot arrive already thinking carefully about others if you've been thinking only about yourself en route. *Katanoōmen* (hortatory subjunctive) calls for a prior, deliberate act of other-oriented consideration.

## 13. δοκιμάζω (*dokimazō*)

**Transliteration:** dok-ee-MAH-zoh
**Form in 1 Cor 11:28:** *dokimazeto* (present active imperative, 3rd sg.) — "let him examine/test"
**Greek:** from *dokimos* (tested, approved, genuine) → *dokimazō* (to test, to assay, to examine critically)

### Etymology — Metallurgical
*Dokimos* refers to metal that has been tested and proven genuine — as opposed to adulterated or counterfeit. *Dokimazō* is the act of *testing* metal to determine its authenticity. Applied to persons: assessing whether one's interior state is what one thinks it is, or whether it is adulterated by self-deception.

### BDAG
"To make a critical examination of something to determine its genuineness, put to the test, examine, interpret, verify."

### Key NT Occurrences
- **1 Cor 11:28** — "let a person examine (*dokimazeto*) himself, and so eat of the bread and drink of the cup" — self-examination before the Lord's Supper
- **Rom 12:2** — "that you may prove (*dokimazein*) what is the will of God, what is good and acceptable and perfect" — testing to determine what is genuinely from God
- **2 Cor 13:5** — "Examine (*peirazetē*) yourselves, to see whether you are in the faith. Test (*dokimazete*) yourselves" — note the pairing of two test-words; *dokimazō* is the deeper, more definitive examination
- **Gal 6:4** — "let each one test (*dokimazeto*) his own work, and then his reason to boast will be in himself alone and not in his neighbor"
- **1 Thess 5:21** — "test (*dokimazete*) everything; hold fast what is good"
- **Phil 1:10** — "so that you may approve (*dokimazein*) what is excellent, and so be pure and blameless"

### 1 Cor 11:28 — The Specific Examination Commanded
Paul commands this *dokimazō* specifically before the Lord's Supper because the failure he's correcting (11:17-22) is a failure of *recognizing the body* — the wealthy eating without the poor, social divisions reproduced in the assembly. The examination Paul commands is: *Am I in a posture to recognize the body that will be present?* The examination is not about general spiritual worthiness — it is specifically about whether the examiner can see and honor the other members as the body of Christ.

The aorist form (*dokimazeto* — completed action) suggests this is a *prior act* completed before participation, not an ongoing process during the meal.

### Extension Beyond the Supper
The logic extends to any genuine assembly: *genuine* assembly requires body-recognition, and body-recognition requires an examined interior — one that has registered its own state and is not about to project, guard, or consume unconsciously. The examination questions the interior's readiness for other-oriented presence.

## 14. ἡγέομαι (*hēgeomai*)

**Transliteration:** hay-GEH-oh-my
**Participial form in Phil 2:3:** *hēgoumenoi* (present active participle) — "counting, regarding, assessing"
**Greek:** to lead, to guide; in the cognitive domain: to think, to consider, to assess, to lead in judgment

### BDAG
"To engage in an intellectual process, consider, regard, think." Distinct from simple *nomizō* (to think) or *oiomai* (to suppose) — *hēgeomai* in the cognitive sense carries the connotation of an *evaluative leading*, a conclusion arrived at by deliberate assessment.

### The Military/Leadership Register
*Hēgeomai* is also the word for a military commander or leader (a *hēgemōn* — hence "hegemony"). In its cognitive use, it retains this flavor: not "I happen to feel" but "I have assessed the situation and arrived at a judgment." It is the cognitive act a commander performs when evaluating terrain or relative strength.

### Phil 2:3 — The Specific Command
*Hēgoumenoi allēlous hyperechontas heautōn* — "counting/assessing others as surpassing yourselves." This is not a command to feel humble or to suppress self-regard. It is a command to *actively reason your way to a specific conclusion* — that others matter more. The participial form suggests this assessment is ongoing (*hēgoumenoi* = "while continuing to count/assess").

### Key Occurrences
- **Phil 3:7-8** — Paul "counts (*hēgoumai*) as loss" his previous gains; "count (*hēgoumai*) them as rubbish." The same deliberate evaluative act applied to his own credentials: he has reasoned his way to a conclusion about their worth.
- **Phil 3:8** — "I count (*hēgoumai*) everything as loss because of the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord"
- **1 Tim 1:12** — "I thank him who has given me strength, Christ Jesus our Lord, because he judged (*hēgēsato*) me faithful"
- **Heb 11:26** — Moses "considered (*hēgēsamenos*) the reproach of Christ greater wealth than the treasures of Egypt" — the word for Moses's prior calculative act that determined his life direction
- **Heb 10:29** — the apostate who has "profaned (*hēgēsamenos*) the blood of the covenant" — the same word for a deliberate evaluative act, here in the negative: actively counting as common what God counts as holy

### Why This Is Preparation Language
Phil 2:3's *hēgoumenoi* is preparation language disguised as a posture ethic. The *prior cognitive-dispositional act* of assessing others as surpassing you must be completed *before* you enter the room — not negotiated in the moment when self-interest reasserts. Under activation (conflict, comparison, threat to status), you will revert to your default assessment. The only protection against this is having done the assessment so thoroughly and repeatedly that it has become *hexis* — a stable, formed disposition that operates without deliberation.

## 15. ἕξις (*hexis*)

**Transliteration:** HEX-iss
**Key text:** Heb 5:14 — *tōn dia tēn hexin ta aisthētēria gegymnasmena echontōn pros diakrisīn kalou te kai kakou* — "those who have their powers of discernment trained by constant practice to distinguish good from evil"

### Aristotelian Background — Technical Philosophical Term
*Hexis* is an Aristotelian technical term that entered Hellenistic Greek philosophical discourse. In Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* and *Categories*:

**Categories, 8b** — Aristotle distinguishes *diathesis* (a temporary condition or arrangement, easily reversed) from *hexis* (a stable, durable *state* or *disposition* — difficult to change, held firmly). A *hexis* is what you *have* so thoroughly that it *has you* — it shapes perception and response without deliberate effort.

**Nicomachean Ethics** — Aristotle's entire account of moral virtue (*aretē*) is built on *hexis*: virtue is a stable disposition of character (*hexis*) formed through habituated practice (*ethos* → *ethikos*). You become courageous by repeatedly performing courageous acts until courage becomes your default mode of response. The disposition operates without deliberation once formed. This is *hexis*.

### NT — One Occurrence
*Hexis* appears only once in the NT, at Heb 5:14. This makes the philosophical pedigree significant — the author of Hebrews is using a technical philosophical term for a stable, formed disposition. The argument: mature believers have faculties (*aisthētēria* — see below) that have been trained (*gymnazō*) through *hexis* (practice/habit) to distinguish good from evil.

### *Aisthētēria* — Organs of Perception
*Aisthētēria* (literally "sensory organs, faculties of perception") is used metaphorically for the *moral and spiritual perceptive capacity* — the capacity to discern, to read, to sense what is of God and what is not. The physical sensory analogy is operative: just as physical senses require development and practice to become precise (a trained musician hears what an untrained ear misses; a trained physician reads what an untrained observer cannot see), the spiritual perceptive faculty requires *hexis* — practiced formation — to function precisely.

### *Gymnazō* — The Method
The verb for how *hexis* is produced in Heb 5:14 is *gymnazō* — literally "to exercise, to train as an athlete" (from *gymnos* — naked, as athletes trained). The athletic training metaphor: formation of *hexis* requires disciplined, sustained, repeated practice under load. It does not happen by attending lectures or accumulating information. It happens by *doing* until the doing shapes the doer.

Same root appears in Heb 12:11 — "those who have been trained (*gegymnasmenos*) by [discipline]" — connecting *paideia* (formative discipline from God) with *gymnazō* (athletic training) and *hexis* (stable disposition) as a single formation chain.

### *Hexis* ≈ Implicit Memory
The document and its sibling draw an explicit bridge: *hexis*, as Aristotle and Hebrews define it, is functionally equivalent to what contemporary neuroscience calls *implicit memory* — the memory system that stores practiced patterns, dispositions, and responses that operate below conscious deliberation. This is not an anachronistic imposition; it is observing that both frameworks are describing the same phenomenon from different angles.

## 16. παιδεία (*paideia*)

**Transliteration:** pie-DAY-ah
**Greek:** from *pais* (child) → *paideuō* (to train, educate, discipline) → *paideia* (training, instruction, discipline, formation)

### Hellenistic Background
In Hellenistic culture, *paideia* was the comprehensive program of Greek education and character formation — not merely intellectual instruction but the cultivation of the *whole person* toward *aretē* (excellence/virtue). It encompassed: gymnastics, music, poetry, rhetoric, philosophy, civic responsibility. The goal was not information transfer but character formation — making a person capable of virtue and civic excellence. The NT takes this concept and reframes it around God's formative work.

### NT Semantic Range
**Two primary registers:**
1. **Corrective discipline:** Heb 12:5-11 — "the Lord disciplines (*paideuo*) the one he loves" (citing Prov 3:11-12); *paideia* appears 6 times in these 7 verses. God's discipline as a father's corrective formation of a child toward maturity.
2. **Formative instruction:** Eph 6:4 — "bring them up in the *paideia* and instruction (*nouthesia*) of the Lord"; 2 Tim 3:16 — "Scripture is profitable for teaching (*didaskalia*), reproof (*elenchos*), correction (*epanorthōsis*), and training in righteousness (*paideia en dikaiosynē*)."

### Heb 12:5-11 — The Formation Chain
"For the moment all *paideia* seems painful rather than pleasant, but later it yields the peaceful fruit of righteousness to those who have been trained (*gegymnasmenos*) by it." The chain: *paideia* → *gymnazō* → "peaceful fruit of righteousness." The same *gymnazō* root as Heb 5:14's *hexis* passage. The two texts form a pair: Heb 5:14 names the result (trained *hexis*); Heb 12:11 names the process (sustained *paideia* working through *gymnazō*).

### Titus 2:11-14 — Grace as the Trainer
"For the grace of God has appeared... *training* (*paideuousa*) us to renounce ungodliness and worldly passions, and to live self-controlled, upright, and godly lives." The *paideia* agent here is grace itself. The grammar is present participle (*paideuousa* — "training us," ongoing action). Grace is not just the basis of forgiveness; grace is the *formative agent* actively shaping the believer's character toward the telos.

## 17. νουθετέω (*noutheteō*)

**Transliteration:** noo-the-TEH-oh
**Form in 1 Thess 5:14:** *noutheteite* (present active imperative, 2nd pl.) — "admonish, warn"
**Greek:** nous (mind) + tithenai (to put, to place) → "to put something in the mind, to set before the mind"

### BDAG
"To counsel about avoidance or cessation of an improper course of conduct, admonish, warn, instruct." The word implies *verbal, corrective engagement* with a specific behavioral or motivational issue.

### Nouthetic Counseling
Jay Adams's nouthetic counseling movement (1970s) took its name from this word, emphasizing direct corrective instruction as the primary mode of care. The word does carry a corrective register but is not inherently harsh — the range extends to constructive instruction, not just rebuke.

### Key Occurrences
- **1 Thess 5:12** — "those who labor among you and are over you in the Lord and admonish (*nouthetountas*) you" — pastoral leadership function
- **1 Thess 5:14** — "admonish (*noutheteite*) the idle (*ataktous*)"
- **Rom 15:14** — "you yourselves are full of goodness, filled with all knowledge and able to instruct/admonish (*nouthetein*) one another" — *allēlōn* application of *noutheteō*
- **1 Cor 4:14** — "I do not write these things to make you ashamed, but to admonish (*nouthetōn*) you as my beloved children" — corrective but parental and loving in tone
- **Col 1:28** — "Him we proclaim, warning/admonishing (*nouthetountes*) every person and teaching (*didaskontes*) every person with all wisdom, that we may present every person mature in Christ"
- **Col 3:16** — "admonishing (*nouthetountes*) one another in all wisdom" — *allēlōn* function in assembly

### The Target — *ataktos*
In 1 Thess 5:14, *noutheteō* is directed specifically toward the *ataktous* — the disorderly or idle. *Ataktos* is a military term: soldiers out of formation, not fulfilling their assigned position. In Paul's context: members not fulfilling their responsibilities to the community. The response to this specific presentation is *verbal correction* — not consolation or gentle support, but direct engagement with the behavior.

**This is diagnostic information for the practitioner:** *noutheteō* is the wrong tool for *oligopsychos* (the fainthearted — see *paramytheomai*). Applying corrective admonition to a contracted, fearful, or grieving person crushes what is already fragile. The capacity to *read which presentation is present* and *deploy the right response* is exactly what *hexis* produces.

## 18. παραμυθέομαι (*paramytheomai*)

**Transliteration:** par-ah-moo-THEH-om-eye
**Form in 1 Thess 5:14:** *paramytheisthe* (present middle imperative, 2nd pl.) — "console, comfort, encourage"
**Greek:** para (beside, alongside) + mythos (word, speech, story) → "to speak alongside, to address with words that bring comfort"

### BDAG
"To speak in a friendly manner in order to alleviate grief or anxiety, comfort, console, cheer up." The word is *gentler in register* than *parakaleō* — more consolatory, less directive.

### Key Occurrences
- **1 Thess 2:12** — Paul "encouraging (*paramythoumenoi*) you and comforting you like a father with his children" — parental warmth; the context is urging believers to walk worthily
- **1 Thess 5:14** — "comfort/console (*paramytheisthe*) the fainthearted (*oligopsychous*)"
- **John 11:19** — Jews had come "to console (*paramythēsōntai*) them [Martha and Mary] concerning their brother"
- **John 11:31** — "supposing that she was going to the tomb to weep there, they followed her" — the crowd following Mary "*to console*" (*paramythēsontai*) her
- **Phil 2:1** — "any *comfort* (*paramythion*) from love" — *paramythion* is the noun form; Paul uses it to ground the appeal for unity

### The Target — *oligopsychos*
*Oligopsychos* — literally "small-souled" (*oligos* = small + *psychē* = soul/spirit). The person whose interior is contracted, whose spirit is diminished — the fainthearted, the discouraged, the person without interior resources to face what is coming. This is not a character failure; it is a state of depletion or fear.

The response to *oligopsychos* is *paramytheomai* — coming alongside with words that comfort and console, that make the contracted person feel less alone, that widen the interior space. Not correction (that would further contract the already-small soul), not exhortation (that assumes more interior resource than is present), but presence with comforting words.

### The Precision Paul Is Requiring
The 1 Thess 5:14 taxonomy (*noutheteō* for *ataktos*, *paramytheomai* for *oligopsychos*, *antechomai* for *asthenōn*) demonstrates that the body is called to *differentiated response* — reading the specific presentation of the "off" member and deploying the appropriate intervention. This requires *hexis* — a trained perceptive faculty that can distinguish the idle from the fainthearted, the weak from the disorderly, and modulate response accordingly. One-size pastoral care misses the diagnostic precision Paul is describing.

## 19. ἀντέχομαι (*antechomai*)

**Transliteration:** an-TEKH-om-eye
**Form in 1 Thess 5:14:** *antechesthe* (present middle imperative, 2nd pl.) — "hold onto, support, cling to"
**Greek:** anti (against, over against, facing) + echō (to have, to hold) → "to hold firmly toward/against something, to cling to, to support"

### BDAG
"To hold firmly to, cling to; to give full attention to, take an interest in, help." The middle voice form (*antechomai* rather than *antechō*) indicates personal involvement — "to hold on behalf of oneself" or "to take it upon oneself to hold firmly."

### The Physical Metaphor
*Antechomai* carries a physical image: grabbing hold of something (or someone) and steadying or supporting it against collapse. The arms-around, body-steadying image is operative. Applied to the *asthenos* (the weak) — the one without strength to stand — this is the action of physically coming alongside and *holding them up*.

### Key Occurrences
- **Matt 6:24 / Luke 16:13** — "no one can serve two masters... he will be devoted (*antechetai*) to the one and despise the other." *Antechomai* is the word for *devotion* — holding firmly to a master in allegiance. This is the strongest use of the word's relational attachment sense.
- **Tit 1:9** — the bishop must "hold firm (*antechomenon*) to the trustworthy word as taught." Clinging to the word as a resource, maintaining one's grip on it.
- **1 Thess 5:14** — "help/support (*antechesthe*) the weak (*asthenōn*)" — bodily support extended to those without strength

### The Target — *asthenōn*
*Asthenōn* — the weak. *Asthenes* in the NT has several domains:
- Physical weakness or illness (Mark 6:56; Acts 5:15)
- Moral/spiritual weakness — those weak in faith or conviction (Rom 14-15 — "welcome the one who is weak in faith")
- General lack of strength or capacity

In 1 Thess 5:14, the *asthenōn* is most naturally read as those without interior strength or resource — not the disorderly (who need *noutheteō*) and not the fainthearted (who need *paramytheomai*), but those who lack the capacity to stand or function on their own. The response is *antechomai* — active, sustained holding. Not a brief word but an extended posture of support.

### Contrast with *paramytheomai*
The *oligopsychos* (fainthearted) is small-souled but may be capable of hearing and receiving comforting words. The *asthenos* (weak) may be beyond the reach of words — they need to be *held*, supported structurally, their weight borne by another member until they can bear it again themselves. Gal 6:2 ("bear one another's burdens") is in the same register.

## 20. μακροθυμέω (*makrothymeō*)

**Transliteration:** mak-ro-thoo-MEH-oh
**Form in 1 Thess 5:14:** *makrothymeite* (present active imperative, 2nd pl.) — "be patient, be long-tempered"
**Greek:** makros (long, far-extending) + thymos (passion, spirit, temper, emotion) → "to have a long *thymos*, to be long-tempered"

### The *Thymos* Background
*Thymos* in classical Greek was the seat of passion and emotion — both positive (courage, zeal) and negative (anger, rage). A person with a short *thymos* (*oxythymos* — quick-tempered) flares and reacts immediately. A person with a long *thymos* (*makrothymos*) sustains composure under provocation over an extended period. The word implies *duration under pressure* — not absence of feeling but maintenance of composure despite feeling.

### BDAG
"To bear up under a difficult situation without losing patience or composure, be patient, wait." Specifically: patience in relation to *persons*, not circumstances (distinguished from *hypomonē*, which is often patience in relation to *circumstances* or *trials*).

### Key Occurrences
- **1 Cor 13:4** — "*agapē* is patient (*makrothymei*)" — the first attribute of love in Paul's hymn. *Makrothymia* is not a secondary virtue but the *first* description of what love looks like in practice.
- **Jas 5:7-8** — "Be patient (*makrothymēsate*) therefore, brothers, until the coming of the Lord. See how the farmer waits for the precious fruit of the earth, being patient (*makrothymōn*)." Long-tempered endurance through a slow process.
- **Jas 5:10-11** — "the prophets who spoke in the name of the Lord as an example of suffering and patience (*makrothymias*)." The prophets as models of sustained faithfulness under prolonged opposition.
- **Heb 6:15** — Abraham "having patiently waited (*makrothymēsas*) obtained the promise." The long duration before the fulfillment tests and demonstrates *makrothymia*.
- **2 Pet 3:9** — God himself is "patient (*makrothymei*) toward you, not wishing that any should perish." God's own *makrothymia* as the basis for the delay of judgment.
- **1 Thess 5:14** — "be patient (*makrothymeite*) with all (*panton*)"

### The Direction of *panton* — "With All"
In 1 Thess 5:14, after three targeted commands (admonish the *ataktos*, comfort the *oligopsychos*, support the *asthenos*), the fourth command is undifferentiated: *makrothymeite panton* — "be long-tempered with all." This appears to function as the governing disposition underlying all three targeted responses:
- The *ataktos* who resists correction requires *makrothymia* — repeated admonition without losing patience
- The *oligopsychos* who continues to contract despite consolation requires *makrothymia* — sustained comfort without frustration
- The *asthenos* whose weakness is prolonged requires *makrothymia* — extended holding without withdrawal

*Makrothymia* is the disposition that makes all three specific responses *sustainable* over time.

## 21. τέλος (*telos*)

**Transliteration:** TEH-los
**Greek:** from *teleō* (to complete, to finish, to accomplish) → *telos* (end, goal, purpose, completion)
**Note:** Unlike terms 1–20, *telos* is not used in *Assembled, Not Attending*. It is drawn from the sibling document *Formation & Transition — The Telos of a Formed Congregation*, added here at Andrew's request because the two documents are read together in the same Formation & Transition track.

### Etymology and Classical Background
In classical Greek philosophy, *telos* denotes the end *toward which* a thing moves — not mere termination (*pausis*, simple stopping) but the purpose or completion that gives a process its point. This is the root of Aristotle's teleology: every process has a *telos*, a "final cause" that explains *why* it moves as it does. An acorn's *telos* is the oak tree — the end state that makes sense of every stage along the way.

### BDAG
"The goal toward which a movement is being directed, end, close, conclusion... the outcome of a specific act or process... the goal/objective toward which an activity is directed." BDAG distinguishes this teleological sense (purposive end) from the purely temporal sense (mere cessation) — both occur in the NT, and context determines which is active.

### NT Semantic Range
- **Rom 10:4** — "Christ is the *telos* of the law for righteousness to everyone who believes." *Telos* here carries both senses at once (debated in Pauline scholarship): the law's termination *and* the law's goal, fulfilled in Christ.
- **1 Tim 1:5** — "the *telos* of our charge is love that issues from a pure heart." The stated purpose/goal of instruction — not merely where it stops, but what it is *for*.
- **1 Pet 1:9** — "obtaining the outcome (*telos*) of your faith, the salvation of your souls." The completion toward which faith moves.
- **Phil 3:19** — "their *telos* is destruction" — negative use: the end that a certain way of life is moving toward.
- **Matt 24:14** — "and then the *telos* will come" — eschatological/temporal use: the end of the age.

### Document Usage — The Corporate Telos (Eph 4:13)
*Formation & Transition — The Telos of a Formed Congregation* names the *telos* this track is built toward, citing Eph 4:11–16:

> "...to equip the saints for the work of ministry, for building up the body of Christ, until we all attain to the unity of the faith and of the knowledge of the Son of God, to mature manhood, to the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ... the whole body, joined and held together by every joint with which it is equipped, when each part is working properly, makes the body grow so that it builds itself up in love." (ESV)

The document is explicit that this *telos* is corporate, not merely the sum of individually formed members: "A formed congregation is not a collection of formed individuals. It is a body whose joined life displays what no individual formation alone can produce." The *telos* is recognized by what it produces under pressure — conflict, grief, the presence of a stranger — not by doctrinal knowledge or attendance.

### The Mechanism/Telos Distinction
The document draws a structural distinction that resolves an apparent circularity in the track's argument: *paideia* (mechanism) produces *hexis* — the individual formation that makes participation in genuine assembly possible, and this precedes assembly. *Ekklēsia* (telos) is both the destination of that formation *and* the site of a formation that private *paideia* cannot produce on its own. These operate in different registers — one names what must precede genuine assembly, the other names what genuine assembly itself produces. Ephesians 4:16 supplies the grammar for this: the body's functioning (*allēlōn* commands operating, *sympatheia* active) is not just the expression of a *telos* already achieved — it is the *progressive achievement* of that *telos*. The body builds itself up through the act of building itself up.

### Cross-Reference — Individual vs. Corporate Telos
A sibling document, *Covenant Identity — The Interior Life as Telos*, treats *telos* at the individual level (the eight qualities of a formed interior). *The Telos of a Formed Congregation* treats it at the corporate level (what a formed body displays that no individual formation alone can produce). The two are related as part to whole, not as competing claims.

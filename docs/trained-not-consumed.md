# Covenant Identity — Trained, Not Consumed
## The Biblical Case for Active Formation at the Implicit Level

---

This is not a survey of everything Scripture says about spiritual growth. It is an argument, laid out so a careful reader can test it. The claim: Scripture consistently describes spiritual maturity as the fruit of *training* — an active, ongoing, practiced reshaping of a person's deepest dispositions — not as the accumulation of content, experiences, or even correct beliefs. A great deal of what passes for spiritual growth in contemporary church culture is the latter. Covenant Identity coaching is built on the former.

Boxes marked **Language Note** throughout this document carry the original-language detail — Hebrew and Greek terms, transliteration, lexical background. They are not required to follow the argument, but they are where the argument's precision lives. Skip them on a first read if you want the case in outline; return to them if you want to see the joints.

---

## 1. Naming What This Is Against

Walk into most American churches and listen to the language people use for their own spiritual lives. They go to *get fed*. They look for a church that *meets their needs*. They consume a sermon, a podcast, a worship set, the way they consume any other content — and the measure of whether it "worked" is how it made them feel on the way out. None of this is necessarily wrong in itself. But notice the posture: receptive, evaluative, passive. Something is done *to* the person, or *for* them, or *at* them. Almost nothing is asked *of* them.

Dallas Willard named this pattern with unusual precision. In *The Great Omission*, he points out that the Great Commission does not end at "make disciples." The full charge is:

> *"Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you."* (Matthew 28:19–20, ESV)

> **Language Note — *didaskontes tērein* (Matthew 28:20)**
> The verb here is *didaskō* ("teach"), paired with *tērein* ("to keep, observe, guard"). This is not the *paideia* word group that anchors Section 4 below — it's worth being precise about that. But it is the same functional claim: the commission includes an ongoing, practiced *keeping* of what Christ commanded, not merely an initial transmission of information about him. Willard's argument is about this functional gap, not a specific Greek term.

Willard's diagnosis is that the church largely executes the first half — *getting people to a decision* — and treats the second half as optional, or as something that happens automatically afterward. He calls this the Great *Omission*: discipleship, understood as ongoing training in actually living the way Jesus lived, has been quietly dropped from what most churches think their job is. What remains is what he elsewhere calls "the gospel of sin management" (*The Divine Conspiracy*) — a transaction concerned with getting a person's standing with God settled, with little expectation that their actual character, their actual moment-to-moment functioning, will change.

In *The Spirit of the Disciplines*, Willard makes the distinction that gives this document its title: the difference between **trying** and **training**. Trying is gritting your teeth in the moment of temptation or crisis and attempting, by sheer will, to do the right thing. Training is arranging your life — over time, through practice — so that the right response is *already there* when the moment comes, because it has become who you are. Most consumer-Christian formation aims people at trying harder. It almost never asks them to train.

This document exists to show that the "training" side of that distinction is not a Willard innovation. It is the dominant biblical vocabulary for spiritual growth — older than the church, running through both Testaments, and converging on a specific claim about *where* training does its work: not primarily on behavior, but on the dispositions that produce behavior before a person has time to decide.

## 2. Old Covenant Foundations — Training as a Way, Not an Event

### Training transmits a path

Long before "training" appears as a topic, it appears as a practice — embedded in how God describes forming a covenant people. Of Abraham, God says:

> *"For I have chosen him, that he may command his children and his household after him to keep the way of the LORD by doing righteousness and justice, so that the LORD may bring to Abraham what he has promised him."* (Genesis 18:19, ESV)

The verb is "command," but the object is "*the way* of the LORD" (Hebrew *derek*) — a path, a manner of life, a trajectory. Abraham's household isn't given a doctrine to affirm. They're given a way to walk, and Abraham's job is to see that it gets walked — generation after generation.

### Proverbs 22:6 — the anchor verse, handled honestly

> *"Train up a child in the way he should go, and when he is old he will not depart from it."* (Proverbs 22:6, ESV)

> **Language Note — *ḥānak* and the "way" debate**
> The verb *ḥānak* (חָנַךְ) means "to dedicate, initiate, train" — related to the noun for "dedication" (as of a house or the temple, e.g., Deuteronomy 20:5, 1 Kings 8:63). The phrase "in the way he should go" translates *al-pi darko*, literally "according to the mouth/measure of his way." There are two live readings of this phrase in the commentary tradition: (1) train the child toward *the* way — God's prescribed path; or (2) train the child according to *his* way — his own bent, temperament, or nature, as a piece of wisdom-literature realism about individual differences rather than an unconditional promise of outcome.
>
> This matters because Proverbs is wisdom literature, not case law — its sayings are generally true observations, not guarantees. The popular reading treats this verse as a formula ("train them right and they'll never leave the faith"). The more defensible reading is narrower: *training*, rightly aimed and suited to the person, *shapes the trajectory* a life tends to follow. Notice that **both readings assume training is active, ongoing, and formative** — they only disagree about whether the target is a universal path or a personalized one. Either way, the verse does not describe a single decision or a single conversation. *Ḥānak* describes a process with a beginning, sustained over time, aimed at a settled outcome ("when he is old").

### Training is saturative, not occasional

The Shema makes the *how* explicit:

> *"And these words that I command you today shall be on your heart. You shall teach them diligently to your children, and shall talk of them when you sit in your house, and when you walk by the way, and when you lie down, and when you rise."* (Deuteronomy 6:6–7, ESV)

This is not a curriculum with a start and end date. It's a description of formation woven into the ordinary rhythms of a day — sitting, walking, lying down, rising. The contrast with consumer Christianity is almost too on-the-nose: this is the opposite of "the thing that happens on Sunday morning." Training, in this picture, *is* the texture of daily life, or it isn't training.

### Training's purpose is hope and obedience, not information

> *"He established a testimony in Jacob and appointed a law in Israel, which he commanded our fathers to teach to their children, that the next generation might know them, the children yet unborn, and arise and tell them to their children, so that they should set their hope in God and not forget the works of God, but keep his commandments."* (Psalm 78:5–7, ESV)

The stated goal of this multi-generational transmission isn't "so they would know the facts." It's "so that they should set their hope in God... and keep his commandments." Knowledge is the vehicle. Hope and obedience — dispositional outcomes — are the destination.

> **Language Note — *musar*, the LXX bridge to *paideia***
> Hebrew wisdom literature has its own word for this formative process: *musar* (מוּסָר), usually translated "discipline" or "instruction" — e.g., Proverbs 1:2, "to know wisdom and *instruction* (*musar*)," and Proverbs 1:7, "fools despise wisdom and *instruction* (*musar*)." When the Hebrew Bible was translated into Greek (the Septuagint, LXX), *musar* was regularly rendered by the Greek word ***paideia*** — the exact term that dominates the New Testament's training vocabulary, taken up in Section 3. This is not a thematic parallel Andrew is drawing; it's a translation choice made by Greek-speaking Jews centuries before the New Testament was written. The OT's *musar* and the NT's *paideia* are, lexically, the same word-field.

### The covenant bridge

> **Language Note — applying OT formation texts under the New Covenant**
> Deuteronomy 6 and Psalm 78 are addressed to the corporate, national people of Israel under the Mosaic covenant — not directly to individual New Covenant believers. Carrying their *pattern* (saturative, multi-generational, hope-and-obedience-oriented formation) into application for a New Covenant individual or coaching relationship requires the same hermeneutical bridge used throughout this model: progressive covenantism (Gentry & Wellum) — the pattern of how God forms a covenant people continues across covenants, even where the covenant's specific administration (national Israel vs. the church) does not. See *covenant_framework_definition* for the fuller statement of this bridge. This document uses these OT texts for *pattern*, not for direct command-transfer.

## 3. New Covenant Development — *Paideia* and the Vocabulary of Training

The Greek word group *paideia* (noun) / *paideuō* (verb) is broader than the English "discipline" usually suggests. Rooted in *pais* ("child"), it spans the whole formative process — education, upbringing, correction, and consequence — in a way that closer parallels "upbringing" or "formation" than "punishment," though it can include punishment as one tool within that larger process.

### Training "of the Lord" — source and content both

> *"Fathers, do not provoke your children to anger, but bring them up in the discipline and instruction of the Lord."* (Ephesians 6:4, ESV)

> **Language Note — *paideia kai nouthesia kyriou***
> "Discipline and instruction" translates *paideia kai nouthesia*. *Nouthesia* is verbal admonition — appeal to the mind and will, closer to "teaching" in the cognitive sense. *Paideia* is the broader formative process that includes it. Both are *kyriou* — "of the Lord" — a genitive that can mean training that *comes from* the Lord, or training *in the character/ways of* the Lord, or (most likely) both at once. This is the closest the NT comes to directly glossing "training up... in the ways of the Lord."

### Scripture's own fourfold curriculum

> *"All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness, that the man of God may be complete, equipped for every good work."* (2 Timothy 3:16–17, ESV)

Notice the sequence: **teaching** (positive instruction) → **reproof** (naming what's off) → **correction** (re-orienting) → **training** (*paideia* — the ongoing practice that turns a correction into a new pattern). The first three can happen in a conversation. The fourth cannot — by definition, training takes time and repetition.

### The governing verse: grace is the trainer

Everything in this document has to be read through one verse, or the whole case curdles into legalism:

> *"For the grace of God has appeared, bringing salvation for all people, **training us** to renounce ungodliness and worldly passions, and to live self-controlled, upright, and godly lives in the present age..."* (Titus 2:11–14, ESV, excerpted)

> **Language Note — *paideuousa* (Titus 2:12)**
> The participle *paideuousa* — "training" — has *grace* (*hē charis*) as its subject. Grace is not the reward for successful training; grace is what does the training. And note the object of that training: not behaviors first, but *passions* (*epithymias*, v.12) — the desire-level, the thing a person wants before they decide anything. This is the NT's clearest statement that the active, practiced formation this document describes is itself a work of grace operating beneath the level of conscious decision — not a human achievement that earns standing with God. Section 4 will use modern vocabulary ("implicit") for this same sub-decisional level; Titus 2:12 already locates training there, in the passions, two thousand years earlier.

### The telos of training is becoming like the teacher

> *"A disciple is not above his teacher, but everyone when he is fully trained will be like his teacher."* (Luke 6:40, ESV)

Training isn't aimed at acquiring abstract virtues. It's aimed at a person — conformity to Christ. This is what keeps "training" from collapsing into self-improvement: the shape being trained *toward* is given, not invented.

### Training requires direction, not just effort

> *"So I do not run aimlessly; I do not box as one beating the air. But I discipline my body and keep it under control..."* (1 Corinthians 9:24–27, ESV, excerpted)

Paul's athletic imagery (*gymnazō* is the root underlying "discipline" here, and reappears as the key term in Section 4) makes a point easy to miss: training without a clear telos is just motion. "Not aimlessly" — training presupposes a target, which is why Section 5 connects this to the *Interior Life as Telos* document. Without a picture of where training is headed, "train more" is not actionable counsel.

> **Language Note — the wider *paideia*/*gymnazō* inventory**
> For completeness at the teacher level: the *paideia* word group also covers corrective/punitive uses (Luke 23:16,22; 1 Corinthians 11:32; 2 Corinthians 6:9; 1 Timothy 1:20; 2 Timothy 2:25; Revelation 3:19) and general-education uses (Acts 7:22; Acts 22:3; Romans 2:20). 1 Timothy 4:7–8 ("train yourself for godliness... bodily training is of some value, godliness is of value in every way") is a brief but direct one-line reinforcement of the athletic-training frame from 1 Corinthians 9. None of these change the picture above; they round it out.

## 4. The Implicit Level — Where Training Lands

This is the section that distinguishes this document's claim from a generic "be more disciplined" exhortation. The biblical vocabulary for training doesn't just describe a *process* (practice over time) — in one place, it describes the *kind of result* that process produces, and that result is a disposition that operates **before** conscious deliberation.

### Trained perception, not trained willpower

> *"...for everyone who lives on milk is unskilled in the word of righteousness, since he is a child. But solid food is for the mature, for those who **have their powers of discernment trained by constant practice** to distinguish good from evil."* (Hebrews 5:13–14, ESV)

> **Language Note — *hexis*, *aisthētēria*, *gegymnasmena***
> Three words carry this verse, and the order matters:
>
> - ***aisthētēria*** — "powers of discernment," literally the perceptive faculties, the senses. This is about *perception*, not reasoning.
> - ***hexis*** — translated "constant practice," but this is the technical term from Greek ethical philosophy (especially Aristotle) for a *settled disposition formed by repeated action* — what Aristotle calls "second nature." His argument in the *Nicomachean Ethics* is that a person with trained *hexis* doesn't reason their way to right perception each time; the disposition itself perceives rightly, the way a skilled craftsman's hands "know" the work without conscious calculation.
> - ***gegymnasmena*** — "trained," from *gymnazō* (the athletic-training verb already seen in 1 Corinthians 9). Perfect passive participle — a completed process with an ongoing result.
>
> **Here is the bridge this document is making, stated plainly so it can be tested:** *hexis* — "a disposition formed by repeated practice that operates without deliberation" — is functionally what contemporary psychology calls **implicit**: automatic, pre-conscious, operating beneath the level of decision. Hebrews 5:14 does not use the word "implicit"; no first-century text could. But the *thing it describes* — a perceptual faculty so practiced it no longer requires reasoning — is the same reality. This is an interpretive bridge from an ancient ethical category to a modern psychological one, not a claim about first-century vocabulary. It is, however, a *well-supported* bridge: it's the closest available ancient category to the modern one, and — notably — **it is not a new move for this model.** *Covenant Identity — Functional vs. Confessional Theology — 20 Diagnostic Contrasts* already uses "implicit" (via Thompson's explicit/implicit memory systems) as core CI vocabulary. This document supplies the biblical-exegetical grounding underneath vocabulary the model already uses.

### Discipline produces trained people, who bear fruit

> *"For the moment all discipline seems painful rather than pleasant, but later it yields the peaceful fruit of righteousness to those who have been **trained** by it."* (Hebrews 12:11, ESV)

This verse fuses the two words from Sections 3 and 4 in one sentence: *paideia* (discipline — the process, v.11a) produces *gegymnasmenoi* (trained ones — the result, v.11b), who bear *karpos eirēnikos dikaiosynēs* — "the peaceful fruit of righteousness." Note the word *fruit*. Fruit is not manufactured by effort in the moment; it grows out of what a plant *has become*. That's the difference between trying and training in one image.

The wider context (Hebrews 12:5–10) frames this *paideia* as something that often comes through hardship — "no discipline seems pleasant at the time" (v.11a) — and explicitly as the action of a Father who loves his children (v.6–7) "for our good, that we may share his holiness" (v.10). This is Titus 2:11's grace-as-trainer, from a different angle: the same Father who saves is the one forming the *hexis*.

### The corporate stakes — this doesn't stay private

> *"Strive for peace with everyone, and for the holiness without which no one will see the Lord. See to it that no one fails to obtain the grace of God; that no root of bitterness springing up causes trouble, and by it many become defiled."* (Hebrews 12:14–15, ESV)

This is the verse that answers "why does my inner formation matter to anyone but me?" The author moves, in the space of one sentence, from *personal* training (v.11) to its *corporate* consequence (v.14–15). An untrained inner pattern — "a root of bitterness" — doesn't stay contained. It "springs up" and "defiles many." The implicit-level work this document argues for isn't a private project for personally ambitious Christians. Left undone, it has a cost the whole body pays. Done, it's part of what "strive for peace with everyone" actually requires.

### Self-examination as the corporate check

> *"Let a person examine himself, then, and so eat of the bread and drink of the cup. For anyone who eats and drinks without discerning the body eats and drinks judgment on himself... But if we judged ourselves truly, we would not be judged. But when we are judged by the Lord, we are disciplined so that we may not be condemned along with the world."* (1 Corinthians 11:28–32, ESV, excerpted)

Paul ties self-examination directly to "discerning the body" — a corporate act, not a private devotional exercise. And when self-examination fails, *paideia* (v.32, "we are disciplined") is the Lord's corrective fallback — explicitly framed as protective, not punitive in the destructive sense. The pattern from Hebrews 12:14–15 repeats here from a different angle: the implicit/dispositional layer and the health of the gathered community are not two separate concerns.

## 5. Connection to Covenant Identity Coaching

Put the pieces together, and the case is this: **Scripture's vocabulary for spiritual growth describes an active, ongoing, practiced process (*paideia*/*ḥānak*/*musar*) whose target is a person's *hexis* — a trained disposition operating beneath conscious deliberation (what this document, following the model's existing vocabulary, calls the *implicit* level) — produced by grace (Titus 2:11), often through difficulty (Hebrews 12:5–11), aimed at conformity to Christ (Luke 6:40), and consequential for the whole body, not just the individual (Hebrews 12:14–15).**

This is, point for point, a description of what Covenant Identity coaching does and why it's structured the way it is:

- **Between-session practice is not homework — it's where the training happens.** *Covenant Identity — Spiritual Disciplines Guide* already frames its disciplines through Willard's training-vs-trying distinction; this document supplies that distinction's biblical floor. A session is where false identity gets *named* (the diagnostic work); the disciplines between sessions are where *hexis* actually re-forms — because *hexis* is formed by *gymnazō*, practice, not by insight alone.
- **The telos is the fruit, not the entry condition.** *Covenant Identity — The Interior Life as Telos* describes eight qualities — Settled, Undivided, Free, and so on — explicitly as fruit, not goals to perform. That framing is exactly Hebrews 12:11's "peaceful fruit of righteousness... to those who have been trained by it." The Interior Life document describes the fruit; this document describes the tree-growing process that produces it.
- **Resistance and slowness are expected, not failures.** *Covenant Identity — Formation Mechanism Guide* already names why formation is slow (2 Cor 3:18) and why resistance is expected per wound type. Hebrews 12:11's "painful rather than pleasant... for the moment" is the same expectation stated biblically — *paideia* that doesn't feel like training while it's happening is not evidence it isn't working.
- **"Implicit" is not new vocabulary — this document grounds it.** The model already distinguishes explicit from implicit (Thompson, via *Covenant Identity — Functional vs. Confessional Theology — 20 Diagnostic Contrasts*). Hebrews 5:14's *hexis* gives that distinction a biblical-exegetical floor: Scripture itself names a category of trained, pre-deliberative disposition as the *target* of spiritual maturity, centuries before the neuroscience had a name for it.

**Where this document fits in practice:** this is framing/rationale material — most useful early (Phase 1), when a client needs to understand *why* the coaching will ask things of them between sessions, rather than functioning like the "go to get fed" model they may be used to. It does not replace *Covenant Identity — Formation Mechanism Guide* (which covers the *experience* of formation) — it makes the *biblical case* underneath both that document and the Spiritual Disciplines Guide.

## 6. The Map, Not the Territory

One more thing has to be said, and it's the thing this entire document has been pointing toward without quite being able to do itself.

You have just read an argument that spiritual growth is *training* — active, practiced, ongoing — and not the consumption of content. And what you did to engage with that argument was... consume content. Read it, think about it, maybe agree with it. That's not a flaw in the argument. It's the argument's own blind spot, and it's worth naming directly rather than pretending it isn't there.

Hebrews 5:14's *hexis* is not formed by reading about *hexis*. It's formed by *gymnazō* — repeated practice, over time, often through things that don't feel like progress while they're happening (Hebrews 12:11). This document is the map. It can tell you that the territory exists, where it is, and why it matters that you go there. It cannot walk you through it.

The territory is the actual disciplines — the between-session practices, the rule of life, the slow re-formation of what you reach for without thinking. That's where *training*, in the biblical sense this document has tried to establish, actually happens. Everything here is groundwork for going there with eyes open — not a substitute for going.

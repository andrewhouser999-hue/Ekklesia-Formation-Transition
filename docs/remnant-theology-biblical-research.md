# Formation & Transition — Remnant Theology — Biblical Research

## Purpose

This document provides the exegetical foundation for the working assumption stated in the Development Plan, First Things §5: *God consistently works with a formed minority before expanding through a larger body.* That claim is the structural logic beneath the entire development track — the reason the track is organized around cohort formation rather than mass programming. It cannot bear that weight as an assertion. This document either establishes it or requires §5 to be restated.

The central research question: **Is the remnant pattern normative — a theological account of how God consistently advances his purposes — or merely descriptive — a pattern observed in specific historical circumstances that cannot be transferred as operational logic?**

*Complete when:* the pattern is established exegetically across the key canonical texts with a specific finding about normativity sufficient to confirm or revise First Things §5.

---

## Part 1 — What "Remnant" Means and What It Doesn't

The Hebrew *shʾērit* (remnant, what is left) and related terms (*pelēṭāh* — escaped remnant; *yether* — remainder; *shear* — what remains) cover a semantic range wider than formation strategy. The broadest use is simply: *those who survive.* Lot escaping Sodom is a remnant. Survivors of a plague are a remnant. Israel returning from exile is a remnant.

This breadth requires a distinction before any normative claim can be made:

**Survival remnant** — a subset of a population that survives destruction or judgment while the larger group does not. The qualitative characteristic is survival itself, not interior formation. Isaiah 37:31-32 (the surviving remnant of Judah shall take root downward and bear fruit upward) begins this way — they are those who escaped the Assyrian siege.

**Qualitative remnant** — a subset identified not by survival but by interior disposition, covenant faithfulness, or formation that the larger group does not share. Isaiah's *shear-yashub* (the name means "a remnant shall return") functions this way — the remnant is not merely whoever survives but those oriented toward return. Zephaniah's humble and lowly remnant (Zeph 3:12-13) is qualitative in the most explicit sense: the interior disposition (humility, lowliness) is the identifying characteristic.

**The normative claim requires the qualitative category.** A pattern of God working with a survival remnant is simply: people died and some did not. That carries no transferable logic for development strategy. A pattern of God working with a *qualitative* remnant — a formed minority whose interior disposition is the distinguishing characteristic — is a theological claim about God's operating method that the development track can legitimately draw on.

The research question for each text: *Is the remnant distinguished by survival or by quality of formation and orientation?*

## Part 2 — Old Testament Canonical Pattern

### Isaiah — The Holy Seed

Isaiah's remnant theology is the most developed in the canon.

**Isaiah 6:13** — The stump after the great burning is described as the *holy seed* (*zera' qodesh*). Two features: the characteristic is *holiness* (a qualitative term, not a survival term), and the seed language implies generative capacity — the remnant is not the end of the story but the source of what follows. This is the earliest OT text that combines the remnant idea with a qualitative-formational marker and future generative function.

**Isaiah 7:3** — God tells Isaiah to bring his son *Shear-jashub* (a remnant shall return) to meet Ahaz. The son's name embeds the promise in the prophetic event: whether threatening (only a remnant) or hopeful (at least a remnant shall return) is contextually debated, but the directional quality is clear — return, orientation back toward God, is the defining characteristic of the remnant.

**Isaiah 10:20-22** — "In that day the remnant of Israel and the survivors of the house of Jacob will no more lean on him who struck them, but will lean on the LORD, the Holy One of Israel, in truth. A remnant will return, the remnant of Jacob, to the mighty God. For though your people Israel be as the sand of the sea, only a remnant of them will return. Destruction is decreed, overflowing with righteousness." The critical phrase: *lean on the LORD... in truth.* The remnant is defined by what they orient toward and the quality of that orientation. The contrast with the larger group is explicit: the many will not return; the remnant will.

**Isaiah 11:11-16** — God will recover the remnant a second time from the nations. The point here is agency: God *recovers* the remnant. This is not the remnant asserting itself; it is God selecting and gathering. The remnant is preserved by divine action, not self-selection.

**Isaiah 37:31-32** — "The surviving remnant of the house of Judah shall again take root downward and bear fruit upward. For out of Jerusalem shall go a remnant, and out of Mount Zion a band of survivors." Survival remnant language here — but the agricultural image (root downward, fruit upward) points toward the generative function: the remnant is not just those who survived but the root from which future fruitfulness emerges. The remnant is the seed, not merely the residue.

**Pattern in Isaiah:** The remnant is consistently:
1. A minority of the larger population
2. Distinguished by orientation and interior disposition, not merely by survival
3. Preserved by divine initiative, not self-organization
4. Generatively functional — the source of what follows, not the end of the line

### Elijah's Seven Thousand

**1 Kings 19:18** — "Yet I will leave seven thousand in Israel, all the knees that have not bowed to Baal, and every mouth that has not kissed him."

This text is theologically precise on all three elements:

*God is the active agent:* "I will leave" — the remnant's preservation is a divine act, not a human achievement. Elijah did not know they existed. The remnant was hidden, preserved by God without a spokesperson or institution.

*The qualitative marker is interior and dispositional:* Knees that have not bowed. Mouths that have not kissed. The distinction is not organizational (those who attended the right institution) or intellectual (those who held correct doctrine) but dispositional — what they have done with their bodies in the presence of the dominant counterfeit. This is implicit-level formation language: the formation shows up in embodied, habitual response, not in declared position.

*The number is specific and limited:* 7,000 in a nation of potentially millions — God's work advances from a tiny qualitative nucleus.

### Zephaniah's Humble and Lowly Remnant

**Zephaniah 3:12-13** — "But I will leave in your midst a people humble and lowly. They shall seek refuge in the name of the LORD, those who are left in Israel; they shall do no injustice and speak no lies, nor shall there be found in their mouth a deceitful tongue."

This is the most formation-explicit remnant text in the OT. The qualitative markers: *humble* (*'anî*) and *lowly* (*dal*) — interior disposition terms. Their behavioral fruit: no injustice, no lies, no deceitful tongue — not as commanded performance but as the organic expression of their interior. The remnant is not instructed to be humble; they *are* humble. The formation is assumed as complete; the fruit is the evidence.

The connection to the CIC model is direct: *hexis* as formed interior disposition producing consistent behavioral output without willpower management.

### Summary of OT Pattern

Across Isaiah, the Elijah narrative, Zephaniah, and supporting texts (Mic 5:7-8; Hag 1:12-14; Zech 8:11-12):

- The remnant is consistently a minority distinguished by interior disposition and covenant orientation, not organizational membership or survival luck
- God preserves and gathers the remnant by divine initiative
- The remnant is generative — the seed from which what follows emerges
- The remnant's formation is prior to their function; they are not formed *by* the events that follow their identification but *already formed* when God gathers them

## Part 3 — New Testament: Continuation and Application

### Paul's Explicit Theological Use — Romans 9 and 11

Paul does not treat the OT remnant pattern as historical background. He applies it as a live theological argument about the present.

**Romans 9:27-29** — "And Isaiah cries out concerning Israel: 'Though the number of the sons of Israel be as the sand of the sea, only a remnant of them will be saved.'" Paul is quoting Isaiah 10:22 and applying it to his argument about Israel and the Gentiles in the present era. The size of the nominal community (sand of the sea) does not determine God's operative method — the remnant does.

**Romans 11:1-5** — The decisive text: "I ask, then, has God rejected his people? By no means! For I myself am an Israelite... Do you not know what the Scripture says of Elijah, how he appeals to God against Israel? 'Lord, they have killed your prophets, they have demolished your altars, and I alone am left, and they seek my life.' But what is God's reply to him? 'I have kept for myself seven thousand men who have not bowed the knee to Baal.' So too at the present time there is a remnant, chosen by grace."

Paul's argumentative move here is critical for the normative/descriptive question: he does not say *the Elijah situation was similar to now.* He says *the same pattern is operative now.* "So too at the present time" (*houtōs oun kai en tō nyn kairō*) — the Elijah pattern is the active mechanism of God's faithfulness in Paul's present. The remnant is not a historical observation; it is a theological account of how God works that Paul applies to his own moment.

This is the strongest canonical evidence that the pattern is normative. Paul treats the remnant pattern as a description of God's character and consistent method, not as a historical curiosity.

### The Twelve as Formed Nucleus Within Israel

**Luke 6:12-16** — Jesus spends the night in prayer before selecting the twelve. The selection is deliberate, preceded by extended seeking, and from a larger group of disciples. The twelve are not drawn from the general population; they are drawn from the already-oriented.

**Three years of formation before Pentecost.** The entire Synoptic discipleship narrative is a formation arc: progressive exposure, testing, failure, correction, progressive revelation. The twelve are being *formed* through the events of the gospels — not simply instructed. The implicit-level markers are consistent with *paideia/hexis* formation: the disciples fail regularly (Mark is particularly unsparing), are corrected relationally, and are gradually shaped into something they were not at the beginning.

**John 17 — The High Priestly Prayer over the Formed Nucleus.** "I have given them your word, and the world has hated them because they are not of the world... Sanctify them in the truth; your word is truth." (vv.14, 17) Jesus prays over the twelve as a qualitatively distinct group — given the word, hated by the world, being sanctified. This is the remnant pattern: a formed minority, distinguished from the larger population by what they have received and what that reception has done to their interior orientation.

**Acts 1:15 — The 120.** The nucleus of the post-resurrection community that waits for Pentecost is 120 formed believers. They are not randomly gathered; they are *proskarterountes* (habitual, sustained orientation toward prayer) in Acts 1:14. Pentecost does not produce this community; it lands on this community and extends from it.

### Acts 2 — The Remnant as Generative Nucleus

The 3,000 who respond to Peter's Pentecost sermon join a community that is already formed. The sequence is: formed nucleus → Spirit's outpouring → public expansion through the demonstration of genuine *ekklēsia*. This maps exactly the pattern First Things §5 claims: "God consistently works with a formed minority before expanding through a larger body."

Acts 2:42's description of the community (*proskarterountes tē didachē*... *koinōnia*... *klasis tou artou*... *proseuchas*) is not a description of what the 3,000 were converted into immediately; it is a description of the community they joined — practices already established in the 120. The formation preceded the expansion.

### Paul's City Strategy

Acts 13-20 documents a consistent pattern across multiple city entries:

- **Antioch Pisidia (Acts 13:14-48):** Paul begins in the synagogue with those already oriented toward the God of Israel. Resistance from some; receptive remnant (v.48: "as many as were appointed to eternal life believed") forms the nucleus.
- **Thessalonica (Acts 17:1-9):** Paul enters the synagogue; some Jews, a great many devout Greeks, and leading women join — the already-oriented, not a random cross-section of the city.
- **Corinth (Acts 18:5-8):** When the synagogue resists, Paul relocates to the house next door. Crispus, the synagogue ruler, believes. Paul's response to God's vision in v.10 ("I have many in this city who are people of mine") suggests the nucleus God is gathering precedes Paul's identification of them — the pattern from Elijah's 7,000 applies: God has kept them.
- **Ephesus (Acts 19:1-7, 8-10):** Paul begins with twelve disciples who received John's baptism — already oriented, already partially formed. He forms them further (v.9: "taking the disciples with him and reasoning daily in the hall of Tyrannus") before the larger expansion begins.

The pattern across these cases: Paul identifies the already-oriented, forms them into a genuine nucleus, and the nucleus constitutes the *ekklēsia* from which expansion proceeds. He does not attempt mass programming and then form whoever emerges. Formation precedes functional *ekklēsia*; functional *ekklēsia* precedes expansion.

### Revelation's Remnant

**Revelation 3:4** — "You have still a few names in Sardis, people who have not soiled their garments, and they will walk with me in white, for they are worthy." In a church described as dead (v.1), a formed few remain. God's work in Sardis is not abandoned because the majority are dead; it continues in the remnant. The qualitative marker — unsoiled garments — is the Zephaniah pattern (no injustice, no deceitful tongue) applied in John's apocalyptic frame.

The eschatological remnant texts (Rev 7, 14 — the 144,000 as sealed, qualified nucleus within the larger population) extend the pattern into the final chapter: God's culminating work is still organized around a distinct, formed, identifiable community whose character distinguishes them.

## Part 4 — Normative vs. Descriptive: The Finding

**The case for merely descriptive:**

- The OT remnant is partially a national-survival category (those who survive exile or judgment) — not all remnant texts are formation texts
- The twelve are a historically unique case: apostolic selection cannot be generalized directly to formation strategy
- There is no explicit NT prescription to "work with a remnant first" — the pattern is observed in narrative but not commanded
- Paul's apostolic strategy reflects his specific calling and mobility; it cannot be read as a universal methodology

**The case for normative:**

- The pattern is consistent across *all three eras of redemptive history*: Mosaic period (Elijah's 7,000 within Ahab's Israel), prophetic era (Isaiah's *shear-yashub*, Zephaniah's humble remnant), NT founding (the Twelve, Acts 2 community, Paul's city strategy), and eschatological (Revelation 3:4, 7:4-8)
- The qualitative marker is present across multiple texts in different eras — not merely survival but interior disposition and covenant faithfulness that the larger group does not share
- Paul's explicit theological application in Romans 11:1-5 is not historical description — "so too *at the present time*" applies the Elijah pattern to his moment as the active mechanism of God's faithfulness; this is normative theological reasoning, not historical analogy
- The consistency reflects something about God's character and method: he does not advance his purposes through the impressive, the numerous, or the institutionally powerful — he works from the formed and available; this is a *theological* claim about God, not merely an observation about history

**Finding:**

The remnant pattern is **normative as theological principle**, though **not prescriptive as method.** Paul's use of the Elijah pattern in Romans 11 establishes that it is not merely historical — God works this way *now*, in the present. The qualitative consistency across texts — the remnant is always distinguished by interior disposition and covenant faithfulness, not organizational membership or numerical strength — reflects a stable theological reality about how God operates: from the formed toward the many, not from the many in hopes that some will form.

This is not a prescription that every ministry strategy must begin with a small group. It is a theological account of what has consistently happened when genuine formation has occurred: the formed function as a generative nucleus, and the demonstration of what is produced in them constitutes the invitation and proof for those who carry latent hunger for the same.

## Part 5 — Implication for First Things §5

The working assumption in First Things §5 is **confirmed with qualification.**

**Confirmed:** The remnant strategy is not Andrew's invention or a misapplication of OT ethnic categories to a Protestant ecclesiology. The pattern is canonical, theologically normed by Paul's explicit application, and qualitatively consistent: God works from a formed minority whose demonstration of genuine *koinōnia* and *sympatheia* constitutes the invitation to those who cannot yet name what they are hungry for. The development track's cohort-first model stands on theological ground.

**Qualification required:** First Things §5 should not claim that the development track's formation cohort *is* the OT/NT remnant. The claim is more precise: **the same theological logic applies.** The remnant pattern reflects God's operating method — not because the model mandates small-group first, but because formation cannot be compressed, the Spirit works in available people before he works through them, and the demonstration of genuine *ekklēsia* is always more persuasive than the argument for it. These are not structural prescriptions; they are theological descriptions of how the Spirit works in human communities.

**§5 revision recommended:** The current §5 language — "God consistently works with a formed minority before expanding through a larger body" — is accurate. The revision is to add that this is a *theological account of God's method* (not a strategic prescription), and that the exegetical basis now exists to confirm it rather than assert it. The practical implication remains: the development track's cohort-first model reflects the biblical pattern, not merely pragmatic preference.

# Formation & Transition — Practitioner Curriculum Source Library

## Purpose

This file catalogs the non-scriptural sources that inform practitioner training for the Formation & Transition development track. It is a curriculum-in-preparation reference — organizing sources by the essential capability they build in a practitioner, not by the development-plan gaps they were originally identified to fill and not as a research bibliography.

Used when building practitioner training modules, assessing what a practitioner must have engaged before facilitating a formation cohort, or determining which domains to prioritize in curriculum sequence.

**Status notation used throughout:**
- ★ New to the track (not yet in any track document)
- ◆ Cross-track (in Andrew's broader evidence base or another track document; listed here for curriculum completeness and placement)
- △ Partially in track (referenced in a track document but not yet fully engaged for this purpose)

---

## Domain 1 — The Interior Mechanism of Change

*What actually happens inside a person when genuine change occurs — not behavior modification but transformation of implicit-level dispositions. A practitioner who has not worked in this domain will mistake information transfer for formation, misread resistance as obstinacy, and lose patience with a process that requires time for structural reasons they don't understand.*

---

**★ William Bridges, *Transitions: Making Sense of Life's Changes* (3rd ed., Da Capo Press, 2004)**

Essential design contribution: Bridges maps the interior phenomenology of genuine transition in three phases — Ending (the actual loss that must be grieved, not merely named), Neutral Zone (the disorientation between old identity and new; the phase where most people reverse course), and New Beginning. The Neutral Zone is the phase the Development Plan's First Things §6 describes theologically as *paideia* but does not map experientially. Without Bridges, practitioners will misread normal transition suffering as failure — and so will the people they are accompanying.

Practitioner capability built: Recognizing which phase of transition a person or congregation is actually in; holding steady through the Neutral Zone without rescuing people back into the consumer pattern they left; reframing deterioration before improvement as evidence of genuine movement rather than wrong direction.

---

**★ Jack Mezirow, Transformative Learning Theory**
Key texts: "Transformative Learning: Theory to Practice," *New Directions for Adult and Continuing Education* 74 (1997); *Learning as Transformation* (Jossey-Bass, 2000)

Essential design contribution: Adults with stable meaning-making frameworks — which describes most church attenders — change through *disorienting dilemmas*: experiences their existing framework cannot accommodate and therefore cannot assimilate without changing. This is the mechanism by which pre-contemplation cracks open. It also explains why information alone does not produce formation: information gets assimilated into the existing framework without requiring the framework to change. The practitioner needs to understand what a disorienting dilemma is, what conditions make it possible, and why engineering one is not the same as creating conditions for it.

Practitioner capability built: Distinguishing information delivery from formation facilitation; recognizing and holding disorienting dilemmas as formation events rather than pastoral crises to resolve; resisting the impulse to explain away disorientation before it has done its work.

---

**★ Robert Kegan & Lisa Laskow Lahey, *Immunity to Change* (Harvard Business Review Press, 2009)**

Essential design contribution: The immunity map surfaces the hidden competing commitments and big assumptions that prevent people from making changes they sincerely intend — what the track calls defended interiority, mapped with structural precision. The four-column format (improvement goal → doing/not doing → hidden competing commitment → big assumption) makes the invisible structure of resistance visible without treating it as moral failure. Highly applicable to pastoral leaders whose stated commitment to formation coexists with behaviors that preserve the consumer model.

Practitioner capability built: Diagnosing why a leader or congregant who genuinely believes the formation framework still does not change; mapping the structure of resistance without defaulting to moral or spiritual interpretation; holding the diagnostic without the corrective posture.

Note: Already in Andrew's RPA evidence base (Kegan/Lahey). Needs to be applied to the formation context; the immunity-to-change dynamic is at least as present in pastoral leaders as in business executives.

---

**◆ James K.A. Smith, *You Are What You Love: The Spiritual Power of Habit* (Brazos Press, 2016)**

Essential design contribution: Liturgy as formation of desire — repeated embodied practices form the *telos* we actually pursue, operating beneath the level of what we consciously believe or intend. Consumer culture's liturgies (shopping, scrolling, entertainment) form desire continuously at a frequency and depth that vastly exceeds congregational contact. The practitioner must understand this to design between-session practices that counter-form at the same level rather than competing with consumer formation only at the cognitive level.

Practitioner capability built: Designing formation practices that operate on embodied desire, not cognitive assent; evaluating whether a proposed between-session practice reaches the level where consumer formation operates or only addresses it cognitively.

Note: Already in Constraint Matrix basis. Core to Domain 1; listed here for curriculum completeness and placement.

---

**◆ James Prochaska & Carlo DiClemente, Transtheoretical Model / Stages of Change**
Key text: *Changing for Good* (HarperCollins, 1994) or Prochaska, Norcross & DiClemente, *Handbook of Psychotherapy Integration*

Essential design contribution: Pre-contemplation → Contemplation → Preparation → Action → Maintenance maps the spectrum of individual readiness for change. Most consumer attenders are pre-contemplative about formation — they do not know they do not know, and they are not experiencing a deficit. The invitation architecture cannot be designed without a theory of where people are in this spectrum and what actually moves them from one stage to the next; the same approach applied to a pre-contemplative and an action-stage person will fail one of them.

Practitioner capability built: Reading where an individual is on the formation-readiness spectrum before making an invitation; calibrating accompaniment to stage rather than applying a uniform approach; not confusing contemplation for readiness to act.

Note: Already in Andrew's RPA evidence base. Needs direct application to the formation context here.

## Domain 2 — How Groups and Institutions Change

*Scaling, diffusion, and organizational culture dynamics that make congregational change categorically different from individual formation. A practitioner without this domain will assume that formed individuals automatically produce a transformed congregation, and will be unprepared for the institutional resistance that has nothing to do with theology and everything to do with how organizations actually work.*

---

**★ Everett Rogers, *Diffusion of Innovations* (5th ed., Free Press, 2003)**

Essential design contribution: How new practices spread through social systems — innovators → early adopters → early majority → late majority → laggards. The remnant-to-congregation scaling question is a diffusion problem. Rogers' core finding: adoption spreads through *observed behavior within trusted social networks*, not through persuasion, instruction, or program. This is the sociological validation of the Development Plan's historical principle that demonstration is more powerful than argument, and it predicts the critical failure mode precisely: when adoption is *solicited* rather than *observed*, what spreads is performance of the new practice, not genuine uptake.

Practitioner capability built: Designing remnant-to-congregation expansion as a diffusion process rather than an instruction or enrollment process; identifying early adopters vs. those who require observable demonstration before they can move; not mistaking early-adopter enthusiasm for evidence of broad readiness.

---

**★ Edgar Schein, *Organizational Culture and Leadership* (5th ed., Wiley, 2017)**

Essential design contribution: Three levels of organizational culture — artifacts (visible structures and programs), espoused values (stated beliefs and aspirations), and *basic assumptions* (unconscious, taken-for-granted beliefs that operate below explicit awareness). Consumer church's basic assumptions include: attendance equals participation; content delivery equals formation; numerical growth equals health; congregant preferences are a legitimate criterion for ministry design. These assumptions operate at a level that theological argument and program change do not reach. Reform efforts that change artifacts and espoused values while leaving basic assumptions intact consistently fail — which explains most church renewal history.

Practitioner capability built: Distinguishing surface-level reform (rearranging programs and stated emphases) from the basic-assumption work that genuine institutional transition requires; reading which level of culture a proposed change is actually addressing and what level of resistance it will encounter; not confusing agreement with the formation vision (espoused value change) for readiness to embody it (basic assumption change).

---

**◆ Ronald Heifetz, *Leadership on the Line* (Harvard Business Review Press, 2002) / *The Practice of Adaptive Leadership* (2009)**

Essential design contribution: Technical problems are solved by applying existing knowledge and expertise; adaptive challenges require changed values, beliefs, and ways of working and cannot be solved by technical means. The ekklēsia transition is adaptive, not technical. Leaders who apply technical solutions to adaptive challenges produce activity without transformation and exhaust themselves and their congregations in the process while the adaptive gap remains untouched.

Practitioner capability built: Diagnosing whether a congregation or its leadership is treating the formation challenge as technical or adaptive; naming the adaptive work clearly enough that leaders do not mistake program change for genuine transition; holding the frame when technical pressure is high.

Note: Already in Development Plan Part 4. Listed here for curriculum domain placement.

---

**◆ Edwin Friedman, *A Failure of Nerve: Leadership in the Age of the Quick Fix* (Seabury Books, 2007)**

Essential design contribution: Differentiated leadership — the capacity to maintain one's own non-anxious presence in a system that is pulling toward reactivity. The leader's own interior regulation (what the Development Plan calls *hexis* under pastoral pressure) is the primary variable in whether the institutional change holds. A leader who cannot remain non-anxious under consumer pressure — financial, attendance, criticism, congregant distress — will revert to the consumer model regardless of theological conviction, not out of faithlessness but because the system's reactivity exceeds their regulatory capacity.

Practitioner capability built: Assessing a pastoral leader's differentiated capacity as a formation prerequisite; maintaining their own non-anxious presence when the system pushes back against the transition; reading congregational reactivity as a systems phenomenon rather than a personal failure.

Note: Already in Development Plan Part 4. Listed here for curriculum domain placement.

## Domain 3 — Diagnostic Precision: What Formation Is Working Against

*Naming what consumer church is actually producing at the implicit level — not as cultural critique but as diagnostic precision. A practitioner without this domain will be confused by why intelligent, sincere people resist genuine formation, will underestimate the depth of the counter-formation they are working against, and will default to corrective posture when what they encounter surprises them.*

---

**★ Christian Smith & Melinda Denton, *Soul Searching: The Religious and Spiritual Lives of American Teenagers* (Oxford University Press, 2005)**

Essential design contribution: The most rigorous empirical documentation of *Moralistic Therapeutic Deism* (MTD) as the operative theology of most American Christian congregants — cutting across denomination, stated orthodoxy, and attendance frequency. MTD holds: God exists and created the world; God wants people to be nice and feel good about themselves; the goal of life is to be happy and feel good; God is not particularly involved in daily life except in a crisis; good people go to heaven. This is the implicit theology the formation track is working against — not conscious atheism or identified consumerism, but a fully formed alternative framework operating below the level of stated belief.

Practitioner capability built: Naming what they are working against with diagnostic precision rather than generic "consumer culture" language; distinguishing stated theological belief from operative functional theology; not being surprised or morally offended when MTD shows up in a theologically literate congregation.

Note: Documented in a teenage cohort; the pattern holds for adults and is reinforced by *Souls in Transition* (Smith & Snell, 2009), which tracks the same cohort into young adulthood.

---

**★ Robert Bellah et al., *Habits of the Heart: Individualism and Commitment in American Life* (University of California Press, 1985; updated edition 1996)**

Essential design contribution: The cultural genealogy of American religious individualism — "Sheilaism" (each person constructs their own private religion from personal experience) as the cultural default that consumer church has accommodated rather than contested. Bellah establishes that individualism is not merely a preference or attitude but a culturally formed disposition operating at the implicit level, shaped by repeated social practices over generations — which means formation must address it at that level, not through argument or instruction about community values.

Practitioner capability built: Tracing the cultural formation behind what they encounter in a congregation rather than treating consumer patterns as individual moral failures or failures of will; understanding why communal formation practices feel threatening or alien to people who have been formed by American religious individualism; not personalizing what is structural.

## Domain 4 — Practitioner Accompaniment Methodology

*How to invite people into the formation arc without coercion; how to hold a group formation container; how to accompany people through genuine transition without rescuing them from it. This is the practitioner's practical craft — what they are actually doing in relationship to the people they accompany. This domain should enter the curriculum earlier than its position in this list suggests.*

---

**★ Dietrich Bonhoeffer, *Life Together* (Harper & Row, 1954; German original 1939)**

Essential design contribution: The most important theological treatment of what genuine Christian community requires and what happens when wish-dream community meets actual human beings. Bonhoeffer's central warning — the person who loves their *idea* of community will destroy real community when actual people do not conform to the ideal — names the operative dynamic in consumer church precisely and without moral softening. He built an actual formation community (Finkenwalde, 1935–1937) under extreme external pressure; the community's structure and Bonhoeffer's own non-anxious pastoral posture within it constitute a historical formation model the Scaling Mechanism Research does not yet include.

Practitioner capability built: Holding the formation container without performing idealized community; recognizing and naming wish-dream dynamics when they appear without shaming the people who carry them; maintaining the distinction between genuine assembly and its performance when that distinction is costly.

---

**★ Ruth Haley Barton, *Transforming Community: Creating the Presence of God in Your Group* (Jossey-Bass, 2007)**

Essential design contribution: Group spiritual formation methodology — specifically, how to structure communal retreat, shared discernment, group direction, and formation practices in ways that produce genuine interior work rather than group therapy, performance, or accountability culture. This is the practitioner methodology resource for Phase 2 formation track design.

Practitioner capability built: Designing and facilitating group formation sessions; structuring communal practice so the group container holds genuine formation work rather than collapsing into content delivery or mutual affirmation; knowing the difference between a well-run formation group and a group performing formation.

Note: *Strengthening the Soul of Your Leadership* (Barton) is already in Development Plan Part 4 as a pastoral leader formation resource. *Transforming Community* fills a distinct function: group facilitation methodology rather than individual leader formation. Both belong in the curriculum but in different modules.

---

**◆ William Miller & Stephen Rollnick, *Motivational Interviewing: Helping People Change* (3rd ed., Guilford Press, 2013)**

Essential design contribution: Evoking motivation rather than installing it — the practitioner's method for invitation without coercion. A person invited into a formation cohort must find their own reasons for entering the arc; those who enter because they were persuaded, expected to, or obligated will not sustain the between-session practices that formation requires. The MI spirit — partnership, acceptance, compassion, evocation — is the posture of the formation invitation and of accompaniment when resistance surfaces.

Practitioner capability built: Identifying formation-ready individuals; making an invitation that draws out existing hunger rather than implanting expectation; holding the accompaniment posture when someone resists the formation arc without escalating to correction or pressure.

Note: Already in Andrew's RPA evidence base. Direct application to the formation context is needed here; the invitation problem is an MI problem.

## Domain 5 — Missional Ecclesiology: The Theological Vision the Practitioner Holds

*Why genuine ekklēsia matters beyond congregation health or attendance metrics — the larger frame that sustains a practitioner through a 5–7 year arc with limited visible fruit. A practitioner who holds only the formation framework without the missional frame will lose the "why" when the transition is slow and costly.*

---

**★ Lesslie Newbigin, *The Gospel in a Pluralist Society* (Eerdmans, 1989)**
Key chapter: 18 — "The Congregation as Hermeneutic of the Gospel"

Essential design contribution: The congregation's visible common life — *sympatheia* active, *allēlōn* functions operating, *koinōnia* real — is the primary argument for the gospel's truth in a post-Christian context. Not programs, not apologetics, not individual behavior: the observable quality of the community as a community. This frames the entire formation project at its theological depth and connects it to the church's missionary identity. A congregation that functions as genuine *ekklēsia* is not merely healthier — it is the gospel made visible in the way the world most needs to see it.

Practitioner capability built: Articulating why the transition matters beyond institutional metrics; holding the missional frame under institutional pressure; resisting the reduction of the formation project to a church-improvement strategy.

---

**★ Rodney Stark, *The Rise of Christianity: A Sociologist Reconsiders History* (HarperOne, 1996)**

Essential design contribution: Sociological documentation of how the early church actually spread — through visible mutual aid, dramatic care for the sick and dying during plague years, and the observable quality of communal life. Not primarily through preaching or evangelistic programs. Stark's finding confirms the Development Plan's historical principle (demonstration more powerful than argument) with empirical evidence from the period the Development Plan most frequently cites, and provides the sociological parallel to Newbigin's theological framing.

Practitioner capability built: Grounding the remnant-as-demonstration strategy in historical evidence rather than theoretical argument; resisting pressure to accelerate the transition by substituting argument or program for visible formation; holding confidence in the long arc when no visible fruit has yet appeared.

## Curriculum Build Suggestions

These apply to any training design built from this source library, regardless of how the modules are eventually structured.

**1. The practitioner's own formation arc must precede curriculum engagement, not run parallel to it.**
A curriculum designed for people simultaneously in formation will produce different outcomes than one for people who have been through a formation arc. The most important design decision before any curriculum is built is whether this curriculum assumes practitioners are in formation now, have completed a formation arc, or are beginning one. That decision changes sequence, pace, and what the practitioner can actually integrate. Building the curriculum before this question is answered is building on an unverified assumption.

**2. Domain 4 — Accompaniment Methodology — should enter the curriculum earlier than its domain position suggests.**
The temptation in curriculum design is theology first, theory second, methodology at the end. For formation practitioners, methodology should surface early — not because it precedes theological understanding, but because *doing the accompaniment work* is itself a formation experience. Bonhoeffer and Barton placed late in a curriculum will be processed as interesting ideas. Placed early, with reflection on the practitioner's own formation experience, they become formation tools.

**3. The curriculum needs a practitioner self-assessment before any source domain is engaged.**
Gateway condition from First Things §4, applied to the practitioner-in-training: Where am I in my own formation arc? What are my own defended interiorities? Where does my *hexis* not yet hold under pressure? Without this at the front, practitioners learn about formation without detecting their own unformation — and will teach what they have not yet lived.

**4. Domain 3 — Diagnostic Precision — is the domain practitioners will be most tempted to skip and the most undervalued.**
The practitioner who skips this domain will be confused by why intelligent, sincere people resist genuine formation. They will underestimate the depth of the counter-formation operating in the people they accompany. They will default to corrective posture when formation posture does not produce quick movement. Smith's MTD framework and Bellah's cultural genealogy are not supplementary reading — they are the practitioner's primary diagnostic resource for what they will encounter every week.

**5. Distinguish the minimum viable curriculum for cohort facilitation from the comprehensive practitioner formation curriculum.**
A cohort facilitator — someone who can hold a group formation container of 8–12 people — needs Domains 1, 4, and enough of Domain 2 to understand the scaling dynamics they are operating within. A congregational consultant — someone who can advise a pastoral leader through the full Phase A → Phase B transition — needs all five domains plus the full Development Plan as a framework. Attempting to build both simultaneously will produce neither. Identify the minimum viable practitioner target before designing a single module.

**6. The curriculum itself must be field-tested against a live cohort facilitation experience before it is treated as complete.**
No amount of theoretical correctness substitutes for running a cohort and discovering what the curriculum actually equips vs. leaves the practitioner unprepared for. The Development Plan's governing principle — research before building — applies equally here: the curriculum is not finished until it has been tested against what a practitioner actually encounters in the room.

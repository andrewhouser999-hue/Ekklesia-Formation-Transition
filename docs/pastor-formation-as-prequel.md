# Formation & Transition — Pastor Formation as Prequel to Remnant Cohort Formation

---

## Purpose

Movement 5 of the Pastoral Briefing Guide establishes that the pastor's own formation is the gateway condition for a congregational transition. The Pastoral Formation Pathway Assessment establishes which existing pathways can produce that formation. Neither document addresses the question a pastor actually facing this decision will ask next: **formation for what, first, exactly — and in what order relative to forming anyone else?** This document answers that question by reading the pathway assessment against the remnant strategy: the pastor is not forming himself in order to lead the whole congregation through formation. He is forming himself as a prequel to leading a *formed minority* — and the two processes turn out to overlap rather than run in sequence.

*When used:* When a pastor (or Andrew, briefing one) is past the "has our pastor been through this" question and needs to know what to actually do next — not as a checklist, but as a sequencing logic.

*Who uses it:* Andrew, in direct work with a pastor or pastoral group past the gateway-condition conversation; also functions as seed material for the track's own Phase 2, Item 7 (Remnant Cohort Design).

## Part 1 — Two Layers the Track Has Kept Separate

The Pathway Assessment answers one question: *what forms the pastor's own hexis* — his stable interior, his non-anxious regulatory capacity, the *hexis* that holds under cost. It does not answer a second, distinct question: *what equips a formed pastor to hold formation space for someone else.*

These are not the same competency, and the track's own evidence already shows the gap:

**Wesley's circuit riders.** The Scaling Mechanism Research names the mechanism directly: riders were deployed only after having "been through the container themselves... formation was the prerequisite to facilitation — not ordination." Being formed was necessary for facilitating, but the deployment step — training someone who has been formed to *hold the container for others* — was a distinct, deliberate act. It was not assumed to follow automatically from the rider's own formation.

**Peer accountability's failure mode, read in reverse.** The Pathway Assessment's verdict on peer accountability names why it fails: "a peer cannot provide [implicit-level formation] because a peer shares the same implicit-level blind spots... A formed guide is required to hold formed space." Read positively, this states the facilitator requirement precisely: someone further along the formation arc, able to perceive what the person being formed cannot yet see in themselves, and able to hold that posture without sliding into correction. A pastor who has met his own gateway condition has become *further along* — but "further along" and "able to hold that space for others without collapsing into correction under relational pressure" are not guaranteed to arrive together.

**1 Peter 5:3 — *typos*, not instruction.** The Polity Foundation document reads *typoi ginomenoi tou poimniou* ("becoming examples to the flock") as formation-by-exemplified-*hexis* — the elder's formed character becomes a reference pattern the body's *sympatheia* mechanism registers and mirrors, largely without the elder doing anything active. This is a real mechanism, but it is passive: it describes how a formed pastor's presence affects the body generally. It is not the same as the active, differentiated skill of sitting with a specific person's affections diagnostic, false-identity surfacing, or grief without either fixing it or absorbing it. *Typos* explains ambient influence. It does not explain facilitation competency.

**What none of the four assessed pathways guarantee.** CI coaching, spiritual direction, the Ignatian Exercises, and peer accountability were all assessed in the Pathway Assessment as *receiving* containers — what forms the pastor. None were assessed for whether they train the pastor to *hold* formation space for someone else. A pastor could complete 18 months of CI coaching, meet all three gateway criteria, and still have no practiced capacity to sit through another person's dysregulation without either taking control of it or being destabilized by it. The two capacities are correlated — a well-formed interior is a precondition for facilitation capacity — but they are not identical, and the track has not yet assessed the second one directly.

## Part 2 — Why "Prequel" Is the Wrong Shape (and What the Right Shape Is)

"Prequel" implies a completed first stage followed by a second. Three pieces of evidence already in the track resist that shape:

**The Pathway Assessment's own Response A** rejects sequencing on completion: a pastor "may begin the transition before the gateway condition is fully met," on two conditions — the transition is understood as an extension of his own formation (Development Plan §6: "the transition itself is *paideia*"), and he remains in active formation *through* it, not before it.

**1 Peter 5:3's grammar.** *Ginomenoi* is a present participle — "becoming," not "having become." The Polity Foundation document notes this directly: "Formation in the elder is not a completed prerequisite that is then done; it is an ongoing *conversatio morum*." The elder is continuously in the process of being formed while continuously constituting the reference pattern for others. The text does not contemplate a pastor who finishes forming and then starts forming others.

**Acts 20 and the Twelve.** Paul's three years with the Ephesian elders — "I did not cease night or day to admonish every one with tears" (v.31) — and Jesus' three years with the Twelve are not a formation phase followed by a facilitation phase. They are one sustained, overlapping process in which the leader's own formation (Jesus excepted, as sinless — but the disciples' formation of each other post-Pentecost follows the same overlapping pattern) and the followers' formation happen in the same relational container at the same time.

**The right shape: threshold, not completion, followed by concurrent deepening.** The pastor needs to have crossed a *threshold* in his own formation — not finished it — before opening a remnant cohort. After that threshold, leading the cohort becomes the next layer of his own *paideia*, tested under exactly the kind of relational pressure gateway criterion 3 (*hexis* that holds under cost) requires. The cohort does not wait for him to arrive. It becomes part of what forms him further. This is the Development Plan's §6 claim applied specifically to the moment of opening a cohort, not just to the later congregational transition.

## Part 3 — What the Threshold Requires, Practically

Read against Parts 1 and 2, the pathway assessment's own diagnostic questions (Part 5) function differently than they first appear. They are not a one-time gate to clear before starting. They are an ongoing checkpoint that should be re-run once the pastor is actually facilitating, because facilitation pressure will surface things solitary formation work did not.

**Before opening a cohort:**

1. **A personal formation container is active** — ranked by the existing assessment: CI coaching with intentional targeting of pastoral-specific implicit patterns (most mechanism-appropriate, contingent on practitioner availability); extended spiritual direction with a highly formed director, 3+ years; or 19th Annotation Ignatian Exercises with sustained post-retreat integration. Peer accountability alone does not qualify as this container.
2. **The pastor's formed guide remains in place**, not concluded. This is the safeguard implied by Wesley's own practice but not stated as a rule anywhere in the track: "Wesley traveled constantly to hold the class meeting to its actual function, not just its form." A pastor facilitating a remnant while his own *hexis* is still being tested needs someone doing for him structurally what he is attempting to do for the cohort. Without this, the facilitation pressure is the thing that reveals — too late — that the threshold hadn't actually been crossed.
3. **The remnant is identified, not recruited.** The city-strategy pattern in the Remnant Theology document (Antioch, Thessalonica, Ephesus) is consistently "the already-oriented" — people who already show some hunger for depth over performance, not a program announced to the whole congregation. A recruitment posture reintroduces the consumption architecture the whole track exists to move away from.

**Once the cohort is meeting, as an ongoing checkpoint (re-run the Pathway Assessment's Part 5 questions periodically, not just once):**

4. **Early friction is read as data, not failure** — Development Plan §7 ("failure is diagnostic, not spiritual") applies at cohort scale before it ever reaches congregational scale. A cohort session that goes badly is information about where the pastor's *hexis* is not yet holding, not evidence the whole approach is wrong.
5. **The "not yet" question stays live** — Pathway Assessment Part 5, Q4: can the pastor receive "not yet" about his own readiness without reorganizing to disprove it? This question is more diagnostic *after* facilitation has begun than before, because the defensiveness it's testing for is most visible under the pressure of actually leading someone else's formation, not in the abstract.

## Part 4 — What Remains Unresolved

The facilitator-specific competency this document names in Part 1 — what, precisely, a pathway must add on top of personal *hexis* formation to produce someone who can hold formation space for others — has not been assessed anywhere in the track. CI coaching trains practitioners in the facilitating role elsewhere in the Covenant Identity materials, but the Pathway Assessment evaluated CI coaching only as a container that forms the *pastor as recipient*, not as training for the *pastor as facilitator* of a remnant cohort. That is a real, unaddressed gap.

This document is not a substitute for Phase 2, Item 7 (Remnant Cohort Design), which the Development Plan already specifies requires Item 1 (Scaling Mechanism Research, complete) and Item 3 (Leader Formation Prerequisite Assessment). Item 3 was never built as its own document — its intended function was substantially absorbed into the Pathway Assessment's Part 1 (Gateway Criteria), but that absorption was not deliberate, and it leaves the facilitator-competency question without a dedicated home. Flagging this explicitly: **Item 3, as originally scoped, is not actually complete**, and Item 7 cannot be built soundly until either this document or a proper Item 3 closes that gap with more than inference from Wesley and 1 Peter 5.

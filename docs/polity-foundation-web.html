Formation & Transition — Core Track · Phase 1, Item 2

Polity Foundation

Purpose

This is the Phase 1, item 2 document of the Formation & Transition development track. Its job is to fill the gap the founding documents left open: they established that genuine ekklēsia requires formed members and that the sympatheia mechanism depends on individual hexis — but they were silent on how ordained leadership fits into that mechanism.

This is not a denominational polity argument. It does not adjudicate between elder-led, congregational, or episcopal governance structures. It is a biblical-theological account of a narrower question: how does the formation (or unformation) of a congregation's leaders shape the conditions for genuine assembly? The texts addressed here are read through the hexis/formation lens the founding documents establish. What emerges is not an organizational chart but a formation chain — and a clear account of where the chain's load-bearing link is located.

Scope note

Leadership texts carry the formation question further than the founding documents could, but they do not change the founding documents' argument. They extend it: what individual hexis formation does for a member's capacity to participate in genuine assembly, leadership hexis formation does for the whole congregation's capacity. The mechanism is the same. The stakes are larger.

What the New Testament Assumes

The NT does not describe a flat ecclesiology. Every letter addressing congregational life assumes the presence of recognized leaders — episkopoi (overseers), presbyteroi (elders), diakonoi (deacons). This is not incidental. The NT authors are not organizational pragmatists who tolerated hierarchy for administrative convenience. They are describing a body whose design includes differentiated roles. Whether one tradition names those roles bishop, elder, or pastor, the NT assumption is consistent: the ekklēsia has people in it who bear particular responsibility for the body's health, who will be held to a higher account (James 3:1), and whose character shapes what the body can and cannot become.

This is the foundational claim the polity question rests on: leadership is not external to the body's formation mechanism — it is structural to it. The elder does not manage formation from outside. The elder's own formed (or unformed) interior is inside the body's sympatheia network, and the sympatheia network is constitutionally incapable of screening it out.

1The Qualification Lists as Formation Diagnostics

The NT's primary leadership qualification texts are 1 Timothy 3:1–7 and Titus 1:5–9. They are almost universally read as candidate screening criteria — a checklist applied before someone is appointed. That reading is not wrong, but it misses the theological architecture underneath the list. The qualifications are not character requirements imposed from outside on an otherwise neutral role. They are a description of what a formed person looks like — and a diagnostic for whether the hexis formation that makes genuine pastoral function possible has actually occurred.

"Therefore an overseer must be above reproach, the husband of one wife, sober-minded, self-controlled, respectable, hospitable, able to teach, not a drunkard, not violent but gentle, not quarrelsome, not a lover of money. He must manage his own household well, with all dignity keeping his children submissive, for if someone does not know how to manage his own household, how will he care for God's church? He must not be a recent convert... He must be well thought of by outsiders."1 Timothy 3:1–7, ESV

Language Note — the qualification vocabulary

The list in 1 Tim 3 is dominated by compound adjectives built on alpha-privatives (negating prefixes) — an-epilēmptos (above reproach: un-graspable, no handhold for accusation), a-machon (not quarrelsome), a-philarguron (not a lover of money) — and by sōphrōn and nēphalios (sober-minded, self-controlled; both from word-groups describing temperate, regulated interiority). These are not behavioral rules but descriptions of a stable, regulated interior that does not overshoot in the directions each alpha-privative names. The list is a portrait of hexis, not a compliance checklist.

The Titus 1 list adds egkratēs (self-controlled, disciplined — from kratein, to hold; the person who has been trained to hold their interior rather than be moved by it) and mē authadē (not self-willed — the person whose interior is not dominated by self-reference). Egkratēs is an explicitly formation word — it describes a capacity produced by training, not a quality some people happen to have.

Titus 1:5–9 adds one element that 1 Timothy 3 does not: the elder's children are to be believers "not open to the charge of debauchery or insubordination" (v.6). The formation requirement extends to the household. This is not a genetic claim about children. It is an extension of the formation logic from 1 Tim 3:5 — the leader's hexis is visible in the lives of those most continuously exposed to it. If the leader's unformed patterns are suppressing sympatheia in their own home, they will suppress it in the congregation.

Language Note — 1 Tim 3:5, household management and epimeleisthai

"If someone does not know how to manage his own household, how will he care for God's church?" The verb for "care" is epimeleisthai — to take care of, to give attention to. It is the same word used for the Good Samaritan's care of the wounded man (Luke 10:34–35) — careful, attending, costly provision. The logic is comparative: the household is the smaller formation context. Hexis formed (or not formed) there is the evidence for whether hexis will hold in the larger one. The congregation is not a different kind of challenge from the household; it is a larger version of the same challenge. The elder who cannot hold non-anxious presence with a difficult child has not been shown to be disqualified from any role whatsoever — they have been shown that their formation is not yet complete for this particular function.

The lists, read through the formation lens, say this: the NT does not appoint leaders and then hope they will become formed. It looks for formation evidence before appointing, because the leader's formed or unformed interior will be inside the body's sympatheia mechanism from the moment they are recognized as a leader.

2The Formation-First Sequence in Pastoral Practice

Acts 20:17–35 is the NT's most extensive description of what formed pastoral leadership looks like in practice. Paul addresses the elders of Ephesus — people he has already recognized as leaders — and what he describes is not organizational strategy. It is the interior posture of a formed person in a pastoral function.

"Pay careful attention to yourselves and to all the flock, in which the Holy Spirit has made you overseers, to care for the church of God, which he obtained with his own blood."Acts 20:28, ESV

Language Note — prosechete... heautois (Acts 20:28)

"Pay careful attention to yourselves" — prosechete heautois, literally "hold your attention toward yourselves." The word prosechō means to bring the mind alongside something and keep it there — the same root as proskarterountes (Acts 2:42, the devoted, continuous attention of the early community to the apostles' teaching). The sequence is explicit: yourselves first, then the flock. Not as a self-care concession but as a formation prerequisite. The elder who has not attended to their own interior cannot shepherd others' interiors. They have nothing formed to bring into the sympatheia network. Acts 20:28 is the pastoral equivalent of the founding documents' individual formation prerequisite: self-examination (dokimazō) before entering the assembly.

What Paul describes of his own pastoral formation in this address (vv. 18–21, 31, 33–34) is instructive:

  • "I did not shrink from declaring to you anything that was profitable" (v.20) — non-anxious honesty; the interior settledness that can name difficult things without self-protection
  • "I did not cease night or day to admonish every one with tears" (v.31) — three years of sustained, relational, emotionally present formation. Not content delivery. The tears indicate sympatheia functioning: Paul's interior was registering the Ephesian elders' states and responding with felt-with engagement, not clinical instruction
  • "I coveted no one's silver or gold or apparel" (v.33) — financial independence from the congregation's approval. This is a formation marker: an elder dependent on the congregation's financial approval cannot hold non-anxious presence when the congregation is displeased. The approval-dependence creates a deference posture that makes genuine pastoral formation impossible.
  • "I labored, working with my own hands" (v.34) — the formed leader holds their economic life such that pastoral function cannot be leveraged against them

The formation Paul describes in himself is precisely what he is now passing to the Ephesian elders through this address: the interior posture that makes pastoral function possible is formed through relationship, sustained over time, and visible in specific behavioral patterns.

The warning that follows (vv. 29–30) connects directly to the hexis framework: "fierce wolves will come in among you, not sparing the flock; and from among your own selves will arise men speaking twisted things." The hexis trained by paideia is Hebrews 5:14's "powers of discernment trained by constant practice to distinguish good from evil." The elder whose hexis is not formed cannot reliably distinguish the wolves from the sheep, because that discernment is a trained perceptive faculty — not a one-time doctrinal decision.

The formation-first sequence in Acts 20:28: pay careful attention to yourselves → attend to the flock. The attending-to-the-flock that skips the first clause is not neutral — it is an unformed person operating in the body's sympatheia network without the hexis required to navigate it without damage.

3The Elder as Formation Reference

Two texts establish the elder's specific function as a formation reference — not merely a doctrinal authority or organizational head.

1 Peter 5:1–4

"...shepherd the flock of God that is among you, exercising oversight, not under compulsion but willingly, as God would have you; not for shameful gain but eagerly; not domineering over those in your charge, but being examples to the flock."1 Peter 5:2–3, ESV

Language Note — typoi ginomenoi tou poimniou (1 Pet 5:3)

"Being examples to the flock" translates typoi ginomenoi — literally "becoming patterns/impressions." Typos (from typtō, to strike or imprint) is the word for a die that strikes a coin and leaves its impression — or the impression itself. In Phil 3:17 ("keep your eyes on those who walk according to the typos you have in us") and 1 Tim 4:12 ("be an typos to the believers in speech, in conduct, in love, in faith, in purity"), the same word functions as: a person whose life pattern can be imprinted in others through imitation. This is not charismatic leadership or positional authority. It is formation by exemplified hexis — the elder's formed character becomes the concrete reference around which others' formation orients.

Ginomenoi is a present participle — "becoming," not "having become." The elder is continuously in the process of being formed, continuously constituting the reference pattern. Formation in the elder is not a completed prerequisite that is then done; it is an ongoing conversatio morum (to use the Benedictine term from the Scaling Mechanism Research). The congregation's orientation toward the elder's pattern is not passive reception — it is the body's sympatheia mechanism registering the elder's interior and mirroring it, whether the elder is aware of this or not.

The elder who is not formed does not simply fail to provide a positive reference. They actively provide a formation reference — of what unformed pastoral leadership looks like: defended, approval-dependent, reactive to threat, consuming of the flock's care rather than giving it. The body's sympatheia mechanism does not distinguish between good and harmful formation references. It mirrors what it registers. 1 Pet 5:3's positive framing implies the negative: an unformed elder is not a neutral presence in the body's formation network.

Hebrews 13:7 and 13:17

"Remember your leaders, those who spoke to you the word of God. Consider the outcome of their way of life, and imitate their faith."Hebrews 13:7, ESV

Language Note — mimeithe tēn pistin and theōrountes tēn ekbasin tēs anastrophēs

"Consider the outcome of their way of life" — theōrountes tēn ekbasin tēs anastrophēs — "observing the outcome/issue of their conduct/manner of life." Anastrophē is the whole behavioral pattern of a life — the manner of moving through the world. Ekbasis is the outcome, the end-point, the issue. The congregation is directed to observe not just what the leader says or believes but what their life produces — the fruit that is visible in the trajectory of their character. This is hexis assessment applied to the leader: what has their formation produced, and is it worth imitating?

"Imitate their faith" — mimeithe tēn pistin — is direct formation language. The congregation is designed to orient its formation around a concrete, observable human being whose hexis is visible in the pattern of their life. This is not hero worship; it is the body using the formation reference the Spirit has placed within it.

"Obey your leaders and submit to them, for they are keeping watch over your souls, as those who will have to give an account. Let them do this with joy and not with groaning, for that would be of no advantage to you."Hebrews 13:17, ESV

Language Note — agrupnousin hyper tōn psychōn hymōn

"Keeping watch over your souls" — agrupnousin hyper tōn psychōn hymōn — agrupnoō is to stay awake, to keep vigil, to maintain watchful attention. The elder who "keeps watch over souls" is not primarily a doctrinal guardian (though that is included). They are maintaining perceptual attention to the interior state of the people in their care — noticing, attending, registering what is happening in the body's members. This is the pastoral function of formed hexis: Hebrews 5:14's "powers of discernment trained by constant practice" applied specifically to the care of souls. The elder's trained hexis produces the perceptual capacity to see what is happening in the body that the body itself may not yet name.

"As those who will have to give an account" — the heightened accountability James 3:1 names ("we who teach will be judged with greater strictness") is grounded here in the pastoral watchfulness function. The elder will answer for whether they maintained the vigilant attention this function requires. An elder whose own formation has not produced the hexis for interior perception cannot exercise this watchfulness — they will miss what they have not been formed to see.

4The Mechanism: How Leader Hexis Flows into Body Sympatheia

Pull the texts together and the mechanism becomes visible:

The design: The body of Christ has sympatheia built in — 1 Cor 12:26's indicative: when one member suffers, the whole body is constituted to register it. That mechanism does not operate only peer-to-peer. It operates through leadership as well. The congregation is constitutionally oriented toward its leaders (Heb 13:7: "consider the outcome of their way of life, and imitate their faith"). This is not a cultural dynamic that could be designed away; it is built into the body's formation architecture.

The chain: The leader's hexis (formed or unformed) → registered by the body's sympatheia mechanism → mirrored in the congregation's dispositions → shapes the conditions for (or against) genuine assembly.

When the chain carries formed hexis
Non-anxious, stable interiorthe typos of 1 Pet 5:3 → Sympatheia registers stabilityconcrete orientation point for the congregation → Perceptual watchfulnessagrupnoō, Heb 13:17 → Differentiated response1 Thess 5:14's four-part taxonomy → Formation conditions available
When the chain carries unformed hexis
Defended, dysregulated, or approval-dependent interior → Registered by sympatheia before any word is spoken → Congregation mirrors the patternnot by conscious choice → Unformed interiority becomes the pastoral norm → Formation conditions suppressed at the source

This is why James 3:1 is not an isolated severity claim. "Not many of you should become teachers, for we who teach will be judged with greater strictness" is a recognition that the teacher/elder/leader is inside the formation mechanism with amplified effect. Their hexis does not produce proportional formation influence; it produces disproportionate formation influence because the body is designed to orient toward its leaders.

The downward formation chain stated plainly: leader formation → leader hexis → leader non-anxious presence in the body → body sympatheia can function freely → allēlōn functions possible → genuine assembly. Interrupted at any point in this chain, and the body can still gather. It cannot yet assemble.

5What the Texts Do Not Resolve

They do not prescribe a polity structure. Whether elders govern by council, whether a senior pastor has different authority from other elders, whether a bishop oversees multiple congregations — these questions are not answered here and are not the right questions for this development track. The formation-shaped ecclesiology this document establishes is polity-neutral in its structure: the mechanism operates the same way regardless of whether the congregation calls its formed leaders elders, pastors, or bishops. The load-bearing element is the formation, not the title.

They do not specify what elder formation looks like in practice. Acts 20 describes Paul's formation of the Ephesian elders — three years, relational, emotionally present, with tears. It does not prescribe a curriculum. What the formation must produce (the hexis qualifications of 1 Tim 3 and Titus 1) is clear. What container produces it is not specified. This is the Phase 1, item 3 question (Leader Formation Prerequisite Assessment), and that document cannot be built without the Scaling Mechanism Research (item 1) having established what formation containers actually work.

They do not address the elder who has been in role without prior formation. The qualification texts describe prerequisites for appointment; they are silent about what happens when someone is already recognized as a leader and has not yet been through formation. The transition case — "I am the current pastor and I need to be formed" — is real, common, and not addressed in these texts. That is Item 3 territory.

6Implications for Phase 1, Item 3

The Leader Formation Prerequisite Assessment (item 3) can now be built on this foundation. What the polity texts establish for that document:

What the assessment must evaluate: The 1 Tim 3 and Titus 1 qualifications, read as formation diagnostics, describe the hexis markers the assessment should probe — not as a checklist ("are you above reproach?" is not an answerable question by self-report) but as descriptors of what formed interiority produces in the pastoral context: non-anxious presence, financial independence from approval, the capacity to be honest without self-protection, regulatory stability under congregational pressure.

The formation evidence question: The household management test (1 Tim 3:5) provides a model for the assessment's approach: not "do you believe the right things about formation?" but "where can formation evidence be observed in your life under pressure?" The household is the smaller context. The pastoral record is the larger one. The assessment should look for evidence, not self-report.

The self-attention prerequisite: Acts 20:28 ("pay careful attention to yourselves first") names the daily practice the assessment should probe: does this leader have a structured practice of attending to their own interior before attending to the flock? A formed leader who has no regular practice of katanoeō — deliberate prior consideration of their own interior state — is not exercising the watchfulness function (agrupnoō) Acts 20 and Heb 13:17 describe.

The formation reference question: 1 Pet 5:3 (typos) and Heb 13:7 (anastrophē — the observable pattern of a life) suggest the assessment should include an external observation component: what do people in sustained proximity to this leader observe in the pattern of their life? Self-report and observed pattern are both needed; a discrepancy between them is itself a diagnostic finding.

The amplified-stakes framing: James 3:1 and Heb 13:17 together establish that the assessment is not primarily about protecting the leader from a role they're not ready for. It is about protecting the congregation from having an unformed hexis inside their sympatheia network at the highest-structural-weight position. The assessment must be honest about this — and honest with the leader about what that weight means for their formation obligation.

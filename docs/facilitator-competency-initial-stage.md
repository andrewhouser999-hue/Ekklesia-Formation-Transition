## Purpose

**What it is:** A capacity and posture framework defining what a person must be able to do and hold in order to lead modern Western congregants through the initial stage of encounter with the biblical participatory assembly model.

**When used:** When a pastor or pastoral team needs to assess whether a specific person — themselves or another — is the right person to introduce the ekklēsia model to a congregation that has not yet heard it.

**Who uses it:** The pastor leading the exploration, or a team determining who facilitates initial-stage conversations with congregants.

**What it is not:** A credential checklist, a theological certification standard, or a qualification framework for mid-stage or implementation-stage work. This document is scoped exclusively to Stage 1 — the moment of first hearing.

**Development Plan note:** This document satisfies Item 3b (facilitator competency). Item 3a (pastor-as-recipient gateway assessment) remains open and is a separate build.

---

## The Initial Stage Defined

The initial stage is the moment when congregants encounter the participatory assembly model for the first time — not as an academic concept, but as a claim about what their own congregation is meant to be.

This stage is categorically different from what follows. Congregants at this point are not wrestling with what to do; they are processing whether the claim is true, whether it threatens what they have given themselves to, and whether the person presenting it can be trusted. The institutional immune response of an established congregation to a paradigm challenge is nearly automatic. At the individual level it sounds like: *"You are telling me that what I have given my life to was inadequate or wrong."*

Nothing about Stage 1 is primarily informational. It is relational and formational, and it requires a facilitator who understands that distinction and whose entire approach is shaped by it.

---

## Why Standard Qualification Thinking Fails Here

The instinct to ask "who is qualified?" is correct. The problem is how "qualified" is typically defined. Theological expertise in ecclesiology and strategic competence in leadership are Stage 3 qualifications imported into a Stage 1 context. They will produce the wrong result.

A person who can argue the NT case for participatory assembly but cannot hold the anxiety of the initial stage is not qualified — they are dangerous. The theological precision that makes a person effective in mid-stage wrestling or late-stage implementation will, in Stage 1, come across as argument, and argument in Stage 1 triggers defense rather than formation.

There is also a framing error to name explicitly. If the facilitator presents the passive gathering model as *wrong* — a failure the congregation must repent of — the congregation will hear an accusation. The frame that the initial stage requires is *recovery*: the congregation is being invited back to something they were designed for but have not yet accessed. Recovery language opens; correction language closes. A facilitator who cannot maintain the recovery frame under pressure cannot hold Stage 1.

---

## Two Configurations This Framework Addresses

This document uses "facilitator" to mean the person who holds Stage 1 conversations with congregants. That person may be the pastor himself or an external person the pastor brings in. The framework applies differently in each case.

**Case A — The pastor is the facilitator.** He is introducing the model to his own congregation. The relational trust condition is already met — but the nature of that trust creates a specific complication: the congregation's trust in him was formed in the context of the model he is now questioning. A pastor who has been the primary leader of the passive gathering model may find that existing trust works against reception — congregants may hear a self-indictment in his challenge of the model he built. The accountability structure in Case A runs to the pastor's elders or governing body rather than to himself, and Diagnostic Question #5 should be read in that direction.

**Case B — An external person is the facilitator.** The pastor brings in a third party to hold the initial-stage conversation. The framework applies throughout as written. Relational trust is the primary condition to establish before the external facilitator enters the room, and the accountability structure runs directly to the pastor.

Case B is the cleaner application of this framework. Case A requires reading Capacity #2 (relational trust) and Capacity #6 (pastoral accountability) with the modifications noted above.

## The Capacity Framework: What the Facilitator Must Be Able to Do

These are observable, testable capacities — not postures (those follow in the next section). Each can be evaluated before the facilitator enters the room.

**1. Present the model as recovery, not correction.**
The facilitator can describe the ekklēsia model using language that locates the congregation as people made for something they have not yet experienced — not people who have been doing something wrong. The specific test: ask the facilitator to explain why the passive gathering model fell short. If their explanation assigns blame to persons or institutions, they are in a corrective posture. If it locates the failure in structural and historical forces (Christendom captivity, consumer liturgy) while naming what the congregation is designed for, they are in a recovery posture.

**2. Establish or carry relational trust before speaking into the paradigm.**
The facilitator either already holds relational equity with the specific congregation, or they can name how that gap will be explicitly bridged before they speak. An outsider presenting a paradigm challenge to a congregation that does not yet trust them will produce a debate about the model, not formation toward it. The content cannot land through a relational gap. Bridging the gap is not optional; it is a prerequisite condition that must be resolved before Stage 1 begins.

**3. Hold questions open without forcing resolution.**
When a congregant asks a hard question or voices resistance, the facilitator can receive the question, name it honestly, and not be pulled into immediate answer or defense. The initial stage requires that the question be allowed to remain open long enough for the Spirit to work with it. A facilitator who feels compelled to resolve every question before leaving the room is compressing the formation timeline to their own comfort level, not the congregation's readiness. Heifetz calls this "productive disequilibrium" — the discomfort that motivates adaptive work must be sustained long enough for the congregation to do the work themselves; a facilitator who resolves it prematurely removes the very pressure that enables formation (*Leadership Without Easy Answers*, 1994).

**4. Name structural failure modes without assigning blame to persons.**
The facilitator can identify the three structural failure modes of the passive gathering model as structural patterns — not as failures of the pastor, the elders, or the congregation. *Consumption architecture* means the gathering is designed to be received rather than participated in. *Parallel presence* means members attend together but without registering or being registered by one another. *Platform delivery* means the body's ministry flows from one person to many, rather than among members. (These are defined fully in *Formation & Transition — The Allēlōn Functions — What Active Operation Requires.*) This distinction matters because the people in the room are likely invested in those structures. Naming the structure as the problem while honoring the people who have inhabited it is a skill that must be practiced, not assumed.

**5. Recognize and name grief as a valid response.**
When congregants realize they have been in a gathering model that did not equip them for full body function, the appropriate emotional response is often grief, not immediate enthusiasm. The facilitator must be able to name this — "That is a real loss, and it makes sense that it lands that way" — without redirecting grief toward action or reframing it prematurely as opportunity. A facilitator who is uncomfortable with congregational grief will suppress it, and suppressed grief becomes resistance.

**6. Operate under pastoral accountability, not autonomously.**
The facilitator does not determine pace, scope, or continuation independently. All of those decisions belong to the pastoral leadership already in place. The facilitator's role is to hold the formation space the pastor has authorized — not to extend it, advocate for its continuation, or make arguments for why the process should go further. A person who regularly lobbies for their own continued role is operating with the wrong posture.

## The Posture Framework: What the Facilitator Must Be Able to Hold

Postures are interior dispositions that shape every interaction. They are harder to assess than capacities, but they are also harder to fake — particularly under pressure. The following five postures are required for Stage 1.

**1. Non-anxious presence.**
The facilitator's own anxiety about the congregation's response cannot leak into the room. Anxiety in a facilitator is contagious — it signals to the congregation that the question is dangerous rather than generative. A non-anxious facilitator does not perform calm; they are genuinely unafraid of the congregation's resistance, confusion, or refusal. The way to assess this is not by asking the facilitator how they feel about conflict; it is to observe them in a high-friction conversation where the stakes are real and see whether their responses come from their own formation or from the pressure in the room. To create the observation opportunity: raise a hard objection in the pre-briefing conversation ("Several people will feel this is a criticism of everything we've built — how do you hold that?"), observe the candidate when pushed back on their own convictions, or examine a known instance from their ministry history where they faced genuine resistance. Anxiety that is manageable in low-stakes settings will amplify in high-stakes ones. Only what is observed under real friction is reliable evidence. (Friedman, *A Failure of Nerve*, is the primary source for non-anxious presence as a leadership formation concept.)

**2. Formation posture, not corrective posture.**
The facilitator enters the room oriented toward what the congregation is being drawn *toward* — not away from. This is not naivety about the problems of the passive model; it is a consistent priority: the congregation's destination governs the conversation, not their departure point. A facilitator who is more animated by what is wrong than by what is possible has drifted into corrective posture, regardless of how carefully they use recovery language. The energy behind the words is as informative as the words themselves.

**3. Invitation without agenda.**
The facilitator wants the congregation to move — but they are not controlling the pace or the outcome. Invitation without agenda means the facilitator is genuinely willing to hold Stage 1 for as long as the congregation needs it, without working against that timeline because they have somewhere they want the congregation to arrive. A facilitator who privately views Stage 1 as a stage to move through quickly — rather than a stage to inhabit fully — is not in the right posture. The congregation's readiness, not the facilitator's vision, sets the pace.

**4. Condition-creator, not transformation agent.**
The facilitator does not cause transformation; they create conditions in which transformation can occur. This distinction is grounded in the track's established mechanism: the Spirit works through the conditions that paideia creates — regular, structured exposure to the body functioning as it was designed — not through the facilitator's skill or persuasion directly. The congregation's movement toward participatory assembly is Spirit-wrought, not facilitator-produced. A facilitator who treats the congregation's resistance as their own failure, or their movement as their own accomplishment, has collapsed this mechanism. The job is to hold the space faithfully; the Spirit produces the result.

**5. Pastoral submission.**
The facilitator holds the form posture of 1 Peter 5:3 — *ginomenoi typos tou poimniou*, becoming an example to the flock rather than lording over it. Applied to Stage 1, this means the facilitator leads by demonstration and invitation, not by authority or argument. The facilitator does not claim standing over the congregation's existing structures; they stand alongside the congregation as someone who has walked the path ahead of them. This posture is incompatible with the facilitator positioning themselves as the congregation's expert or corrective voice.

## The Lived-Experience Prerequisite

There is one non-negotiable qualification that precedes all others: the facilitator must have personally participated in a functioning participatory assembly — not studied one, read about one, or visited one. They must have discovered and exercised their own gifts within a body where all-member ministry was the actual operating pattern, and they must be able to speak from that experience rather than from theory.

The reason this is non-negotiable is not sociological but anthropological. According to Hebrews 5:12–14, solid food belongs to those whose faculties have been trained by practice. The initial stage — helping congregants hear and receive a paradigm challenge about the nature of their assembly — is solid food work. A facilitator whose only relationship to the participatory model is intellectual cannot hold it with the embodied confidence that makes it credible. The difference between *this is what the text teaches* and *this is what I have lived* is detectable to the congregation, and it is the second statement that opens rather than merely informs.

For the purpose of this diagnostic, a "genuinely participatory assembly" means a gathered body where (a) the agenda is not controlled by one person, (b) multiple members contribute from their gifts without being assigned or scripted, and (c) the structure depends on member participation to function — it would visibly fail if members remained passive. A small-group discussion program embedded in a conventional church does not meet this floor. A home church, covenant cohort, or intentional gathering organized around all-member ministry does. The diagnostic question: *if no one showed up prepared to contribute, would the gathering fail?* If the structure would still function with passive attenders, it is not participatory assembly.

The specific lived-experience markers to look for:
- The facilitator can name specific gifts they have exercised within a participatory assembly and describe what happened in the body as a result
- The facilitator has experienced the disruption of moving *from* a passive gathering structure *to* a participatory one — they know what the transition costs and what it produces
- The facilitator can speak about both what worked and what was difficult in the participatory context, without idealizing or minimizing either

A facilitator who cannot speak to all three of these from personal experience is not ready for Stage 1.

## Biblical Grounding

The biblical criteria for the initial-stage facilitator do not look like a credential checklist. They look like character, Spirit-filling, wisdom, relational embeddedness, and a specific function.

**Acts 6:3 — Structure work requires Spirit-filled wisdom, not technical expertise.**
When the early church needed to address a structural inequity, the criterion was not administrative skill. It was *men of good repute, full of the Spirit and of wisdom.* Acts 6 addresses diaconal administration, not formation facilitation — the parallel is analogical, not direct. But the criteria themselves transfer with strong warrant: good repute (relational trust), Spirit-filling (the facilitator is themselves under formation), and wisdom (not cleverness, but the formed judgment that comes from walking with God through difficulty). (**Strong inference**, not direct parallel.)

**Ephesians 4:11–13 — The goal of equipping is a body that no longer needs the equipper.**
The equipping gifts exist not to perform ministry but to build the body into its own ministry until the body reaches the fullness of Christ. The qualified facilitator in Stage 1 is therefore the person who can make themselves unnecessary — who succeeds when the congregation no longer needs them to hold the participatory model in front of them, because the congregation has internalized it. A facilitator whose continuation is an indicator of success has misunderstood the function.

**Hebrews 5:12–14 — Solid food requires trained faculties.**
The writer of Hebrews distinguishes between those who need milk — teaching about the basic principles of the faith — and those who are ready for solid food, whose faculties have been trained by practice to distinguish good from evil. Helping a congregation hear and receive the ekklēsia model is solid food. Only a facilitator who themselves has trained faculties can hold that space. This is the primary biblical warrant for the lived-experience prerequisite.

**1 Corinthians 12–14 — The participatory model is a description of a body that has discovered and exercises its gifting.**
Paul does not describe a participatory assembly as a program to be implemented; he describes the body functioning as it was designed to function. The facilitator who presents this to a congregation that has not seen it must carry it as a reality they inhabit — not as an argument they are making. The congregation will receive it differently depending on which of those is true of the person in front of them.

**1 Peter 5:3 — *Typos*, not lording.**
The text addresses elders in established pastoral relationship with the flock under their care; applying it to an initial-stage facilitator is analogical, not direct. (**Analogical transfer** — hold with appropriate weight.) The principle transfers: the elder leads by being a formation reference — *ginomenoi typos tou poimniou*, becoming an example to the flock rather than lording over it. Applied analogically to the initial-stage facilitator: the congregation learns what participatory assembly looks like in part by watching how the facilitator carries themselves — whether they embody the non-anxious, gift-exercising, other-oriented posture they are describing. The facilitator's own formed life is part of the curriculum.

## Diagnostic Assessment: Five Questions for the Pastor

These questions are for the pastor assessing whether a specific candidate — themselves or another — is ready to hold Stage 1 with their congregation.

**1. Relational trust: Is this person already trusted by the people who will be in the room?**
If not, name the gap explicitly: *How large is it, and can it be bridged before they speak into the paradigm?* A gap that cannot be bridged is a disqualifier for Stage 1, not a problem to work around. The trust condition is upstream of the content — without it, the content cannot land.

**2. Lived experience: Can this person describe what participatory assembly has looked like in their own practice — as a participant, not a student of the model?**
Ask them directly: *Tell me about a time the body functioned participatorily. What were you doing? What happened?* If the answer is primarily textual or theoretical, they are not ready. If it is personal and specific, proceed to the next question.

**3. Framing check: When this person describes the passive gathering model, what language do they use?**
Listen for the difference between *loss and recovery* and *failure and correction*. If their natural language is corrective — if they assign fault, express frustration, or treat the passive model as something people should be embarrassed about — they will carry that posture into the room regardless of what they intend. Corrective framing disqualifies for Stage 1.

**4. Non-anxious presence: Can this person sit with resistance, silence, or pushback without pressing for resolution?**
Do not assess this by asking; observe it. Put the candidate in a real conversation that involves friction and watch what happens when someone pushes back. Does their response come from the content, or from the pressure in the room? Anxiety that is manageable in low-stakes settings will amplify in high-stakes ones. Only what is observed under real friction is reliable evidence.

**5. Accountability posture: Will this person operate under your authority — and stop when you say stop, without arguing that continuation is warranted?**
This question is best asked directly: *If I determine that this conversation needs to pause for a season, what will you do?* A person who begins strategizing for continuation, or who frames the question as a test of your vision, is not in the right posture. A person who simply says *I'll follow your lead* and means it is.

## Stage Boundary

This framework governs Stage 1 only. The competencies required for subsequent stages are different:

- **Stage 2** (congregants wrestling with implications): requires greater theological precision, capacity to facilitate congregational conflict, and a more active teaching role. The non-anxious presence and recovery posture remain, but the facilitator must now hold more complexity.
- **Stage 3** (implementation): requires organizational development competency, structural design knowledge, and sustained formation support across time.

Do not use Stage 3 qualifications to select a Stage 1 facilitator. The person who is right for Stage 1 may or may not be right for Stage 3, and importing a Stage 3 qualifier too early will skip over the formation work that makes Stage 3 possible.

**Transition criterion — when Stage 1 is complete:** Stage 1 is complete when congregants can articulate *why* the participatory model is normative — not merely describe what it is — and are asking questions about what to do rather than whether to engage. The transition from Stage 1 to Stage 2 is a pastoral judgment call, not a programmatic milestone. The pastor, in consultation with the facilitator, makes that call. The facilitator does not make it unilaterally.

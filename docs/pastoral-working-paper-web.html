Formation & Transition — Start Here

A Pastoral Working Paper

For any pastor who already holds this understanding, first — and then, with their review, for the small group walking through it with them.

Purpose

This paper is for anyone who already holds the premise it is built on — that formation, not mere attendance, is what a congregation actually needs; that a room can be physically gathered without being genuinely assembled; that the vocabulary here (formation, hexis, gathered-versus-assembled) is naming something real and consequential, not offering a passing framework to try on. If that premise already rings true to you, what you likely have not yet had is the whole thing laid out as one continuous argument, with the seams visible: which parts of this are things Scripture states outright, which parts are careful inference from what Scripture states, and which parts are explanatory tools borrowed from outside disciplines to help the inference make sense.

That distinction is not a hedge. It is the point of this paper. A framework this consequential — one that would ask a congregation to absorb years of cost or a leadership team to face its own gaps honestly — has to be trustworthy enough to build a future on. That means being exact about what kind of claim each piece is, not letting a good synthesis borrow the authority of a direct command it doesn't actually carry.

This paper does not resolve what comes next. It exists to put the whole shape in front of anyone who already shares this understanding, so that conversation can happen with the full picture in view, not fragments.

1Why This Exists

Most congregations in the West run on an inherited assumption that was never actually examined: that people come to church to receive something — a sermon, a worship experience, a program — and that if attendance holds and satisfaction is high, the church is doing its job. Dallas Willard called this pattern the "Great Omission": churches that get people to a decision about Christ and then quietly drop the second half of the Great Commission — "teaching them to observe all that I have commanded" (Matthew 28:19–20, ESV) — treating ongoing formation as optional rather than as the actual assignment.

The result is a congregation that can be full on Sunday and still not function as the body Scripture describes. Paul names this possibility directly: "when you come together it is not for the better but for the worse" (1 Corinthians 11:17, ESV). Physical gathering and genuine assembly are not the same event. A room can be occupied without being an ekklēsia — without the members actually carrying, feeling, and building one another up the way the New Testament assumes they will.

This paper exists to answer one question honestly: what would it actually take for a congregation to close that gap — and what part of the answer is something Scripture requires, versus something we've had to work out ourselves in order to explain how the requirement functions?

2The Chain — How Formation Becomes a Functioning Body

The track's research converges on a single explanatory sequence:

Paideia → hexis → regulatory capacity → sympatheia → the allēlōn functions becoming operable.

Read it in plain English first: sustained training shapes a person's settled disposition. That settled disposition is what lets a person stay steady rather than reactive when someone else's pain or dysregulation shows up. Staying steady is what allows the body's built-in capacity to feel-with one another to actually function. And only when that capacity is functioning can the New Testament's "one another" commands — bear one another's burdens, weep with those who weep, confess to one another — be lived rather than merely known.

Each link carries a different kind of theological weight. Naming which is which is not academic throat-clearing — it's what makes the chain trustworthy enough to build a congregation's future on. We'll take them one at a time.

Paideia — training is what maturity is made of

This is not one method among several for producing spiritual growth. It is Scripture's own vocabulary for what growth is.

"For everyone who lives on milk is unskilled in the word of righteousness, since he is a child. But solid food is for the mature, for those who have their powers of discernment trained by constant practice to distinguish good from evil."Hebrews 5:13–14, ESV

"For the moment all discipline [paideia] seems painful rather than pleasant, but later it yields the peaceful fruit of righteousness to those who have been trained by it."Hebrews 12:11, ESV

"For the grace of God has appeared, bringing salvation for all people, training us to renounce ungodliness and worldly passions, and to live self-controlled, upright, and godly lives..."Titus 2:11–12, ESV

Notice what Titus 2 does to this claim before it can be misused: the subject of "training" in that sentence is grace, not human effort. Formation, in the biblical sense, is something grace does in a person — not a self-improvement project a person undertakes to earn standing with God. This is Scripture's own vocabulary, stated as fact about how spiritual maturity comes to be, applicable to any believer in any era.

This is not a template. It is the floor.

Hexis — a bridge we've named as a bridge

Hebrews 5:14 says maturity belongs to those who have "their powers of discernment trained by constant practice." The Greek word behind "constant practice" is hexis — a term from Greek ethical philosophy (Aristotle, most precisely) for a settled disposition formed by repeated action, the kind of trained reflex that no longer requires conscious deliberation to operate correctly.

Scripture doesn't use the word "implicit" — no first-century text could. But the track's founding research names plainly what it's doing when it borrows that modern category to explain hexis: this is an interpretive bridge from an ancient ethical term to a modern psychological one, not a claim about first-century vocabulary. It is a well-supported bridge — Hebrews 5:14 is describing exactly the kind of pre-deliberative, practiced perception that "implicit" names — but it is a bridge we built, not a term Scripture itself supplies.

This is a template: the best available tool for explaining what the text describes, not itself revealed.

Regulatory capacity — naming what you already know

Here's the honest gap in the chain, and the one that most needs a name before it goes further: what allows a formed disposition to actually produce steadiness in the presence of someone else's pain or dysregulation? Neither Hebrews nor Aristotle supplies that connective language. We borrowed it from Edwin Friedman's work on non-anxious presence and family-systems leadership — the capacity to stay differentiated and steady when the people around you are anxious, rather than absorbing their anxiety or reacting against it.

You don't need Friedman's name to recognize what he's describing. Every pastor has felt both sides of this. Being near someone dysregulated costs you something — you brace, you manage them, and something in the relationship erodes even if nothing is said. Being near someone settled and gracious under real pressure does the opposite — you find yourself willing to say the true thing, and trust deepens rather than depletes. That difference is not a theory. It's something you already know from a hundred rooms you've been in.

This link is sourced, not scriptural.

It is corroborated by common experience — every pastor recognizes it — but it is not a phrase Scripture uses or a category Scripture supplies. It is the clearest place in the whole chain where a leader could reasonably ask, "where does the Bible say this?" and the honest answer is: it doesn't, in these words. What Scripture does say is what this capacity makes possible — which is the next link.

Sympatheia — not metaphor, design

"If one member suffers, all suffer together; if one member is honored, all rejoice together."1 Corinthians 12:26, ESV

Paul is not describing an ideal here. The verb is indicative — a statement of what already is, not an exhortation toward what should be. Verse 24 makes the same point from the other direction: "God has so composed the body." The body's capacity to feel what one member feels is not aspirational language. It is a structural claim about what the body of Christ already is, built in by design, whether or not any given congregation is currently able to exercise it.

This is not a template. It is design.

The allēlōn functions — commanded, not suggested

The New Testament contains over forty "one another" commands — love one another, bear one another's burdens, confess your sins to one another, weep with those who weep, stir one another up to love and good works. These are imperatives. By definition, a command is not optional; the only live question is whether it can actually be obeyed, or only performed.

1 Thessalonians 5:14 makes the operational demand explicit: "admonish the idle, encourage the fainthearted, help the weak, be patient with all" — four distinct presentations, four distinct responses. That kind of differentiated response requires a person who is simultaneously feeling another's state (sympatheia active) and stable within it (regulatory capacity holding). Neither failure mode — numbing the signal out, or flooding and withdrawing from it — produces what the command requires.

This is not a template. It is commanded.

3What Kind of Claim Is the Chain Itself?

Here is the question worth being precise about: is the five-step chain above something Scripture presents as a package, or is it something we built?

The honest answer is that it's built — and that doesn't make it less true, only a different kind of true. Scripture states, directly, that formation produces maturity; that the body is designed with built-in sympatheia; that the one-another commands are commanded. Nowhere does any single text lay out a five-step causal sequence connecting those three realities into one mechanism. What connects them is our synthesis — placing three things Scripture asserts into an explanatory relationship that shows why an unformed gathering cannot obey commands it sincerely wants to obey. That synthesis is the human scaffolding around something God designed. It is not the design itself.

This matters more than a footnote. If a congregation, or a leadership team, adopts this chain because the synthesis is compelling — because the research is careful and the argument holds together — without arriving at their own conviction, grounded in the Word itself, of why formation is necessary, they are building on borrowed commitment. Borrowed commitment does not survive what's coming. Scope creep, a more attractive competing framework, sustained institutional pressure, a few discouraging years — any of these will test whether a leader's conviction was actually anchored in Scripture or only in trust that this document's author did good work. The track has already named one failure mode plainly: "structure decouples from formation" — programs outrunning the formation substance that gave them life. There is a companion failure mode this paper needs to name with equal plainness:

Conviction decoupled from the Word — a leader executing a scaffolding he was persuaded by, rather than one he has personally understood Scripture to require.

Paul names what produces the kind of faith that holds: "faith comes from hearing, and hearing through the word of Christ" (Romans 10:17, ESV). Durable conviction is not produced by a well-built argument, however sound. It is produced by the Word itself doing something in the hearer. That is the reason the tier distinctions above are not throat-clearing — they are what allows you, and the group after you, to test each piece against Scripture directly rather than against this document's persuasiveness, and to arrive at your own settled "yes" instead of borrowing this paper's.

A settled person builds trust. A dysregulated person spends it — Scripture tells you why that's designed, not incidental. You can gather people faster than you can form them, but you cannot gather your way into a body. Only formed people can actually feel and carry one another. Both of those sentences are repeatable to an elder board without this paper in hand — and both point back to something in Scripture, not merely to this paper's argument.

4Why the Ekklēsia Must Govern, Not Just Precede

One more distinction the track has established needs to be in front of you before this goes further, because it changes what kind of process any structural response would need to be.

The claim is not that the Bible hands down a single structural blueprint every congregation must copy — it doesn't. The claim is narrower and more demanding: the biblical portrait of the ekklēsia establishes non-negotiables — about what the church is for, what constitutes it, what its non-negotiable practices are, and what its leaders must have — and any structural reform toward genuine formation must be governed by those non-negotiables throughout the process, not only consulted at the start. A reform that starts with Scripture and then evaluates every subsequent decision by effectiveness, momentum, or what other churches are doing has quietly stopped being governed by it, even if it began well.

This matters because most Western Protestant church structures were never actually built from the biblical ekklēsia in the first place. They were built for Christendom — a culture where the population was assumed to already be Christian, and the church's job was to maintain and serve that population, not form it from nothing. Lesslie Newbigin, returning to England after decades as a missionary in India, named the shock of this directly: the Western church had been so shaped by Christendom's assumptions that it had lost the capacity to see itself as operating in a mission field. That culture is gone. Most church structures were never updated to notice.

The track record confirms it. From roughly 1970 to 2000, Western Protestant churches ran a sustained wave of structural reform — seeker-sensitive churches, megachurches, the small-group movement, purpose-driven models. Each one asked "how do we make this more effective?" — a question that accepts the inherited frame and tries to optimize inside it. None asked "what does the ekklēsia Scripture describes actually require, and which of our assumptions are incompatible with it?" — the only question capable of producing something the inherited frame cannot produce on its own terms. The result, documented across each wave: vocabulary changed, structure didn't, and formation didn't follow.

What this means practically: a pastoral leadership team beginning this work needs more than agreement with the premise. It needs to be able to answer, with specificity, four questions — what this community is constituted to display; what actually constitutes it as ekklēsia rather than a voluntary gathering; which of its practices are structurally foreclosed rather than merely underdeveloped; and whether its own leadership has been through the formation this requires, or is being asked to lead people somewhere they haven't personally gone. That last question is the subject of the next section, and it's the one this track has not yet fully answered.

5Why Now — The Cultural Window

Two things are true at once, and both matter for timing.

First: several cultural forces are converging in ways that will generate real, articulate hunger for genuine community that consumer church structures cannot satisfy — AI companionship normalizing to the point that its absence of real presence becomes felt as deprivation; the visible ceiling of purely clinical responses to the loneliness and mental-health crisis; the loneliness epidemic reaching policy visibility; the ongoing, visible decline of the consumer-attractional church model. None of these individually produces the shift. Their convergence, on current trend lines, is estimated at ten to fifteen years out — a judgment call, not a derived figure, and it should be treated with the same caution any estimate deserves.

Second: a formed cohort — a small group of people who have actually gone through the formation this paper describes — takes an estimated five to seven years to produce, also an estimate, not a measured result. If the cultural window arrives in ten to fifteen years and the formation arc takes five to seven, the arithmetic argues for beginning now rather than waiting for demand to arrive before building the capacity to meet it. The Reformation is the cautionary case: demand for reform arrived before the infrastructure existed to receive it, and one of the reasons it fragmented into competing streams instead of cohering was that formation could not be produced on the timeline demand required.

None of this is a claim that the timing is certain. It is a case for beginning the slower work — leadership formation — before the faster-moving cultural pressure arrives, because formation cannot be compressed no matter when the moment comes.

6What Remains Unfinished — Said Plainly

A document that only tells you what has been resolved is not trustworthy. Here is what has not been.

No widely available pathway reliably produces the formation a leader would need before this could responsibly begin. The track assessed four real options against a specific standard — not "does the pastor believe this" but "has the pastor's actual disposition been formed and tested under real pastoral pressure." Traditional spiritual direction partially delivers, and only with an unusually well-formed director over several years. Covenant Identity coaching is the most mechanism-appropriate option that exists, but requires deliberate targeting of pastoral-specific patterns and a realistic 12–18 month minimum, with the formation tested under real pressure afterward — not merely completed. The 30-day Ignatian Exercises produce real interior movement but need sustained integration afterward to hold. Peer accountability groups, as typically structured, do not deliver this at all — they are corrective in orientation, not formative, and a peer cannot see what a peer shares the same blind spot on. The honest finding: this is the single most structurally dangerous open question in the whole track, and it remains open.

The question of what equips a formed leader to hold formation space for someone else — as distinct from being formed himself — has only begun to be answered. A first-stage framework exists for what a facilitator needs at the point of a first honest conversation. What a facilitator needs to hold a formation process over years has not yet been built.

Neither gap is a reason to set this paper aside. Both are the reason the next step, whatever it turns out to be, cannot start with structural change. It has to start with an honest answer to a question this track has already sharpened into something specific: not "do you believe this is true," but "where are you, personally, in relation to what this would require of you before you could lead anyone else into it."

7Where This Leaves Us

This paper has laid out the whole shape of what the track has established — what Scripture states outright, what we've built by careful inference, what we've borrowed from outside disciplines and named as borrowed, and where the real gaps still sit. What it has not done, and is not meant to do, is answer what comes next for this group specifically.

That's a live question, not a settled one — and it belongs to a conversation among the people in this room, not to a document written before that conversation happens. What this paper can tell you is that the shape is coherent, that the parts that are commanded really are commanded, and that the parts still open are named honestly rather than smoothed over. What it cannot tell you is what you, together, are going to do about it. That's the next conversation.

# Formation & Transition — Plurality, Multiplication & the Architecture of Formed Leadership

---

## Purpose

*Polity Foundation* established the mechanism: a leader's formed (or unformed) *hexis* is inside the body's *sympatheia* network and is registered and mirrored whether the leader intends it or not, with James 3:1's "greater strictness" naming the amplified, structural weight that position carries. That document deliberately stopped short of one question: **what does the formation mechanism imply about how many formed leaders a body should have, and what happens when a body has more formed people than it has seats?**

This document takes up that question. It is not a polity argument in the denominational sense — it does not resolve elder-led versus episcopal versus congregational government, and it inherits Polity Foundation's scope note that the mechanism operates identically regardless of what a tradition calls its leaders. What it argues is narrower and mechanism-specific: **concentrating pastoral function in one person, when a body contains more people whose *hexis* qualifies them for the function, is not a neutral organizational preference — it is a formation-capacity question**, with its own biblical pattern, its own observable failure mode in the contemporary Western church, and its own distinct difficulty when a leader tries to change it.

The document has three movements: (1) what the office's responsibilities actually are, read through the formation lens Polity Foundation already established; (2) the biblical pattern for what to do when formed *hexis* exceeds the available seats; (3) why agreement with that pattern does not produce the change, and what leading it actually requires.

---

## 1. The Office's Responsibilities, Read as Formation Functions

Polity Foundation noted that the NT does not describe a flat ecclesiology — every letter addressing congregational life assumes recognized *episkopoi*, *presbyteroi*, and *diakonoi*. What that document didn't unpack is the fourth term standing behind all three: **poimēn**, shepherd.

> **Language Note — *poimēn* / *poimainō* and the interchangeability of the three office-words**
> *Poimēn* (shepherd) and its verb *poimainō* (to shepherd, tend) carry the root metaphor of feeding, guiding, and protecting — and, critically, of *owning responsibility for the sheep's condition*, not merely directing them from outside. Acts 20 shows this term is not a fourth, separate office alongside overseer and elder — it is the functional description underneath both. Paul summons the Ephesian *presbyteroi* ("elders," v.17) and in the same address calls them *episkopoi* ("overseers," v.28) and charges them to *poimainein* ("shepherd") the flock. Titus 1:5–7 makes the same move: appoint *presbyterous* (elders, v.5), because an *episkopos* (v.7) must be blameless. Three words, one office, described from three angles — dignity (elder), guardianship (overseer), care (shepherd). This matters for what follows: when this document speaks of "elder-qualified" people, it means people whose formed *hexis* qualifies them for this single, three-named function — not for one of three distinct roles.

The office's responsibilities, gathered across the NT's pastoral texts, are formation functions, not administrative ones:

- **Shepherd and feed** — John 21:15–17; Acts 20:28; 1 Pet 5:2
- **Guard against false teaching and predatory influence** — Acts 20:29–30 ("fierce wolves will come in among you"); Titus 1:9b
- **Teach and hold sound doctrine** — 1 Tim 3:2; Titus 1:9a; 1 Tim 4:13
- **Manage well** (*proïstamenos* — see note below) — 1 Tim 3:4–5; 5:17
- **Model a formed life** — 1 Pet 5:3's *typos* language, already established in Polity Foundation §3
- **Equip the saints for the body's own ministry** — Eph 4:11–12
- **Exercise self-oversight before oversight of others** — 1 Tim 4:16; the Acts 20:28 "yourselves first" sequence Polity Foundation §2 already established
- **Answer to the Chief Shepherd** — 1 Pet 5:4; Heb 13:17

This list is not new information relative to Polity Foundation — it is that document's mechanism named at the level of job description. What is new is the historical-theological background behind it, and it matters for the argument that follows: shepherd was ANE royal language before it was pastoral language. Mesopotamian, Egyptian, and Israelite kings were routinely titled "shepherd of the people," and the image's abuse was a standing prophetic complaint — Ezekiel 34's indictment of shepherds who "feed themselves" rather than the flock, who don't seek the lost or bind up the injured (Ezek 34:2–4). When the NT applies this language to elders, it deliberately imports the indictment along with the office: the shepherd inherits royal-scale responsibility but is explicitly de-royalized (Mark 10:42–45, taken up in §3 below). **The office was never designed to concentrate significance in one person. It was designed to distribute care through however many formed people the flock's condition required — and Ezekiel's complaint is precisely against a shepherd who treats the flock as existing for him rather than the reverse.**

## 2. The Architecture Question: When Formed *Hexis* Exceeds the Seats

If the qualification lists (1 Tim 3, Titus 1) are formation diagnostics rather than a scarce credential — which is exactly what Polity Foundation §1 already established — then a body in which the formation mechanism is actually working should be expected, over time, to produce *more* people who meet the diagnostic than any single office needs. The question this section answers: does Scripture treat that outcome as a problem to be managed, or as the mechanism succeeding?

**Numbers 11:10–30 gives the clearest answer, and it comes in two parts.**

First, the change originates in the leader's own confession of limit, not an external verdict of his inadequacy: *"I am not able to carry all this people alone; the burden is too heavy for me"* (v.14, ESV) — Moses goes as far as asking to be put to death rather than continue (v.15). God's response is to distribute the Spirit that rested on Moses across seventy elders (v.25).

Second, and more directly on point: two men, Eldad and Medad, are not among the seventy gathered at the tent, yet the Spirit rests on them anyway and they prophesy in the camp. Joshua's instinct is to treat this as a problem — *"My lord Moses, stop them"* (v.28) — the unassigned-but-gifted read as a threat to the assigned structure. Moses' answer is the load-bearing line for this whole document:

> *"Are you jealous for my sake? Would that all the LORD's people were prophets, that the LORD would put his Spirit on them!"* (Numbers 11:29, ESV)

> **Language Note — the posture Numbers 11:29 establishes**
> Moses does not resolve the tension by bringing Eldad and Medad into the formal seventy, and the text does not record that he needed to. His response is a *posture* toward gifting that exceeds the formal structure: he wants more of it, not less, and he names the instinct to contain it (Joshua's) as a misplaced jealousy for Moses' own position rather than a legitimate concern for order. This is the interpretive anchor for reading every subsequent multiplication text below: **the presence of more formed people than a body's current offices require is not, in itself, a structural problem that Scripture treats as needing correction.**

**Exodus 18:13–27 supplies the structural mechanism Numbers 11 doesn't.** Before the seventy, Moses' father-in-law Jethro diagnoses an unsustainable single-office model — *"What you are doing is not good... you will certainly wear yourselves out, both you and the people with you, for the thing is too heavy for you"* (vv.17–18) — and proposes not a limit on who may serve, but tiers: rulers of thousands, hundreds, fifties, and tens (v.21), selected by the same character standard ("able men... who fear God, trustworthy, hating a bribe"). The wisdom move is architectural, not restrictive: when qualified people exceed a single level of structure, add levels scoped to the number of qualified people available, rather than forcing them all through one office or leaving the surplus unused.

**Acts 6:1–7 shows the same move inside the apostolic church, with a congregational selection mechanism added.** A real need (Hellenist widows neglected) exceeds what the apostles' existing office can absorb without distortion. Rather than absorbing the new demand personally, the Twelve diagnose the structural mismatch — *"It is not right that we should give up preaching the word of God to serve tables"* (v.2) — and hand the selection of seven men "full of the Spirit and of wisdom" to the congregation itself (v.3), not to their own appointment. The result is stated explicitly as growth, not merely relief: *"the word of God continued to increase, and the number of the disciples multiplied greatly"* (v.7).

**Acts 14:23 and Titus 1:5 establish that this isn't a special-case accommodation — plurality is the given baseline.** *"When they had appointed elders for them in every church"* (Acts 14:23) and *"appoint elders in every town"* (Titus 1:5) both assume multiple elders per local body as the ordinary pattern, not an unusual response to unusual abundance. This reframes the whole question: a body with many formed people isn't an anomaly requiring a special solution — it is the condition the NT assumes is normal, and the anomaly the NT actually worries about is scarcity, hence the qualification lists functioning as a brake against hasty appointment under pressure (1 Tim 5:22, "do not be hasty in the laying on of hands") rather than a ceiling on how many may serve.

**1 Timothy 5:17 adds internal differentiation within the plurality.** *"Let the elders who rule well be considered worthy of double honor, especially those who labor in preaching and teaching."*

> **Language Note — *proïstamenos* (1 Tim 5:17) and the "especially"**
> *Proïstamenos* — from *proïstēmi*, to stand before, to manage or govern — is household-management language, the same register 1 Tim 3:4–5 uses to qualify the office. The verse's "especially" (*malista*) marks a real distinction of function within a single office: not every elder who meets the qualification is doing, or needs to be doing, the same thing. Some are recognized specifically for teaching labor, others for governance. **Multiplying the number of recognized elders does not require multiplying identical roles.**

**Acts 13:1–3 shows the most direct precedent for an actual surplus of elder-level people in one body.** Antioch has multiple named prophets and teachers — Barnabas, Simeon, Lucius, Manaen, Saul (v.1) — more than one local body needs to function. The Spirit's resolution is not to give all five identical standing in a single Antioch eldership; it is to *send two of them out* to plant new gospel work while the rest continue at Antioch (vv.2–3). A body with more formed leaders than it needs is treated as a sending resource, not an inventory problem.

**2 Timothy 2:2 makes the multiplication expectation a direct command rather than a narrative pattern.** *"What you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses entrust to faithful men, who will be able to teach others also."*

> **Language Note — *paratithēmi* (2 Tim 2:2)**
> "Entrust" translates *paratithēmi* — to place beside, to deposit for safekeeping. It is the same verb Jesus uses on the cross: *"Father, into your hands I commit [paratithēmi] my spirit"* (Luke 23:46). The word carries the sense of a costly, trust-bearing transfer, not casual delegation. Paul's instruction to Timothy is explicitly a four-generation chain — Paul → Timothy → faithful men → others also — aimed at identifying capable people beyond the current teaching office and deliberately multiplying the function through them. This is the clearest command-form text (as opposed to the narrative precedents above) treating an abundance of qualified people as the intended output of formation, not an incidental surplus to be managed.

**Gathered, the pattern is not one mechanism but three, and they are not interchangeable:**

1. **Scale structure** (Exodus 18) — add tiers or scopes of oversight matched to the number of qualified people.
2. **Differentiate function** (Acts 6; 1 Tim 5:17) — create or recognize distinct roles so qualified people are deployed according to actual gifting rather than uniform title.
3. **Send outward** (Acts 13; 2 Tim 2:2) — treat surplus as the mechanism for planting new bodies and reproducing leadership elsewhere, not as inventory held at the sending body.

The posture underneath all three, stated plainly in Numbers 11:29, is anti-scarcity: the wrong instinct is guarding the office; the right instinct is wanting the *hexis* to spread. Read against Polity Foundation's mechanism, this has a specific formation-capacity meaning: **every formed person whose hexis is not being deployed into the sympatheia network at the leadership level is a formation resource the body is leaving idle** — the same category of loss Polity Foundation §4 identifies when an *unformed* leader is inside the network, just from the opposite direction.

## 3. What This Predicts — and What the Western Church Landscape Shows

> **Epistemic status**
> This section is sociological and diagnostic, not exegetical. It describes observable institutional patterns and names real critique literature from within the traditions concerned. It should be weighted as informed synthesis, not as a claim with the same evidentiary status as the exegesis above — consistent with how this track has flagged design judgments elsewhere (e.g., the Development Plan's §5 working-assumption flag).

If §2's pattern is correct, a Western church culture that has drifted from it should show a specific, predictable shape: institutional structures that gate formed people out rather than multiply them in. That is broadly what the landscape shows.

**Concentration, not multiplication, is the dominant model.** The majority of Western Protestant churches — especially American evangelical and non-denominational — run on a de facto single-pastor model even where a nominal "elder board" exists; the board typically functions as an oversight check on one paid professional who holds the actual teaching, vision, and pastoral-care center of gravity. This is a direct inversion of Acts 14:23 / Titus 1:5's plural baseline, and it is not a fringe observation — it is a well-documented critique within evangelical polity literature, and it has driven a real reform push within Baptist and non-denominational evangelicalism over roughly the last twenty-five years, precisely because practitioners inside those traditions recognized the mismatch.

**The qualification bar has been re-professionalized into a credentialing bar.** 1 Tim 3 and Titus 1 qualify by character — blameless, self-controlled, household-managed, able to teach — available in principle to any mature disciple without institutional credentials. Western practice has substantially replaced this with seminary degree, denominational ordination process, and paid staff status as the practical gate. The effect: people who meet the biblical (formation) qualification but lack institutional credentials are treated as ineligible rather than as the multiplication capital Numbers 11 and 2 Timothy 2:2 assume they are. Read against §2, this is the single largest structural departure from the pattern — an instinct toward scarcity-gating rather than the abundance-multiplying posture Moses models.

**Attractional scaling substitutes for Exodus 18's tiering without delivering plurality.** Multisite and video-venue models do add structural layers to absorb growth, which superficially resembles Jethro's tiers. But critique from within ecclesiology-focused circles argues this scales the *platform*, not the *plurality* — it extends one gifted communicator's reach via screen rather than raising up and sending out additional, independently-eldered bodies in the Acts 13 pattern. It is franchise logic wearing multiplication's clothes.

**Denominational variance is real and worth naming precisely, since it bears on where a given pastor's starting resistance will come from:**

- **Presbyterian/Reformed** polity is the closest formal match — the session is plural by design, and the ruling-elder/teaching-elder distinction directly implements 1 Tim 5:17's differentiation. Even here, the teaching elder (the paid pastor) often functionally dominates the session in practice, so formal structure and functional reality diverge.
- **Baptist/congregational** tradition has historically run single-pastor-plus-deacons, where deacons absorb the Acts 6 practical-service function but the pastor alone carries the Acts 20 / 1 Peter 5 shepherding function — the tradition where the plurality-reform movement above has gained the most traction, precisely because the mismatch is most visible.
- **Anglican/Catholic/Orthodox** traditions have fully institutionalized the monoepiscopacy development flagged as a genuine post-NT development in *Polity Foundation*'s companion research; surplus gifting is channeled into formal ordination tracks or religious orders with a high credentialing bar rather than lay-elder recognition.
- **Pentecostal/Charismatic** streams influenced by fivefold-ministry readings of Eph 4:11 recognize gifted leaders more fluidly, which in principle absorbs surplus gifting more readily — but documented critique from within the movement shows this can just as easily concentrate unaccountable authority in a single charismatic figure, trading one bottleneck for another rather than achieving genuine distribution.

**A minority corrective exists.** House-church and simple-church networks, and church-planting movements organized explicitly around multiplication, attempt to recover the Acts 13 / 2 Timothy 2:2 pattern directly, treating leadership development and sending as the default expectation rather than an exception. These remain a minority relative to the dominant institutional model, and their own track record is mixed — multiplication-focused networks sometimes under-invest in the character-formation rigor 1 Timothy 3 assumes, trading the scarcity problem for the hasty-appointment problem 1 Tim 5:22 exists to prevent.

**Diagnosis:** the consistent pattern is not usually a theological rejection of §2's texts — most Western pastors would affirm plurality and multiplication if asked directly. The gap is institutional incentive: paid-staff budget models need role scarcity to justify structure; denominational credentialing protects doctrinal control and reduces liability exposure; attractional models need a bottleneck personality to sustain the platform. The biblical pattern is affirmed in principle and quietly overridden by the incentives the institution actually runs on. This is the exact shape of a well-documented organizational-culture problem: the espoused value (we should have plurality) survives while the basic, unexamined assumption (the pastor is the indispensable center) governs actual behavior.

## 4. Leading the Change: Why Agreement Doesn't Produce Multiplication

Section 3's diagnosis implies the actual difficulty is not informational. A pastor who can articulate §2's texts accurately and still runs a concentrated model is not lacking knowledge — which means the intervention required is not better teaching. Scripture itself models a different kind of process for this specific change, and it converges with the change-leadership literature already partially seated in this track's evidence base.

**The change originates in the leader's own naming of unsustainability, not an external verdict of failure.** Numbers 11:14–15 (Moses' confession, §2 above) sequences this precisely: distributed leadership follows the incumbent's own acknowledgment of limit. A pastor is far more likely to move toward plurality from his own naming of unsustainability than from being told by a board or a consultant that his model is broken.

**An external, non-rival voice can name what the leader cannot see himself — but the proposal still requires his voluntary uptake.** Exodus 18's structure matters as much as its content: Jethro is not a rival for Moses' position (father-in-law, not a subordinate or contender), which is plausibly why the diagnosis lands, and the text is explicit that Moses chose it: *"So Moses listened to the voice of his father-in-law and did all that he had said"* (v.24). This is close to the actual position an external facilitator occupies in this track's own model (see *Facilitator Competency — Initial Stage*, Case B) — credible and non-competing, naming pattern and proposing structure, not mandating it.

**Selection can be handed to the body rather than imposed from above, and this is not incidental to Acts 6's success.** The apostles diagnosed the structural mismatch but delegated the actual choosing to the congregation (*"pick out from among you seven men,"* v.3) rather than appointing top-down. Multiplication that is imposed and multiplication that is congregationally selected are structurally different interventions, and Acts 6 explicitly chose the latter — worth weighing directly against the well-documented finding that adoption spreads through observed behavior within trusted networks rather than through instruction.

**A leader can model departure and trust rather than indispensability.** Paul at Miletus explicitly prepares the Ephesian elders for his absence, naming the coming danger (*"fierce wolves will come in among you," v.29*) and then deliberately releasing oversight rather than lingering to control it: *"I commend you to God and to the word of his grace"* (v.32). This is the clearest model of a leader who could have made himself indispensable choosing instead to transfer ownership before being forced to — directly relevant where the change target is a pastor whose identity is entangled with being the irreplaceable center.

**The resistance is frequently identity, not structure, and Scripture names this directly rather than treating it as a scheduling problem.** Jesus confronts the disciples' status competition not as an organizational question but as a desire for greatness misdirected — *"whoever would be great among you must be your servant... For even the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve"* (Mark 10:43,45) — in the immediate context of his own self-emptying toward the cross. John 3:30's *"He must increase, but I must decrease"* compresses the required posture into a single line, useful less as an argument than as a coaching anchor once the identity issue has actually been surfaced.

**Reframing precedes behavior change, not the reverse.** 1 Corinthians 12:14–26 doesn't open with a behavioral rebuke of the Corinthians' status-seeking around visible gifts — Paul re-narrates the body's interdependence first (*"the parts of the body that seem to be weaker are indispensable," v.22*), with the behavioral instruction of chapter 14 following only after the identity reframe is laid.

### Where this converges with the track's existing change-leadership sources

- **Adaptive leadership (already in the Practitioner Curriculum Source Library):** distributing pastoral function is an *adaptive* challenge, not a technical one — it requires changed values and loyalties in the congregation, not a better organizational chart. A pastor who treats it as technical (announce a new elder structure, add names to a board) will produce activity without the underlying shift and will exhaust the congregation without closing the gap.
- **Differentiated, non-anxious presence (already in the Source Library):** the pastor's own steadiness is the actual lever, not additional argument or information. A pastor whose stated theological agreement with plurality sits on top of an anxious system — one that reacts to any redistribution of visible role as a threat — will not produce durable change by teaching the texts more clearly; this frame explains why the resistance persists after the theology is settled.
- **Immunity to change (already registered in the cross-track Source Library):** a pastor's stated commitment ("I want plurality") typically sits against a hidden competing commitment (protecting significance, control, or platform) resting on an unexamined big assumption (if I am not central, I am not needed or valuable). This is the identical diagnostic machinery this track's coaching-adjacent work already applies to individuals, run here on a single leader embedded in a congregational system rather than in an individual coaching relationship — worth flagging precisely because it is the same mechanism, not an analogy to it.
- **A newly introduced source, not yet catalogued in the Curriculum Source Library:** addresses the same identity-entanglement dynamic named above, but in explicitly pastoral vocabulary rather than requiring translation from secular organizational-development literature — potentially useful as a bridge text for a pastor who would resist a more corporate-sounding framing. Whether this earns a formal Source Library entry is a decision still pending; it is used here as a directional pointer, not a cited authority on the same footing as the others.

**The convergence, stated once:** the biblical texts and the organizational-change literature independently locate the obstacle in the same place. Pastors mostly know §2's texts. The resistance is identity and loss, not information — Exodus 18 and Numbers 11 sequence the change through the leader's own naming of limit before structure follows; Mark 10 and the differentiated-presence literature both locate resistance in identity/status rather than logistics; 1 Corinthians 12 and the adaptive-leadership literature both require reframing (of belonging, of value) to precede or accompany the structural ask rather than follow it as an afterthought.

## 5. What This Document Does Not Resolve

**It does not specify a target ratio or structure.** Nothing here says how many elders a given body should have, what tier structure (Exodus 18's fifties/hundreds/thousands has no direct congregational analogue), or whether a given surplus of formed people should be absorbed locally (differentiated function), sent out (church planting), or both. That is a case-by-case design decision downstream of this document, not a formula it produces.

**It does not address how to assess whether a candidate's "elder-qualified" status is genuine rather than aspirational.** This document assumes the qualification has actually been met in the sense Polity Foundation's diagnostic reading of 1 Tim 3 / Titus 1 requires — formation evidence, not self-report. The assessment question itself belongs to *Pastoral Formation Pathway Assessment* and the still-open Leader Formation Prerequisite Assessment question, not to this document.

**It does not resolve the sourcing question for the newly introduced pastoral-vocabulary text named in §4.** That source is introduced provisionally and should not be treated as a track-wide catalogued source until that decision is made explicitly.

**It does not carry independent authority for the Western-landscape diagnosis in §3.** That section is synthesis, clearly flagged as such, and should be weighted accordingly if it is ever cited in a document written for an actual pastor or pastoral group — the exegetical claims in §2 and the diagnostic claims in §3 are not the same order of claim, and folding them together in downstream use would overstate §3's evidentiary status.

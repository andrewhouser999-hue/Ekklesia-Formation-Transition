Would That All the Lord's People Were Prophets

For the pastor chairing this, and for the group carrying it with him.

Purpose

You've read the Working Paper. You know the shape of it now — training forms disposition, disposition makes steadiness possible, steadiness lets the body actually feel-with itself, and only then can the "one another" commands be lived instead of merely known. That paper told you plainly what it wasn't going to do: it wasn't going to answer what comes next for your group specifically. That's still true. This paper isn't the answer either.

But there's a piece of what's next that's already alive in your own conversations, even before you've had occasion to name it outright — something about not carrying this alone, about more than one of you being formed enough to hold it, about what it would look like for this not to depend on any single person in the room. This paper exists to give that instinct a name, a text, and an honest account of what it will cost you to actually live it.

1The Ache Has a Name Before It Has an Answer

Every pastor who has actually seen the gap — not just heard about it, seen it, the way you have — eventually runs into the same exhaustion: the sense that you are the only one holding the whole shape of it, that there's no one else in the room who sees what you see the way you see it, that even other pastors don't share the vocabulary. That isn't a personal failing. It's a condition Scripture names directly, in someone who had far more reason to feel it than you do.

"I am not able to carry all this people alone; the burden is too heavy for me. If you will treat me like this, kill me at once, if I find favor in your sight, that I may not see my wretchedness."Numbers 11:14–15, ESV

That's Moses, not exaggerating for effect — asking to die rather than keep carrying it alone. God's answer isn't a pep talk. It's a redistribution: the same Spirit resting on Moses is given to seventy others, so the weight is no longer his to carry by himself (Num 11:16–17, 25).

Then something happens that's easy to read past. Two of the seventy — Eldad and Medad — aren't at the tent when the Spirit comes. They're back in the camp. And the Spirit rests on them anyway, and they prophesy right there, outside the structure that was supposed to contain it. Joshua's instinct is to shut it down: "My lord Moses, stop them" (v.28) — too much, unauthorized, not part of the seventy. Moses' answer is the whole point of this paper:

"Are you jealous for my sake? Would that all the LORD's people were prophets, that the LORD would put his Spirit on them!"Numbers 11:29, ESV

Moses doesn't fold Eldad and Medad into the formal seventy. He doesn't need to. What he does is refuse the instinct to contain the gifting to the structure that currently exists — and names Joshua's protectiveness for what it is: not a concern for order, but a misplaced jealousy over Moses' own position.

This is not a template for how to organize seventy elders. It is the pattern Scripture gives for the exact feeling you're carrying — and it says the fix was never "find a way to bear it alone better." It was always: want more people to carry what you're carrying, and don't be threatened when the Spirit shows up in someone who wasn't standing where you expected.

2More Than One Node Is the Design, Not an Accommodation

If your group's conversation keeps circling something like "more nodes, different meaning-spaces, not just one center" — that instinct doesn't need a new structure invented for it. It needs recognition that this is the New Testament's ordinary practice, not a workaround for unusually gifted congregations.

Before the seventy elders even existed as a solution, the problem had already been correctly diagnosed by someone outside Moses' own head:

"Moses' father-in-law said to him, 'What you are doing is not good. You will certainly wear yourselves out, both you and the people with you, for the thing is too heavy for you. You are not able to do it alone.'"Exodus 18:17–18, ESV

Jethro's fix wasn't to tell Moses to work smarter, and it wasn't to limit who could serve. It was to build tiers — leaders of thousands, hundreds, fifties, tens (v.21) — scaled to the number of trustworthy people available, not to a fixed idea of how many leaders a nation "should" have. And Moses' response is worth sitting with: "So Moses listened to the voice of his father-in-law and did all that he had said" (v.24). The solution came from someone who wasn't competing with him for the role, and he took it.

By the time the church exists, this has become the assumed default, not the exception:

"And when they had appointed elders for them in every church, with prayer and fasting they committed them to the Lord in whom they had believed."Acts 14:23, ESV

Every church. Not the large ones, not the ones fortunate enough to have surplus gifted people. Titus 1:5 gives Titus the same instruction for Crete — "appoint elders in every town." Multiple formed leaders per body was never the special case requiring justification. One leader carrying it alone was.

And multiplying leaders doesn't mean flattening them into identical roles. Paul tells Timothy: "Let the elders who rule well be considered worthy of double honor, especially those who labor in preaching and teaching" (1 Tim 5:17, ESV) — a real distinction of gift and labor inside a single, plural office. More nodes doesn't mean everyone doing the same thing. It means the weight isn't resting on one person's shoulders by default.

3The Wolves Are Already Named — and One Node Is More Exposed, Not Less

You've already named the harder truth underneath this: that some of what threatens a body isn't outside it. It looks formed. It isn't. Paul named the same thing to the Ephesian elders, in the same breath as he handed them the shepherding task:

"Pay careful attention to yourselves and to all the flock, in which the Holy Spirit has made you overseers, to care for the church of God... I know that after my departure fierce wolves will come in among you, not sparing the flock; and from among your own selves will arise men speaking twisted things."Acts 20:28–30, ESV

From among your own selves. Not an outside threat you can screen for at the door. A pattern that grows inside people who already look like leaders.

Here's what a single-node structure actually does with that danger: it removes the only mechanism that could catch it early. If one person is carrying the whole formation load, there is no second formed voice in the room positioned to name a drift in that person without it costing them everything — their position, their income, the relationships built around their role. A plurality doesn't just spread the labor. It's the only structure in which a wolf arising "from among your own selves" can actually be seen and named by someone who isn't structurally dependent on the person it's naming. One node carrying it all isn't the safer, more controlled version of this work. It's the more exposed one — exposed precisely where you already sense the danger is real.

But notice why one node is more exposed, because the reason isn't just that you'd have less time to watch for it. It's that a wolf's pattern shows up first in the moments you structurally cannot be present for: how someone treats the person who just told them no, how they carry themselves toward the one below them with no way to retaliate if they're mistreated. You, standing at the center, mostly see the public and upward-facing version of anyone under your care. Your congregants don't. They sit beside each other in ordinary, unwitnessed relationships all week — which means a formed body doesn't just add labor capacity to the watching. It adds a kind of sight you were never positioned to have on your own, no matter how attentive you are.

It's also worth being honest that the qualifications Paul gives for elders were never only a doctrine test. The same letter that tells Titus to "appoint elders in every town" (Titus 1:5) immediately lists what to look for — and it isn't only "holding firm to the trustworthy word" (1:9), it's temperament: "not arrogant, not quick-tempered... not violent... but hospitable, a lover of good, self-controlled" (1:7–8). Scripture already assumes that sound teaching and safe character are two different things that have to be checked separately — a person can be doctrinally exact and still be the wolf Paul just warned about two verses later. A congregation trained to ask only "does he preach it right" will keep missing exactly what Acts 20 says to watch for. A congregation trained to also ask "how does he treat the person who can't do anything for him, and what happens when he's actually corrected" is asking the question Scripture asks, not a modern substitute for it — and those two questions are the most transferable thing you can actually hand your people. They don't require special training to notice. They require permission to notice, which is the thing a single-node structure quietly withholds simply by never distributing the watching in the first place.

There is a failure that runs the other direction, and it exposes a single node just as badly. Not everyone who makes the room uncomfortable is a wolf. Scripture's own test was never difficulty — it was concealment and fruit revealed over time (Matt 7:15–20). Someone who criticizes badly, withholds giving to register disapproval, or keeps a private ledger of every misstep is genuinely hard to shepherd, but hard to shepherd is not the same danger as hidden and exploitative — one widely used pastoral guide calls this ordinary, non-predatory category a dragon: draining, narrow, sometimes adversarial, but never concealing personal gain the way a wolf does (Marshall Shelley, Well-Intentioned Dragons). Collapsing the two categories has its own well-documented mechanism: a leader whose authority is questioned denies the charge, discredits whoever raised it, and reverses who looks like the injured party — observed closely enough across real cases that it has a name in that literature, DARVO. Calling someone a wolf can be exactly right. It can also be that reversal, dressed in the same vocabulary this paper just gave you.

That is the second reason one node is the more exposed structure, not the safer one. If you are the only formed voice in the room, you are also the only one deciding whether the person currently making your position uncomfortable is a wolf or someone you should be listening to — and you are deciding it about yourself, under exactly the pressure Joshua felt on Moses' behalf. A second formed voice doesn't just help catch a wolf arising from among your own selves (v.30) sooner. It is the only structure in which are we sure this is a wolf, and not someone we should be hearing gets asked by someone whose own position isn't the thing riding on the answer.

4Multiplication Has a Mechanism, Not Just a Mandate

Wanting more formed people isn't enough on its own — Scripture doesn't leave it at a feeling. It gives concrete mechanisms, and it's worth being honest about which of these are direct instruction and which are patterns drawn from what the early church actually did.

This one is a command, not just a pattern.

Paul tells Timothy:

"...what you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses entrust to faithful men, who will be able to teach others also."2 Timothy 2:2, ESV

Four generations deep in a single sentence — Paul to Timothy to faithful men to others again. The word for "entrust" (paratithēmi) is the same word Jesus uses on the cross committing his spirit to the Father — a costly, deliberate handoff, not a casual delegation. This is Scripture instructing a leader to actively look for people beyond his own reach and place the work in their hands on purpose.

These next two are narrative pattern, not direct command — but they're consistent enough to trust as a shape, not a coincidence.

When the apostles found themselves overwhelmed by a need they couldn't absorb without abandoning their primary task, they didn't try to do more themselves. They named the mismatch honestly — "it is not right that we should give up preaching the word of God to serve tables" (Acts 6:2) — and handed the actual choosing to the congregation: "pick out from among you seven men of good repute, full of the Spirit and of wisdom" (v.3). The result: "the word of God continued to increase, and the number of the disciples multiplied greatly" (v.7). And when the church at Antioch had more than one formed prophet and teacher than it needed to function — Barnabas, Simeon, Lucius, Manaen, Saul, named together in Acts 13:1 — the Spirit's answer wasn't to give all five equal standing in one local eldership. It was to send two of them out to start something new, while the rest continued at Antioch (13:2–3).

Three concrete moves, not one: scale the structure to the number of qualified people (Exodus 18), differentiate the function so gifted people are used according to their actual gift rather than one uniform title (Acts 6; 1 Tim 5:17), and send some out rather than holding every formed person at the center (Acts 13; 2 Tim 2:2). Which of these your group needs depends on what you actually have — but the default posture behind all three is the same one Moses names in Numbers 11:29: want the multiplication, don't manage it down to a size that feels safe to control.

5What This Will Actually Cost You

Here is the part not to soften. Agreeing that Scripture wants more formed leaders is not the same thing as being ready to become one voice among several instead of the center one. Those are different commitments, and the gap between them is exactly where most reform efforts quietly stall without anyone naming why.

Jesus confronts this directly, and not as an abstract virtue — as a live argument among his own closest followers about who would be greatest:

"But it shall not be so among you. But whoever would be great among you must be your servant, and whoever would be first among you must be slave of all. For even the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many."Mark 10:43–45, ESV

John the Baptist names the posture in five words, once his own disciples start drifting toward Jesus instead of him: "He must increase, but I must decrease" (John 3:30, ESV). Not grief. Not resignation. A settled statement of what his role was actually for.

This is where the Working Paper's honest gap — the one it named as sourced, not scriptural — becomes personal rather than theoretical. Regulatory capacity, the ability to stay non-anxious rather than reactive when something threatens your position, is exactly what's being asked of you if your group actually moves toward more than one formed node. Every pastor recognizes both sides of this from the inside: being near someone who braces and manages when their centrality is questioned costs the relationship something, even if nothing is said aloud; being near someone settled enough to actually want another formed voice trusted the way they trust themselves does the opposite — it makes room rather than closing it.

So the honest question for you, personally, before your group designs anything: not "do I believe more of us should carry this," but "what in me would resist another formed leader being trusted the way I am currently trusted — and would I know it if that resistance showed up disguised as a concern for order, the way Joshua's did?"

6Where This Leaves You and Your Group

This paper doesn't resolve what your group does with it. That's not a hedge — it's the same honesty the Working Paper insisted on, and it holds here too. What this paper can tell you: the ache for more nodes isn't a distraction from the formation work; it's where the formation work was always headed. What it can't tell you is which of you is actually ready to be trusted the way you currently trust yourself alone, and which of you would need to go through more of your own formation first before that trust would hold under real pressure.

That's not a question this paper can answer for your group. It's the next conversation — the one Numbers 11:29 hands back to you: do you want more of you, or does some part of you need to stay the only one who sees it?

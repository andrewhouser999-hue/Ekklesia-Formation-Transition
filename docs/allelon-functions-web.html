The Allēlōn Functions

What Active Operation Requires

Covenant Identity — Assembled, Not Attending establishes that physical gathering does not constitute genuine ekklēsia. A congregation can be co-located, performing the liturgy, even calling what they do "the Lord's Supper," while Paul says their gathering is "not for the better but for the worse" (1 Corinthians 11:17). The container — the physical fact of assembly — and the content — what the body actually does when it is functioning — are not the same thing. That document names the allēlōn functions as one of the prerequisites of genuine assembly: the 40+ "one another" commands of the New Testament represent the behavioral content of a body doing what bodies do. Where they are absent, the gathering may be many things, but it is not yet ekklēsia in Paul's sense.

This document takes the next step. It asks the question the companion document raises but does not answer in depth: what does it actually take for those functions to operate? It is possible to have members who are genuinely present, who genuinely want the body to function, in a gathering where the allēlōn functions still fail to execute — not from bad will or theological ignorance, but from structural conditions that prevent completion, or from the gap between having something to give and actually giving it. The allēlōn commands are not character descriptions. They are transaction descriptions. They exist only as events that have occurred between specific persons.

This document is written for leaders and practitioners who are working to understand what genuine assembly actually requires — not to critique what is, but to build toward what the New Testament describes.

As with its companion, boxes marked Language Note carry original-language precision and can be skipped without losing the argument.

1What "Actively Operating" Rules Out

Before building a positive account, the phrase requires unpacking — because several things are commonly mistaken for the allēlōn functions operating, and none of them are.

Being an encouraging person

A member may be genuinely characterized by encouragement as a trait. That characterization does not constitute parakalontes heautous (Heb 10:25 — "encouraging one another") having occurred. The allēlōn commands are not virtue claims about character types. They name actions that must be completed: one person acts toward another person who receives it. A gathering of encouraging people who have not actually encouraged anyone specific has not generated parakaleō functioning. The function exists only as a completed event.

Feeling encouraged by content

A member may leave a gathering genuinely strengthened — moved by the sermon, stirred by the singing, met by the Scripture. This is real, and it is not nothing. But it is a one-directional experience of a communicator's output. Parakalontes heautous — "encouraging one another" — requires an agent and a recipient within the congregation, at the member-to-member level. The vector is lateral, not vertical. A room full of people who felt encouraged by the platform, but who did not encourage and were not encouraged by other members, has not generated the function the text names.

Language Note — parakalontes heautous and the paraklētos connection

"Encouraging one another" translates parakalontes heautous — present participle of parakaleō (to call alongside) + heautous (reflexive: "each other"). The present participle marks this as ongoing, habitual action — a mode of common life, not a moment in a service.

Paraklētos — the Spirit's title in John 14–16 (Advocate, Helper, Comforter) — is from the same root. The word family carries pneumatological gravity: the Spirit's defining work is parakaleō — calling alongside, strengthening, interceding. When members execute parakalontes heautous, they participate in what the Spirit does by constitution. The body's mutual encouragement is a Spirit-mediated function. Its absence is not merely a warmth deficit — it is a suppression of the Spirit's ordinary mechanism in the body.

Unexpressed internal response

A member may genuinely feel the state of another member — register that someone is struggling, carry concern for them through the week. If that response remains interior and unexpressed, no allēlōn function has operated. The body's sympatheia mechanism (1 Cor 12:26 — the body's designed felt-with response to its members' states) may have fired. But registration is not the same as response. The circuit requires one more step: the registered state must produce action toward the person.

Group aspiration without individual transaction

A congregation may genuinely value encouragement, burden-bearing, confession — as culture statements, as identity claims. "We are a community where people are known." That aspiration is not the same as Person A having actually borne the burden of Person B in this gathering. The allēlōn commands are always particular: a specific person acts toward a specific person in a specific moment. A congregational culture of encouragement cannot substitute for the transactions the commands require.

2What Active Operation Requires — Four Conditions

For any allēlōn function to be operating, four conditions must be present. Most discussions of community and belonging address the first two and assume the third and fourth. The New Testament gives attention to all four.

Condition 1 — Registration: the other's state must land

For a response to occur, the need must first be known. Someone must register that another member is discouraged, burdened, disordered, weak, grieving, or sinning. This requires the body's sympatheia mechanism to be functioning — the felt-with design to actually receive the signal another member is transmitting.

Registration fails in two modes, both described in Covenant Identity — Assembled, Not Attending:

Numbing — the signal is screened out. The defended interior of the potential responder does not allow another's state to land. They leave unaware that anyone in the room was struggling.

Flooding — the signal overwhelms. The would-be responder feels the other's state but cannot hold it without being destabilized. They withdraw, avoid, or react — which confirms to the struggling member that their state is intolerable to others.

Neither mode produces registration that leads to action. What registration requires is an interior stable enough to receive the signal without dismissing or being swept by it. This stability is a formed capacity — the fruit of sustained discipline that has reshaped the implicit level of response.

Condition 2 — Carrying: the responder must arrive with something to give

"When you come together, each one has a hymn, a lesson, a revelation, a tongue, or an interpretation."1 Corinthians 14:26, ESV

Paul's description of a functioning assembly assumes that every member arrives already carrying something for others — not waiting to see what they will receive before deciding whether to contribute. The gift has already been entrusted to them (1 Pet 4:10 — "as each has received a gift") and their responsibility as oikonomoi (household stewards) is to manage what has already been placed in their care. The oikonomos (steward) does not own the resource; it belongs to the household and is held on behalf of its members.

Language Note — oikonomos (1 Pet 4:10)

Oikonomos — household manager, steward. In the Greco-Roman world, a trusted slave or freedman who managed a wealthy household's affairs — controlled finances, supervised other servants, distributed resources. He had significant delegated authority but zero ownership. Everything he managed belonged to the kyrios (master). Applied to gifts in 1 Pet 4:10: the gift was given to you, but it belongs to the household. Passive receipt of assembly — coming to consume rather than to steward — is a category error about ownership.

Condition 3 — Action: something must be done toward a specific person

This condition is where the gap between character and transaction is most visible. It requires that a member do something — speak, move toward, ask, pray, support, confess, correct, serve — toward a specific other person. Not mentally. Not in principle. Not as a generalized posture. Actually, physically, specifically.

Language Note — hekastos echei (1 Cor 14:26)

"Each one has" translates hekastos echei — hekastos is the distributive singular: not "most have" or "some have" or "the gifted have," but each individual one has. Paul's description of a functioning assembly is radically egalitarian about participation: every member, without exception, arrives as a contributor. The failure of any single member to function reduces the body's output. The contrast with a passive-recipient model is explicit in the grammar.

Condition 4 — Reception: the circuit must close

An allēlōn function extended but blocked is incomplete. Reception is the fourth required condition — and the one least within the control of the giving member. Reception fails when the recipient is too defended to receive what is extended, when shame around the need prevents acknowledging it has been met, or when the relationship between giver and receiver lacks the depth that makes the reception possible.

This is not a failure of the giver's formation alone. It is a function of whether the relationship has enough history to make genuine receipt safe. Which brings the argument to the prerequisite that underlies all four conditions.

3The Prerequisite Behind All Four Conditions — Knowing One Another

The allēlōn functions are, in their majority, impossible to execute toward people you do not know.

This claim is more precise than it first sounds. Walk through the commands:

You cannot paramytheomai (console) the oligopsychos (fainthearted) if you do not know they are fainthearted. You cannot antechomai (hold up) the asthenos (weak) if you cannot see their weakness. You cannot bear someone's burden (Gal 6:2) if they have not disclosed the burden. You cannot confess to one another (Jas 5:16) without prior relational safety. You cannot pray for one another (Jas 5:16) specifically — for the actual named need — without knowing the need. You cannot noutheteō (admonish) someone's disorder if you have no standing in their life and no relational history to give the correction weight rather than offense.

Language Note — proskarterountes (Acts 2:42) and the time requirement

The Acts 2 community is described as "devoting themselves" (proskarterountes) to the apostles' teaching, fellowship, breaking of bread, and prayers. The present participle is durative — a sustained habitual structure of common life, not an event they attended. The daily contact (kath' hēmeran — v.46) is what generates the relational texture in which the allēlōn functions become possible. A weekly hour of parallel presence cannot produce the knowledge of one another that most allēlōn functions require. The Acts pattern is diagnostic: what kind of sustained contact produces the knowledge that makes these functions possible?

Knowing one another in the sense required for allēlōn functioning requires three things that a weekly service structure rarely produces:

Time — accumulated across sustained relationship, enough to have seen a person across varied circumstances, enough to know their characteristic patterns.

Contact — actual relational engagement, not parallel attendance in the same room. Members can sit in the same gathering for years and know almost nothing about each other if the structure keeps everyone oriented toward the platform rather than toward one another.

Disclosure — someone must name their actual state. The functions that involve carrying, bearing, confessing, and praying for require that a need be named. Disclosure requires the safety to be seen as one actually is. A member who cannot disclose is functionally unavailable to most allēlōn functioning on the receiving side — regardless of how present or willing other members are.

4Three Structural Failure Modes

Beyond individual formation, three structural patterns in contemporary Christian gatherings suppress allēlōn functioning regardless of the members' intentions.

The consumption architecture

When a gathering is structured primarily as an experience to be received — worship to be felt, a message to be absorbed, a production to be evaluated — it forms members in a consumer orientation toward assembly. They arrive asking "what will I receive?" rather than "what have I been entrusted to give?" This orientation is not primarily a character failure; it is the predictable output of an architecture that has consistently signaled that the members' role is receipt, not contribution. Members formed by that signal over years of attendance arrive in a posture that makes condition 3 (action toward another) improbable without prior reorientation.

Parallel presence

Members are in physical proximity without relational contact. Each person has a private, individual experience in the presence of others. They may be moved, challenged, or encountered — but the movement is between them and God (or the content), not between them and the other members in the room. The sympatheia mechanism cannot fire when members are not in contact with one another's states. Registration (condition 1) fails structurally.

Parallel presence is self-perpetuating: the more a gathering is structured for individual private encounter, the less occasion for member-to-member contact, and the less practiced members become at that contact, and the more foreign it feels when someone attempts it.

The platform delivery structure

When the primary mode of functioning in a gathering is one-directional — from a platform to a congregation in receipt mode — the allēlōn structure is blocked by the architecture itself. Allēlōn is a reciprocal pronoun. Reciprocal functioning requires every member to be a potential agent and a potential recipient simultaneously. A room full of recipients cannot execute reciprocal commands during the time they are in receipt mode.

This does not require that preaching or teaching be eliminated — 1 Cor 14:26 includes "a lesson" (didachēn) as one of the things members bring. But one-directional delivery of a lesson is one instance of one function among many. Paul's description of functioning assembly is not "one person teaches, everyone receives." It is "each one has — let all things be done for building up."

Language Note — panta pros oikodomēn ginesthō (1 Cor 14:26)

"Let all things be done for building up." Oikodomē — from oikos (house) + domos (structure) — is Paul's governing criterion for everything that happens in assembly. The construction metaphor is operative: building is active, cumulative, purposive. The test Paul applies (panta — all things) implies that every element of assembly is accountable to it. What is given must actually build up the recipients. A gathering where members leave no more built up than they arrived has, by Paul's own criterion, failed its purpose — regardless of the quality of the content delivered.

5Walking Through the Key Function Clusters

The allēlōn corpus covers more than forty commands. Six function clusters are foregrounded in the New Testament as most constitutive of genuine assembly. What follows identifies what each specifically requires to operate.

Encouragement — parakaleō (Heb 10:25; Heb 3:13; 1 Thess 5:11)

What it requires: A member who knows another member is discouraged or fainthearted — whose sympatheia has registered the other's state — who has interior resource available — who acts toward that specific person with words or presence that call them alongside into something larger than their contracted state. The recipient's interior must be open enough to receive rather than deflect.

The Heb 3:13 calibration: "Exhort one another every day (kath' hēmeran), as long as it is called 'today,' that none of you may be hardened by the deceitfulness of sin." The frequency — every day — establishes that the function Paul envisions is not a moment in a weekly service. It is a mode of sustained common life in which member-to-member encouragement is normal relational texture. What happens in the gathering is the concentrated expression of what must be the pattern of the whole common life.

Differentiated response — 1 Thessalonians 5:14

"And we urge you, brothers, admonish the idle, encourage the fainthearted, help the weak, be patient with all."1 Thessalonians 5:14, ESV

This is the most demanding allēlōn passage for the practitioner, because it requires not simply that a response be given — but that the right response be given to the right presentation.

Language Note — four verbs for four presentations

Noutheteite tous ataktous — admonish (noutheteō: corrective verbal engagement; from nous [mind] + tithenai [to place] — "to set something before the mind") the disorderly (ataktos: a military term for a soldier out of formation, not fulfilling assigned responsibility).

Paramytheisthe tous oligopsychous — console (paramytheomai: speak alongside with words that alleviate grief or anxiety; gentler register than parakaleō) the fainthearted (oligopsychos: literally "small-souled" — the interior is contracted, depleted, without resource).

Antechesthe tōn asthenōn — hold onto/support (antechomai: to cling to, to steady; the physical image of grabbing someone to prevent their fall; used in Matt 6:24 for devotion to a master) the weak (asthenos: those without the strength to stand on their own).

Makrothymeite panta — be long-tempered (makrothymeō: from makros [long] + thymos [passion, temper] — to sustain composure under provocation over duration) with all. Undifferentiated: the governing disposition underlying all three targeted responses.

The four verbs are not synonyms and are not interchangeable. Applying noutheteō (corrective admonition) to an oligopsychos (small-souled, contracted) does not merely fail — it compresses what is already compressed. Applying paramytheomai (consolation) to an ataktos (disorderly) does not merely fail — it enables the disorder by treating a behavioral pattern as a wound requiring comfort. The wrong response to a real presentation is not neutral. It causes harm.

What active differentiated response requires: the responding member must be able to read which presentation is in front of them before acting. This is the single allēlōn function that most clearly demonstrates that body-functioning requires prior formation. Differentiated response cannot be improvised in the moment. The perceptive capacity must be trained.

Shared stake — koinōnia (Acts 2:42–45; Gal 6:2; Rom 12:15)

What it requires: Members who know one another's actual situations — financial, relational, health, vocational, spiritual. Members formed in the oikonomos posture: my resources belong to the household and are available for movement toward need. A need that has been named. And then: resource actually moves.

What prevents it: Members present a curated surface — fine, grateful, blessed — that never discloses actual need. Members experience their resources as privately owned, available for discretionary giving but not structurally belonging to the body's needs. The gathering never creates occasion for actual-condition disclosure.

Language Note — Rom 12:15 and affective attunement

"Rejoice with those who rejoice, weep with those who weep" (khairein meta khaironton, klaiein meta klaiontōn). Both member-states require body response. What Paul describes is affective attunement as a body function — the body modulates its collective register to meet the actual state of its members. A body weeping with a grieving member and rejoicing with an honored member is a body whose sympatheia is active and whose members are in genuine contact with one another's states. A body in which the grieving member sits quietly in a room of people who do not know they are grieving is a body whose sympatheia has been suppressed — not failed as an aspiration but suppressed as a mechanism.

Confession and intercession — James 5:16

"Therefore, confess your sins to one another and pray for one another, that you may be healed."James 5:16, ESV

What it requires: A level of relational trust and history that cannot be improvised in a casual gathering. The confessing member must risk being known in actual failure — which requires confidence that the recipient will not weaponize, broadcast, or condemn what is disclosed. The receiving member must hold what is disclosed with makrothymia (long-temperedness, patient steadiness rather than shock) and respond with intercession rather than advice or judgment.

Language Note — iathēte (Jas 5:16) and the conditional structure

Hina iathēte — "so that you may be healed." Iathēte is the aorist passive subjunctive of iaomai (to heal). James names healing as the stated outcome of the completed dual-transaction: confession and prayer, both. Neither half alone generates the outcome. The passive voice (iathēte — "you be healed," not "you heal yourselves") names God as the healing agent; the human transactions are the conditions that place the person to receive what God gives. This is the governing logic of formation applied at the interpersonal level: human action creates conditions; God acts within them. The allēlōn transaction is the condition-creating mechanism for a specific healing outcome. Which means: a congregation where confession and intercession do not occur is a congregation where this specific healing is structurally unavailable to its members.

Building up — oikodomē (1 Cor 14:26; Eph 4:12–16)

Paul's governing criterion (panta pros oikodomēn ginesthō — "let all things be done for building up") imposes a test on every element of assembly. The test is transactional, not evaluative: did specific members leave more built up than they arrived? Not "was it edifying content?" but "did something of constructive value transfer and land?"

Eph 4:16 — the body building itself up: "The whole body, joined and held together by every joint with which it is equipped, when each part is working properly, makes the body grow so that it builds itself up in love." Two requirements: every joint (all the connective relationships between members) and each part working properly (every member executing their function). The body's growth is not produced by a gifted few but by the distributed functioning of all members. A body where most members are in receipt mode is a body where most of the joints are inactive — regardless of how well the active few perform.

6The Diagnostic Test

The test for whether the allēlōn functions operated in a gathering is not structural ("did the gathering occur?"), ritual ("did we complete the liturgy?"), or evaluative ("was it a good service?"). The test is transactional and particular:

  • Did Person A actually encourage Person B? Can both of them name what happened?
  • Did Person C actually confess to Person D, and did Person D actually intercede for Person C?
  • Did Person E actually carry what Person F was carrying?
  • Did Person G's correction of Person H's disorder land and be received?
  • Did Person I's contribution actually build up Person J — is Person J more built up than before?

If you cannot name the persons and the completed transactions, the functions may not have operated. "We had a good service" and "the allēlōn functions operated" are not the same claim.

This is the diagnostic Paul applies in 1 Corinthians 11. The Corinthians are not failing because the service is poor or the teaching deficient. They are failing because the body-constituting transactions are absent: the wealthy eat without the poor, the social divisions of the ambient world are reproduced rather than inverted, and no one is "discerning the body" (v.29) — recognizing and honoring the other members as the body. The gathering is happening. The function is not.

Paul's own precedent is the strongest possible statement that what is being described here is not an idealized standard layered onto existing practice. It is the New Testament's own categorical distinction between physical gathering and genuine assembly — a distinction Paul is prepared to name even when the community in question is calling what they do the Lord's Supper.

7Why This Requires Prior Formation

The allēlōn functions are not primarily difficult as commands. Most members assent to them and want them. They are difficult as states and as perceptual acts — as the interior conditions required for execution and as the trained capacity to read and respond appropriately.

Registration requires a non-defended interior — stable enough to receive the signal another member is "off" without screening it out or being destabilized by it. This is not a naturally occurring condition under pressure. It is formed through sustained discipline that has reshaped the implicit level of response.

Differentiated response (1 Thess 5:14) requires trained perceptive faculty — the capacity to distinguish oligopsychos from ataktos, to deploy the right intervention accordingly. This is what Hebrews 5:14 describes as hexis: "those who have their powers of discernment trained by constant practice to distinguish good from evil." The same faculty that distinguishes good from evil in the abstract also distinguishes the fainthearted from the disorderly in the person in front of you. It cannot be generated in the moment; it must be trained into place.

Carrying something to give requires the oikonomos posture to be formed — an orientation at the implicit level that you arrive for others, that your gifts belong to the household. This disposition must be trained through repeated practice of other-oriented action until other-orientation becomes the default mode.

Sustained functioning over time requires makrothymia — long-temperedness with the member who repeatedly needs what you repeatedly give without apparent change. Makrothymia is the fruit of discipline (paideia) — formed endurance rather than the natural tendency to withdraw when giving is not reciprocated or when the need is prolonged.

None of these capacities can be improvised in the gathering. They must be formed in the between-times — through the sustained disciplines, small acts of other-orientation practiced until they reshape the implicit level, and the kind of communal life in which members are gradually known and know one another across time and circumstance.

This is the direct implication for any congregational transition effort: you cannot simply create the structures for allēlōn functioning and expect formation to follow. The sequence runs the other direction. Formation produces the people capable of using the structures. Without formed people, smaller groups reproduce the parallel-presence and consumption patterns of larger gatherings in a different room. The mechanism is not structural; it is dispositional. And dispositions require formation.

Language Note — paideia (Heb 12:5–11; Tit 2:11–14) and the formation chain

Paideia — the Greek comprehensive program of character formation; in the NT, God's sustained formative discipline and instruction. Heb 12:11: "all paideia for the moment seems painful rather than pleasant, but later it yields the peaceful fruit of righteousness to those who have been trained (gegymnasmenos) by it." The chain: paideia (sustained formative discipline) → gymnazō (athletic training, repeated practice under load) → hexis (stable perceptive disposition, Heb 5:14) → the regulated interior that makes allēlōn functioning possible. Tit 2:12 adds the agent: "the grace of God... training (paideuousa) us" — present participle, ongoing — to renounce ungodliness and live self-controlled, upright, godly lives. Grace is not only the basis of forgiveness; grace is the formative agent actively shaping the person toward the telos. Formation is not self-improvement. It is cooperation with what grace is already doing.

8The Map, Not the Territory

This document cannot do what it describes. Reading about the allēlōn functions does not constitute them operating. Understanding the diagnostic test for genuine assembly does not make a congregation capable of passing it. The document is the map.

The territory is a gathering — not necessarily large, not necessarily liturgically sophisticated — where Person A actually encouraged Person B, where Person C held up Person D through a period of weakness, where Person E disclosed something real to Person F and Person F prayed specifically for it, where Person G's correction of Person H was received rather than defended, and where Person I's contribution built Person J up in a way still operating three days later.

That gathering is the product of formation. The formation precedes and produces the functioning. And the formation requires time, sustained contact, disclosure, the disciplines that reshape the interior at the level below deliberation, and leadership whose own formation runs deep enough to model and call out the allēlōn functioning in others.

The transition from gathering to assembly is not a program change. It is a people change — specifically, the slow formation of people capable of the bilateral, other-oriented, differentiated, sustained transactions the New Testament calls "the body functioning."

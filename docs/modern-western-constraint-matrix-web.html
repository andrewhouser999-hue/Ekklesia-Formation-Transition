Formation & Transition — Core Track

Modern Western Constraint Matrix

Purpose

This is a one-page design reference for the Formation & Transition track. It names the specific structural conditions any modern Western congregational formation architecture must work within or against — not as generic cultural observations but as design constraints with direct implications for what a formation architecture can and cannot do.

Used during Phase 2 design to evaluate whether a proposed structure works with or against each constraint, and whether the constraint is immovable (must design around it) or partially addressable (counter-design available).

Companion to: Formation & Transition — Scaling Mechanism Research (historical models) and Formation & Transition — The Telos of a Formed Congregation (telos the design serves).

The Seven Constraints

ConstraintWhat it does to a formation architectureClassificationCounter-design available?
Residential mobility
avg. 11+ relocations per lifetime; median congregational tenure under 5 years in most denominations		 Makes long-arc formation sequences that require 2–3 years to complete inaccessible to a substantial portion of participants before completion. Undermines the stability that mutual knowledge requires. Immovable Short-arc, high-density cohort structures that front-load depth. Cohort formation that reaches the depth of genuine koinōnia within 12–18 months rather than assuming it will develop gradually.
Exit-option culture
no social cost to leaving a congregation; no authority structure that can enforce prerequisites or maintain formation discipline		 Makes mandatory participation and prerequisite enforcement impossible. Every formation structure competes with the option of leaving. Immovable Intrinsic motivation generated by genuine formation experience — community quality, visible transformation, relational investment — rather than external constraint. Covenant entry (commitment made at the outset rather than defaulted to) approximates the entry-cost function without coercive enforcement.
Digital formation competition
avg. 7+ hours/day on screens; social media algorithms optimized for outrage and tribalism; formation of desire and attention operating continuously		 Competes with formation structures for the affective and attentional conditions formation requires. Participants arriving at gatherings are pre-formed by digital counter-liturgies operating since the last meeting. Partially addressable Counter-practices built into the cohort covenant: digital sabbath, attentional disciplines, silence. Between-session formation accountability that includes attention practices, not only Scripture disciplines. Cannot be eliminated; can be partially countered by intentional structure.
Post-Christendom
Christianity no longer carries cultural authority; ecclesial leadership cannot assume deference; participants often arrive with deeply formed non-Christian identity frameworks		 Eliminates assumed shared vocabulary, cultural deference, and the social weight that made baptism a meaningful community gate in the catechumenate. Requires formation to address deeply formed competing identities that are not merely background noise but organized commitments. Immovable Catechumenate entry logic: the formation architecture must address the de-formation task (identifying and displacing competing formation) as a first-order concern, not a late-stage refinement. The track's existing documents (Assembled, Not Attending; false-identity diagnostic layer) already name this. No assumed shared starting point.
Fragmented and commodified time
dual-income households, children's activity schedules, always-on work culture; formation cannot count on sustained uninterrupted participant availability beyond 60–90 min/week outside Sunday		 Makes formation architectures requiring substantial weekly time investment structurally unsustainable for most participants. Competes with every legitimate demand on participant time. Partially addressable High-density, short-duration formation practices integrated into existing life rhythms rather than extracted from them. The Wesleyan class meeting's 60–90 minute weekly model is the historical reference. Between-session formation built into existing daily rhythms (meals, commute, morning rhythms) rather than requiring additional time blocks.
Therapeutic displacement
self-actualization and emotional wellbeing as the dominant ambient formation telos; faith and spiritual practice frequently instrumentalized toward personal flourishing		 Produces participants whose formation goal is wellbeing rather than Christlikeness — who will unconsciously resist formation that does not serve their therapeutic telos, particularly the cruciform dimensions (suffering as formation mechanism, dying to self, the cross as the path). Partially addressable Entry via therapeutic vocabulary (interior life, emotional health, identity formation) creates access. Telos must be clearly and repeatedly distinguished from self-actualization — but entry vocabulary need not start there. The therapeutic culture has produced emotional literacy and interest in interior life that are real formation capacities waiting for reorientation.
Economic consumption as primary cultural liturgy
the consumer economy as a formation system enacted through repeated embodied practice; forming desire toward acquisition, status, and transaction at a frequency and intensity that vastly exceeds congregational contact		 Forms desire toward a telos (I am what I consume; more is better; all relationships are transactional) that is directly opposed to the ekklēsia's image-bearing telos. Operates below the level of conscious belief — people holding orthodox theology are simultaneously being formed by consumer liturgies they do not recognize as formation. Partially addressable Counter-practices at the embodied level: shared meals, economic mutual aid, gift-giving without transaction, Sabbath from consumption. These counter-form at the level where the consumer liturgy operates — the body and the repeated practice — rather than addressing it only cognitively. Communities that implement these consistently report the counter-formation is real.

Early Church Analog — Why This Matters for Model Selection

Five of the seven constraints have analogs in the pre-Constantinian context in which the early church catechumenate was developed.

Design judgment, not proven equivalence

The specific mechanisms differ; the structural conditions are analogous.

Modern constraintEarly church analogDisanalogy to note
Exit-option cultureVoluntary association; Christianity required active choice to enterDifference: Early church entry carried social cost and physical risk; modern exit carries no cost. The catechumenate leveraged existential stakes unavailable today.
Post-ChristendomPre-Constantinian minority status; no cultural authorityDifference: Early church competed with religious pluralism (gods, temples, mystery cults) inside a framework that assumed gods exist. Modern West includes significant secular atheism — a different kind of competition.
Digital formation competitionCompeting imperial cults, civic religion, patronage economy forming participants continuouslyDifference: Scale and ubiquity of digital formation is qualitatively different from civic religious participation.
Therapeutic displacementHellenistic self-cultivation culture; philosophies offering flourishing as the telosClose analog — early church navigated this directly.
Economic consumption liturgyRoman patronage economy and civic benefaction as formation systemsClose analog — early church's economic sharing was a direct counter-formation practice.

Design implication: Phase 2 cohort design should draw primarily from pre-Constantinian models (catechumenate entry logic, early community economic practices) and the Wesleyan class meeting (the post-Christendom Protestant precedent) rather than from Christendom-era models (Benedictine as residential monastery, liturgical year as state-church infrastructure). The Benedictine Rule contributes principles (informed consent, conversatio morum, between-session rule of life) without requiring its institutional form.
